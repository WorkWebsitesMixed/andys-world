Material selection is not guessing — it is a structured argument. Today you apply ACCESS FM to narrow your choices to the right materials for the right components, then write a specification tight enough for the examiner to test your product against.

<Callout type="exam" title="Paper 3 Section A — material selection" id="why">
  Material selection questions on Paper 3 always ask for a justification. A material name alone scores nothing. The pattern: **material → required property → functional reason → linked to the design context**. Today you build that pattern into your project folder so you can replicate it under exam conditions.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "ACCESS FM", definition: "A specification scaffold: Aesthetics, Cost, Customer, Environment, Size, Safety, Function, Materials. Each heading prompts a different type of requirement. Using all eight headings ensures a specification that is balanced — not just about function, but also about sustainability, cost, and user needs." },
  { term: "6Rs of sustainability", definition: "Rethink (question whether the product is needed at all), Refuse (decline harmful or unnecessary materials), Reduce (use less material), Reuse (design for repeated use), Repair (design for disassembly and repair), Recycle (use recyclable materials and allow recycling at end of life)." },
  { term: "FSC certification", definition: "Forest Stewardship Council certification. FSC-certified timber comes from responsibly managed forests — replanted after harvest, no habitat destruction. Using FSC pine meets a sustainability specification point." },
  { term: "Measurable specification point", definition: "A specification point that can be objectively tested. 'The organizer must be strong' is not measurable. 'The base must support a load of at least 2 kg without deflecting more than 2 mm' is measurable. Measurable points have a number, a unit, and a test method implied." },
]} />

<Section title="Material selection process" icon="🔧" id="selection">

### Step-by-step selection for the desk organizer

1. **Identify the functional requirement** for the component (what must it do?)
2. **Translate to required properties** (what material properties are needed to do that?)
3. **Shortlist candidate materials** that have those properties
4. **Apply secondary criteria** — cost, sustainability, machinability in the school workshop
5. **Justify the final choice** with a named property and a design reason

### Example: selecting material for the organizer base

| Step | Result |
|------|--------|
| Function | Support all compartments and contents; sit flat on a desk |
| Required property | Compressive strength, rigidity, workability |
| Candidates | Pine, MDF, acrylic, mild steel |
| Secondary criteria | Must be cut with tenon saw or pillar drill (school workshop) |
| **Chosen: FSC pine 18 mm** | High compressive strength, workable, screws hold well, FSC-certified meets sustainability point |

### Common choices for a desk organizer

| Component | Common choice | Why |
|-----------|--------------|-----|
| Main structure | Pine 18 mm | Workable, holds screws, FSC available |
| Panels | Acrylic 3 mm | Translucent so contents visible; clean aesthetic finish |
| Fasteners | Mild steel screws | Tensile strength in joint under load |
| Dividers | MDF 12 mm | Flat, stable, takes paint finish easily |

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Applying the 6Rs to material choices">

**Reduce:** Use 3 mm acrylic rather than 6 mm for the side panels — reduces material mass by 50% with no structural loss, since 3 mm at 200 mm height is rigid enough for this application.

**Recycle:** Acrylic off-cuts go to the recycle bin (PMMA, recycling number 7). Pine off-cuts go to the biomass collection.

**Repair:** Join the acrylic panels with countersunk screws rather than permanent adhesive — the organizer can be disassembled for repair or panel replacement.

**Rethink:** Do I need all six compartments, or will three cover 90% of my use cases? Fewer compartments = less material = lower environmental cost.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Selection table + specification" time="90 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["A4 folder pages", "Material selection table template", "Specification template", "ACCESS FM prompt card", "6Rs prompt card"]}>

**Selection table (0–40 min):**
1. Complete a material selection table with two components: (a) main structure, (b) panel.
   - Columns: required property | candidate materials | chosen material | justification (named property + design reason).
2. Note the FSC status of your chosen timber.

**Specification (40–75 min):**
3. Draft the project specification — minimum 6 points. Each point must be:
   - Measurable: has a number or observable criterion
   - Justified: a "because" clause linking it to the user's need
   - Include at least one safety point and one sustainability point.
4. Peer check (75–80 min): swap with a partner. Mark every point as M (measurable) or X (needs a number). Return with suggestions.

**6Rs application (80–90 min):**
5. Apply four of the 6Rs to your material choices. Write one sentence per R applied.
6. Write one distinction: biodegradable vs recyclable for your chosen materials.
</Activity>

<Support title="Specification sentence frame">

"The [component] must [measurable criterion] because [user need or safety requirement]."

Example: "The base must support a load of at least 2 kg without visible deflection because the user will stack books and equipment on it."
</Support>
