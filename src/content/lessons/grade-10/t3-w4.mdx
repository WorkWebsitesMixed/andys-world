Reverse engineering is how designers learn from what already exists. Before you can redesign the water bottle, you must understand how it was made, why each material was chosen, and where its weaknesses are. Today you take it apart.

<Section title="Parts of a typical water bottle" icon="🔧" id="parts">

| Component | Material (typical) | Identification clue | Manufacturing process |
|-----------|------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Bottle body (PET) | PET (polyethylene terephthalate) | Recycling number 1, clear, thin wall | Injection stretch blow moulding |
| Bottle body (PE) | HDPE | Recycling number 2, slightly opaque | Extrusion blow moulding |
| Cap | PP | Recycling number 5, white, slightly flexible | Injection moulding |
| Gasket / seal | LDPE or silicone | White, very flexible, flat ring shape | Compression moulding or extrusion |
| Label | Paper or BOPP film | Paper tears; BOPP is a thin polymer film | Printed flat; adhesive or sleeve applied |
| Base insert | PE or PP | Separate disc at base of some PET bottles | Injection moulding |

### How to identify a manufacturing process from the component

- **Parting line:** a thin line of flash where two mould halves met → injection moulded or blow moulded
- **Gate mark:** a small nub or scar where polymer was injected → injection moulded
- **Seam line (bottom):** a horizontal pinch mark at the base → extrusion blow moulded (where parison was pinched)
- **Uniform wall thickness across the body:** injection stretch blow moulded (biaxial stretching makes thickness even)

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Teacher disassembly notes — what to look for">

**Step 1 — cap:** Unscrew and examine. Look for: thread moulded in (injection moulding), parting line running across the top face, recycling symbol 5 (PP), white colour, slight flexibility.

**Step 2 — gasket under cap:** Lift out the ring. Note: it is white, very flexible, flat — likely LDPE or silicone. Function: seals the thread to prevent leaking under hydrostatic pressure when the bottle is inverted.

**Step 3 — label:** Peel or slide off. Is it paper (tears when wet) or BOPP film (does not tear)? Note the adhesive strip or lack of it (shrink sleeve has no adhesive).

**Step 4 — body:** Examine the base — recycling number? Is there a seam mark at the base (blow moulded) or is the base uniformly formed (injection stretch blow)?

**Step 5 — base insert (if present):** A separate disc inside the concave base of some PET bottles. Injection moulded PP. Function: stabilises a rounded base.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Disassembly + ACCESS FM + annotation drawing" time="90 min" grouping="Groups of 3 for disassembly, individual for drawings" materials={["One PET bottle + one aluminium bottle per group", "Dismantling tray", "Callipers", "Ruler", "Pencil", "Pre-printed parts list template"]}>

**Observation (0–10 min):**
1. Do not write anything for the first 5 minutes. Handle the bottle. Turn it. Note the seams, the marks, the material feel.
2. Write one sentence: "The most surprising thing I noticed before dismantling was ___."

**Disassembly + parts list (10–60 min):**
3. Disassemble the bottle to all separable components.
4. Complete the parts list: component name | material identified | evidence for identification | preliminary process guess | function.
5. Apply ACCESS FM: at least two observations under each of the eight headings.

**Annotation drawings (60–90 min):**
6. Draw two components from the disassembly (freehand, on A4). Proportion matters — hold the real component next to your drawing and compare widths at three points.
7. Add annotation leaders to each drawing: at minimum, material + identification evidence; manufacturing process + visible clue; function of this component in the product system.
8. Drawing colleague circulates: accepts or challenges annotation quality before you file the page.
</Activity>

<Support title="Proportion check for drawings">

Before drawing: measure the real component's height and width with a calliper. Set your drawing dimensions to approximately the same ratio (you may scale up or down, but the ratio must be correct). A cap that is 28 mm wide and 20 mm tall should appear wider than it is tall — not square or taller than wide.
</Support>
