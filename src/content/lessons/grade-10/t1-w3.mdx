Isometric drawing lets you show a 3D object on a 2D sheet without the distortion of perspective. This week you practise it on mechanism components, then apply the same precision to writing a specification — where every point must be measurable and justified, just as every isometric line is drawn at a precise angle.

<Callout type="exam" title="Specification vs wish list" id="why">
  A specification is not a wish list. Every point must pass two tests: **measurable** (can I test whether the design meets it?) and **justified** (can I trace it back to the brief, the user, or a safety rule?). "The door must look good" fails both. "The door must open at least 60° so the façade opening is wide enough for a person to pass through" passes both.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[

<Section title="Isometric drawing: technique" icon="✏️" id="isometric">

### The three axes

Isometric drawing uses three axes:
- **Vertical axis** — goes straight up and down on the page.
- **Left receding axis** — goes up-left at 30° from horizontal.
- **Right receding axis** — goes up-right at 30° from horizontal.

All edges parallel to an axis are drawn at that angle. All edges are drawn at true length (no foreshortening).

### Steps: crating an isometric box

1. Mark your starting corner at the bottom-front.
2. Draw the vertical edge upward.
3. Draw the left and right receding edges at 30° from horizontal.
4. Complete the remaining edges parallel to these three directions.
5. Now sketch the actual form inside the crate: slice away the corners, inscribe curves, add internal features.

### Drawing curved shapes isometrically

Circles become **ellipses** on an isometric face. Method:
1. Draw the isometric square face first.
2. Mark the midpoint of each side.
3. Connect opposite midpoints with light arcs — you will see an ellipse forming inside the square. Refine the curve.

For a disc (cam) or cylinder (roller), draw the front ellipse, then draw the back ellipse offset by the depth, and connect the two with tangent lines at top and bottom.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Isometric crate of a pear cam disc">
**Object:** A circular disc (cam) 60 mm diameter, 15 mm thick.

**Step 1:** Draw a 60 × 60 isometric square face (left and right sides at 30°, top horizontal).

**Step 2:** Inscribe the front ellipse by marking midpoints of all four sides and drawing smooth arcs through them.

**Step 3:** Offset the four corners by 15 mm in the depth direction. Draw the back ellipse (only the visible portion — top and right arcs).

**Step 4:** Add tangent lines connecting the front and back ellipses at the top and bottom of the visible edge.

**Step 5:** Thicken all visible outlines. Add construction lines lightly — they show your method.
</WorkedExample>

<Section title="Specification writing with ACCESS FM" icon="📝" id="specification">

### The ACCESS FM framework

Use each letter to prompt a different kind of specification point:

| Letter | Dimension | Prompt for the door system |
|---|---|---|
| A | Aesthetics | Finish, colour, surface quality of the door panel |
| C | Cost | Affordable within the workshop material budget |
| C | Customer | Firefighter's operational requirements; building occupant safety |
| E | Environment | Sustainable material choices; no toxic finishes |
| S | Size | Must fit within the pre-made façade opening |
| S | Safety | No sharp edges; circuit meets low-voltage safety rules |
| F | Function | Door opens automatically; thermistor trigger; motor-driven |
| M | Materials | Workshop palette materials only |

### The measurable + justified formula

**Formula:** "The [feature] must [measurable criterion] because [reason linked to brief, user, or safety]."

**Example:** "The door must open within 5 seconds of the thermistor reaching 40°C because fire can spread to an adjacent room within 30 seconds."

This point is measurable (5 seconds, 40°C) and justified (fire spread time — a real constraint from the brief).

### Exam practice — sketching mechanism parts

In the bonus session, you draw two mechanism components — a disc and a straight arm — in crated isometric. Both will appear in your finished mechanism design, so this is project work and exam practice simultaneously.

</Section>

<Activity title="Isometric drawing and specification writing" time="90 min (W session) + 45 min (B session)" grouping="Individual" materials={["30-degree isometric guideline paper", "A3 paper", "Pencil", "ACCESS FM prompt card", "Project brief sheet"]}>
**W session:**
1. Draw a crated isometric view of the given component (a box with a hole) using the three-axis method. Add at least one ellipse.
2. Draft your Firefighter Barbie specification: at least five points, each measurable and justified. Use ACCESS FM as a scaffold. Include at least one safety point and one sustainability point.

**B session:**
3. Draw two mechanism components in crated isometric: a disc (cam) and a straight arm (crank link). Aim for a clean outline and at least one ellipse on the disc face.
4. Add light shading to one face if time allows — hatching for a metal surface.
</Activity>

<Support title="Scaffolds for isometric and specification">
**Isometric paper:** Use pre-printed 30° guideline paper. The guidelines are your scaffolding — draw directly over them until you internalise the angles, then try without.

**Specification sentence frame:** "The [component/feature] must [measurable criterion] because [reason from brief, user, or safety rule]."

**ACCESS FM prompt card:** Keep it beside your folder. For each letter, ask: "Do I have a specification point that covers this dimension?" If not, write one.

**Crating sequence card:** (1) bottom-front corner, (2) three edges of the box, (3) complete the box, (4) sketch the form inside.
</Support>

<Section title="Homework & extension" icon="🏠" id="homework">

**Home task:** Finalise your specification to at least six measurable, justified points. Include one safety point (e.g., no exposed sharp edges, low-voltage circuit) and one sustainability point (e.g., FSC-certified wood, recyclable material). Bring the finalised specification to Week 6 for peer review.

<Extension title="Rendered isometric: material texture">
Add material texture to your disc and arm sketches: hatching (parallel diagonal lines) for a metal surface; cross-hatching for cut sections. Research the standard conventions for wood and metal rendering in engineering drawing and apply them to one face of each component.
</Extension>
