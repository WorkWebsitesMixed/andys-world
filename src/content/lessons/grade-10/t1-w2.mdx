Orthographic drawing is the language of the workshop — it communicates exactly how an object is shaped and how it should be built. This week you learn the conventions, practise them on a given object, and then sharpen your Paper 1 function-point writing: two skills that examiners reward in different parts of the same paper.

<Callout type="exam" title="Annotation earns marks; vague words don't" id="why">
  Every annotation on Paper 1 must do one of four things: name a **material** with a reason, describe a **join** with a reason, state a **function** with a reason, or give a **dimension** with a reason. Writing "strong" or "good material" earns zero — the mark scheme needs specifics.
</Callout>

<Section title="Orthographic drawing: the rules" icon="✏️" id="orthographic">

### Two conventions in use worldwide

- **First-angle projection** (common in Europe): the front view is in the top-left, the side view is to its right showing the side you see from the left.
- **Third-angle projection** (common in North America): views are placed on the same side as the direction you look from.

Both are acceptable on IGCSE drawings; always include the projection symbol so the reader knows which you used.

### Line types — why each exists

| Line | Appearance | What it shows |
|---|---|---|
| Outline | Thick continuous | The visible edges of the object |
| Hidden detail | Thin dashed | Edges that exist but are hidden by a nearer face |
| Centre line | Thin chain (dash-dot) | Axes of symmetry, centre of circles |
| Dimension line | Thin continuous + arrowheads | Measurements; never crosses an outline |

### Mark-worthy annotation — the four categories

A single annotation can earn a mark if it states: **material** (what it is made of), **join** (how two parts connect), **function** (what a feature does), or **dimension** (a size with a reason). Each must include a reason to earn the mark.

Examples on a shelf bracket:
- "3 mm mild steel — resists bending under shelf load" (material + reason)
- "Riveted butt joint — permanent, no welding equipment needed" (join + reason)
- "Pivot pin — allows 90° fold for flat-pack transport" (function + reason)

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Orthographic views of a shelf bracket">
**Object:** A simple L-shaped wall bracket with two mounting holes.

**Front view:** Shows the L-shape outline in thick lines. The mounting holes are shown as circles (outlines). Hidden edges behind the bracket are shown as dashed lines.

**Side view:** Placed directly to the right of the front view (or to the left in first-angle). Shows the depth of the bracket as a rectangle.

**Four annotations:**
1. "18 mm mild steel plate — high tensile strength for heavy loads" (material)
2. "Countersunk M5 screws — flush surface, no snagging hazard" (join)
3. "Angled rib — prevents the bracket twisting under a lateral load" (function)
4. "50 mm arm length — matches standard shelf depth" (dimension with reason)
</WorkedExample>

<Section title="Exam practice — Paper 1 function points" icon="📝" id="function-points">

### What Paper 1 part (a) asks

Part (a) gives you an everyday product and asks for four function points — specific statements of what the product must do. The mark scheme rejects:
- Generic words: "strong", "comfortable", "easy to use"
- Aesthetic descriptions: "looks nice", "modern design"
- Material names without a function: "made of plastic"

### The function-point formula

"The [product] must [specific action] so that [user-centred reason]."

Example for a phone stand:
- **Weak:** "The stand must hold the phone."
- **Strong:** "The stand must support a 200 g phone at 70° from horizontal without tipping, so the user can view the screen hands-free while cooking."

### Section A quick-fire

Five short Section A items test your recall of the design process (Week 1 content). Answer in one or two sentences — no wasted words. Rewrite any answer that contains a generic word.

</Section>

<Activity title="Drawing and function-point exercises" time="90 min (W session) + 45 min (B session)" grouping="Individual" materials={["A3 drawing paper", "Pencil, ruler, set square", "Given product for function points"]}>
**W session:**
1. Draw front and side views of the given simple object using correct line types. Check: are outlines thick? Are hidden edges dashed? Are centre lines chain?
2. Annotate with at least three mark-worthy notes — one for material, one for join or construction, one for function.

**B session:**
3. Write four function points for the given product using the formula. No generic words in any answer.
4. Answer five Section A items on the design process. Mark your own answers, then rewrite the two weakest ones to remove any generic language.
</Activity>

<Support title="Scaffolds for drawing and writing">
**Line types reminder card:** Keep it on your desk. Before handing in, check each line in your drawing against the card.

**Annotation checklist:** For each annotation — does it name a material, join, or function? Does it give a reason? If not, rewrite it.

**Function-point frame:** "The [product] must [specific action] so that [reason linked to user or use context]."

**For orthographic:** Use a pre-printed light-grey orthographic grid if you struggle to align the two views. The front and side views must be at exactly the same height.
</Support>
