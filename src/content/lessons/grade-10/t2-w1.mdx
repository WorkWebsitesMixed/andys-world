The Firefighter Barbie system is done. Now you design something for yourself — a desk organizer that must use at least two different material families, fit within a 300 mm × 200 mm × 200 mm envelope, and be made entirely by you in the makerspace. The design decisions are yours: compartment sizes, materials, form, finish.

<Callout type="exam" title="Paper 1 part (c) connection" id="why">
  Part (c) asks for three conceptually different annotated ideas. Today you practise exactly that — three rough organizer sketches, each a genuinely different form. The habit of generating divergent ideas before committing to one is the core skill that part (c) tests.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Material family", definition: "A group of materials sharing common origins and broad properties. The five families are: timbers (natural and manufactured boards), metals (ferrous and non-ferrous), polymers (thermoplastics and thermosets), composites (matrix + reinforcement), and smart/modern materials (react to environmental stimuli)." },
  { term: "Thermoplastic", definition: "A polymer that softens when heated and can be reshaped — the process is reversible. Examples: acrylic (PMMA), ABS, polypropylene. Recycling symbol 1–7 identifies the type. Contrast with thermoset: once cured, it cannot be remelted." },
  { term: "Composite", definition: "A material made from a matrix (binder) and a reinforcement (fibres or particles). GRP (glass reinforced plastic) = glass fibres in polyester resin. CFRP = carbon fibres in epoxy. Composites are engineered to combine properties that neither constituent has alone." },
  { term: "Smart material", definition: "A material that changes a property in response to an environmental stimulus. Examples: shape memory alloy (returns to a memorised shape when heated), thermochromic ink (changes colour with temperature), self-healing polymer (repairs micro-cracks). Different from conventional materials because the response is programmed into the material itself." },
]} />

<Section title="The five material families" icon="🔧" id="families">

| Family | Sub-groups | Desk organizer application examples |
|--------|-----------|-------------------------------------|
| **Timbers** | Hardwoods (oak, mahogany), softwoods (pine, balsa), manufactured boards (MDF, plywood) | Pine structural frame, MDF base |
| **Metals** | Ferrous (iron-based, e.g. mild steel), non-ferrous (e.g. aluminium, copper) | Mild steel fixings, aluminium brackets |
| **Polymers** | Thermoplastics (re-melt: acrylic, ABS, PP), thermosets (permanent: polyester resin) | 3 mm acrylic side panels, PP dividers |
| **Composites** | GRP, CFRP, carbon fibre | Reinforced handle or base (uncommon in school projects) |
| **Smart/Modern** | Shape memory alloy, thermochromic, self-healing | Colour-change panel, spring return hinge |

### What is constrained by the brief

| Fixed — not your choice | Open — your decisions |
|------------------------|----------------------|
| Must use ≥ 2 material families | Number and size of compartments |
| Max envelope 300 × 200 × 200 mm | Exact materials chosen |
| All making uses makerspace tools only | Form, aesthetics, and finish |
| At least one material justified by properties | Colour, texture, personal details |

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Writing a user/context statement for this project">

**Context:** You are both the designer and the user.

**Weak version:**
"My desk is messy and I need somewhere to put things."

**Strong version:**
"I use my desk for homework, drawing, and charging devices. I have rulers, markers, scissors, and cables that currently fall off the surface or end up in a pile. A modular organizer with three or more compartments would let me separate drawing tools from cables, reducing the time I spend searching for things before starting work."

Notice: the strong version names the user, the current problem with evidence, and a functional requirement (three compartments, separation of types of items).
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Project launch exercises" time="90 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["Project brief sheet", "A4 folder pages", "Pencil and ruler", "Material sample set (wood off-cut, metal strip, acrylic sheet, GRP card, smart material card)"]}>

**Warm-up — sort the samples (0–10 min):**
1. Sort the material samples on your desk into the five families.
2. Write one example of each family and a possible role in a desk organizer.

**User/context + brief (10–30 min):**
3. Write a user/context statement: describe yourself, your desk, and at least three organisational frustrations.
4. Draft a one-sentence design brief: "I am designing a ___ for a ___ that ___ so that ___."

**Idea generation (30–55 min):**
5. Sketch three rough organizer concepts in boxes — each must be a genuinely different form, not the same box restyled. No annotation required yet; focus on generating different shapes.

**Plenary (55–60 min):**
6. Write on the material-family wall: one material you think you will use, and why.
</Activity>

<Support title="Idea generation — what counts as different?">

Three different forms might be: (1) a horizontal tray with compartments, (2) a vertical tower with stacked drawers, (3) a wall-mounted panel with hooks and slots. The same idea with slight dimension changes is not different — the overall concept must change.
</Support>
