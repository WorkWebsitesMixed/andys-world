Aluminium water bottles, aerosol cans, beverage tins — these products look simple but each involves a precise commercial manufacturing process that happens in milliseconds under extreme pressure. Today you learn the processes and the quality control that keeps them consistent.

<Callout type="exam" title="Paper 3 Section B — metal processes" id="why">
  Metal processes appear in Section B as separate questions from polymer processes. The format is the same: name the process, describe the sequence, give an advantage, state a QC check. Practise describing die casting and impact extrusion in numbered steps — one step per mark.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Die casting", definition: "Molten metal (aluminium, zinc alloy) is forced under high pressure into a hardened steel die. The die cools the metal rapidly. The die opens and the part is ejected. Products: aluminium handles, zinc alloy toy car bodies, engine housings. High precision and very smooth surface finish. Limitation: only aluminium and zinc alloys — not suitable for steel (melting point too high for a steel die)." },
  { term: "Impact extrusion", definition: "An aluminium slug (a disc of solid aluminium) is placed in a die. A punch strikes down at high speed; the aluminium is forced up around the punch, forming a thin-walled tube. Products: aluminium water bottles, toothpaste tubes, aerosol cans. Very fast process — one stroke per part. The slug becomes a tube in milliseconds." },
  { term: "Deep drawing", definition: "A flat metal sheet is clamped and a punch presses downward into a die, forcing the sheet to flow into a cup or container shape. Products: aluminium beverage cans, steel saucepans, kitchen sinks. The metal is drawn — stretched and shaped — not cut. The process starts with flat sheet and produces a hollow cup shape." },
  { term: "Quality control (QC)", definition: "Checking products during production to identify defects before they pass to the next stage. A QC checkpoint is placed at critical steps where catching a defect is cheaper than downstream correction. QC is reactive (finds defects); Quality Assurance (QA) is proactive (prevents defects from occurring)." },
  { term: "Tolerance", definition: "The permitted variation from a target dimension. Written as: target ± allowable deviation. Example: 28.0 ± 0.1 mm means the dimension can be anywhere between 27.9 mm and 28.1 mm and still pass QC. A go/no-go gauge checks a dimension against its tolerance limits without measuring the exact value." },
]} />

<Section title="Metal processes — summary" icon="🏭" id="metals">

| Process | Starting form | Product shape | Material | Products |
|---------|--------------|--------------|----------|---------|
| Die casting | Molten metal | Complex 3D solid | Al, Zn alloy | Handles, housings |
| Impact extrusion | Solid slug | Thin-walled tube/can | Aluminium | Water bottles, aerosols |
| Deep drawing | Flat sheet | Open cup/container | Al, steel | Beverage cans, pans |
| Rolling | Solid ingot | Sheet or section | Steel, Al, Cu | Structural steel, foil |
| Sand casting | Molten metal | Rough solid | Most metals | Engine blocks, sculptures |

### Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) — four stages

| Stage | Description | Example impact (PET water bottle) | Design intervention |
|-------|-------------|----------------------------------|-------------------|
| 1. Raw material extraction | Oil drilled and refined into PET granules | Fossil fuel consumption; oil spill risk | Switch to bio-based PET |
| 2. Manufacture | Blow moulding; water cooling | Energy use; water consumption | Use renewable energy at factory |
| 3. Use phase | Single-use — used once, discarded | Low recycling rates (≈30% globally) | Redesign for refill (50+ uses) |
| 4. End of life | Landfill or recycling | 70% goes to landfill globally | Deposit-return scheme |

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Impact extrusion of an aluminium water bottle">

**Sequence of operations:**

1. Aluminium slug (disc of solid Al, correct weight for the final wall thickness) placed in the die cavity.
2. Punch strikes downward at high speed — aluminium cannot compress, so it flows upward around the punch.
3. The tube-shaped bottle body forms in one stroke: walls ≈ 0.3 mm thick.
4. Punch retracts; tube is ejected.
5. Tube is trimmed to correct height.
6. Neck is formed by ironing or necking (reducing diameter at the top).
7. Surface anodised: aluminium oxide layer formed electrolytically — corrosion resistant, can be coloured.

**QC checkpoint:** wall thickness checked using an ultrasonic gauge every 500 units; tolerance ±0.05 mm. Units outside tolerance are rejected before the anodising stage.
</WorkedExample>

<Support title="Remembering the metal process types">

**Die casting:** liquid metal forced IN to a die (like injection moulding but for metal).

**Impact extrusion:** solid slug PUSHED DOWN; metal flows UP (upward extrusion). The force direction is down; the material moves up.

**Deep drawing:** flat sheet PULLED DOWN into a die to form a cup (the sheet is "drawn" into shape).

**Rolling:** metal SQUEEZED between two rollers to reduce thickness (like rolling out dough).
</Support>
