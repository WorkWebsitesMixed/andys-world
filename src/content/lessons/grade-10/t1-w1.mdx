The door is pre-made. The firefighter is waiting. Your job is to design the system that opens it — automatically, when the heat sensor fires. This week you meet the brief, understand the constraints, and write your first piece of project evidence while practising the sketching technique that underpins everything you will draw this term.

<Callout type="exam" title="Design brief vs specification" id="why">
  Examiners test whether you know the difference. A **brief** is one or two sentences: what is being designed, for whom, and why. A **specification** (coming in Week 3) is the numbered list of measurable requirements the design must meet. Get the brief right first.
</Callout>

<Section title="Project launch: Firefighter Barbie" icon="🔧" id="launch">

A building is on fire. Temperature at the door rises. A **thermistor** in a circuit detects the heat — its resistance drops, raising the voltage at the transistor base until the transistor switches on. The transistor drives a **DC motor**. The motor turns a **crank or cam mechanism** that pushes the door open. A firefighter enters without touching anything.

You are designing the door, mechanism, and circuit that make this possible.

### What is constrained by the brief

| Constrained — not your choice | Open — your design decisions |
|---|---|
| Pre-made building façade (provided) | Exact mechanism geometry and dimensions |
| Door opens automatically via rising temperature | Form and aesthetic treatment of the door panel |
| DC motor drives a crank or cam | Circuit component values (resistor, thermistor pairing) |
| Thermistor triggers a transistor switch | Motor mounting position inside the façade |
| Materials from the workshop palette | Any decorative choices |

### The design process in this project

You will move through the iterative cycle across all ten weeks. Right now you are at **Identify**: understanding the need and the user. By Week 3 you will be at **Specify**. Making begins in Week 10.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Writing a user/context statement">
**Context:** The Firefighter Barbie scenario.

**Weak version (too vague):**
"A firefighter needs to open a door in a building."

**Strong version (gives the brief real weight):**
"The building occupant may be trapped and unable to open the door manually. The firefighter arriving at the scene needs the door to open automatically when heat exceeds a threshold — without any manual contact — so that entry can happen immediately even if visibility is poor or the door handle is too hot to touch."

Notice: the strong version names the user (occupant and firefighter), the context (fire, poor visibility, hot handle), and the design requirement (automatic, no manual contact, threshold-triggered).
</WorkedExample>

<Section title="Exam practice — sketching fundamentals 1" icon="✏️" id="sketching">

Controlled freehand sketching is the fastest way to communicate a design idea on Paper 1. Three fundamentals this session:

1. **Line confidence** — straight lines drawn with a single smooth stroke (not drawn back and forth). Practise by ghosting: hover the pencil along the intended path before touching the paper.
2. **Ellipses** — circles in a sketch are ellipses. Draw them in one oval motion, not in two arcs. Correct proportion matters.
3. **Crating** — draw the bounding box of the object first (light construction lines), then sketch the actual form inside the box. Remove the construction lines mentally — don't erase them; they show your working method.

Practise on the warm-up sheet: lines, ellipses, and three crated everyday objects (mug, stapler, door handle). Aim for controlled lines, not perfect ones.

</Section>

<Activity title="Project launch exercises" time="90 min (W session)" grouping="Individual" materials={["Project brief sheet", "A4 folder page", "Pencil and ruler"]}>
1. In your project folder (page 1): write a **user/context statement** identifying the building occupant, the firefighter, and what the emergency scenario requires of the door system.
2. Draft a **one-sentence design brief** using this frame: "I am designing a [door system] for a [building façade] that [opens automatically when temperature exceeds a threshold] so that [a firefighter can enter without manual operation]."
3. On the warm-up sketch sheet: complete lines, ellipses, and three crated objects. Note on your sheet which line felt most controlled and which needs more practice.
</Activity>

<Support title="Writing scaffolds">
**User/context sentence starter:** "The [user] needs [product] to [action] because [situation], which means the design must [requirement]."

**Design brief frame:** "I am designing a \_\_\_\_ for a \_\_\_\_ that \_\_\_\_ so that \_\_\_\_."

**For sketching:** Use a grid or pre-printed dotted paper if freehand proportion is difficult. The grid is a scaffold, not a crutch — try without it once you feel more confident.
</Support>
