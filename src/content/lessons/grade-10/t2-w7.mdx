This session opens with 40 minutes of exam conditions — Paper 1 parts (c), (d) and (e) for a fresh brief. After that, the workshop opens and your engineering drawing goes to work for the first time. Bring the printed drawing from Week 6.

<Callout type="exam" title="Graded Moment 1 — 20% of term grade" id="graded">
  **M1 is sat in the first 40 minutes of this session under exam conditions.** Format: a fresh product brief (not the desk organizer) — part (c) three conceptually different annotated ideas, part (d) choose one with criteria-based justification, part (e) a more detailed development sketch of the chosen idea annotated for material, join, and function. <Section title="M1 exam strategy — parts (c)(d)(e)" icon="📝" id="exam">

### Part (c) — the three ideas rule

The marker asks: **are these three genuinely different?** Three rectangular boxes with different numbers of compartments are not three different ideas. Different ideas differ in form, mechanism, or concept:

- Idea 1: horizontal tray — flat, single layer of compartments
- Idea 2: vertical tower — components stacked vertically with drawer action
- Idea 3: wall-mounted — hangs on the wall, uses different structural logic

Each requires at least one annotation: material, join, or function note.

### Part (d) — justification structure

"I chose Idea ___ because it meets the brief criterion of [criterion from the brief]. [Specific evidence from the design — a dimension, a material choice, a mechanism detail]."

Do not say: "I chose Idea 2 because it looks best."
Do say: "I chose Idea 2 because the vertical tower form fits within the 200 mm height limit and keeps the 300 mm horizontal footprint requirement (brief constraint 1), while the drawer action allows the user to access contents without lifting the entire product."

### Part (e) — more detail

More detail means:
- Every visible component named
- At least two materials stated with one property each
- At least one joint shown and labelled
- A note about how two components connect

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Workshop station — marking out from the engineering drawing">

**Before you start:** read the engineering drawing. Identify the dimension of the component you are cutting next. Write it on a sticky note and stick it next to your workpiece.

**Marking sequence:**
1. Select datum edge — longest, straightest edge. Check with try square.
2. Mark length and width from the drawing dimensions.
3. Use try square to transfer the perpendicular line.
4. Label the waste side (cross on the waste).
5. Measure both diagonals — equal diagonals confirm a square.
6. Cut on the waste side of the line.
7. After the cut: measure the cut component against the drawing. Record in your accuracy log.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="M1 exam + Making I" time="90 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["M1 exam paper (distributed by teacher)", "Engineering drawing printout from W6", "All workshop materials and tools", "Accuracy log sheet"]}>

**M1 exam (0–40 min) — exam conditions:**
1. Read the brief for 5 minutes.
2. Part (c): three conceptually different annotated ideas (15 min).
3. Part (d): choose one, justify with brief criteria (5 min).
4. Part (e): development sketch with full annotation (15 min).
5. Teacher collects scripts at 40 minutes.

**Making I (40–90 min):**
6. Safety briefing refresher (5 min).
7. Workshop rotation (technician supervised): mark out all desk organizer components from the engineering drawing.
8. Cut at least two components. After each cut, enter the result in the accuracy log.
9. At 80 min: count and label all cut components. Write what remains to cut for W8.
</Activity>

<Support title="For M1: handling a blank page at part (c)">

If you cannot think of three different ideas immediately, start by writing three different **structural logics**: (1) flat and horizontal, (2) vertical/tall, (3) wall-mounted or hung. Then sketch one form for each logic. You now have three structurally different ideas — even if the annotation comes second.
</Support>
