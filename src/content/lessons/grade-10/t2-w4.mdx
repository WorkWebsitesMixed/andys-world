From this session onwards, your folder decisions become physical material. Before any cut, you need: correct measurement, correct tool, correct safety procedure. All three must be in place before you touch the material.

<Callout type="exam" title="Paper 3 Section A — making processes" id="why">
  Paper 3 asks you to name tools, match them to materials, and describe safe use. Knowing the correct tool for each cut type — and being able to say why — is directly tested. Today's workshop experience is Paper 3 revision.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Datum edge", definition: "The reference edge from which all measurements are taken. Always mark out from a datum edge, not from a previously cut edge (which may be slightly out of square). The datum is usually the straightest edge of the workpiece, checked with a try square." },
  { term: "Try square", definition: "A tool with a stock (handle) and a blade set at exactly 90°. Used to check that an edge is square (perpendicular) to the datum, and to transfer a line across a face at 90° to the datum edge." },
  { term: "Workability", definition: "How easily a material can be cut, shaped and joined using available tools and machines. Pine has high workability (easy to cut with a tenon saw); mild steel has low workability (needs power tools for most cuts). Acrylic has moderate workability — it can be scribed and snapped, or cut slowly with a fine-tooth saw." },
  { term: "Parametric CAD", definition: "Computer-Aided Design where dimensions are entered as editable numbers, not just drawn. In OnShape, a sketch becomes 'fully constrained' when every line has a defined dimension and position — all lines turn black. A parametric model updates automatically if you change a dimension." },
]} />

<Section title="Marking out and cutting tools" icon="🔧" id="tools">

| Operation | Tool | Material | Safety note |
|-----------|------|----------|-------------|
| Mark out timber | Marking gauge + pencil | Pine, MDF | Gauge towards you on the pull stroke |
| Mark out metal/acrylic | Steel rule + scriber | Steel, aluminium, acrylic | Scriber is sharp — keep other hand clear |
| Straight cut — timber | Tenon saw | Pine, MDF | Secure in bench vice; cut on waste side |
| Curved cut — timber | Coping/scroll saw | Pine, MDF | Feed slowly; keep fingers clear of blade |
| Straight cut — acrylic | Scribe-and-break | Acrylic sheet | Score 5–7 passes; clamp to edge; snap cleanly |
| Holes | Pillar drill | Any | Use correct bit type; clamp workpiece; wear eye protection |

### OnShape basics — key concepts

**Sketch tools:** Line, Rectangle, Circle. Build 2D shapes from these.

**Constraints:** Horizontal, Vertical, Coincident. These lock lines to specific relationships.

**Dimension tool:** Adds a number to a line or distance. When every line is dimensioned or constrained, the sketch turns fully black — this is "fully constrained."

**Why fully constrained matters:** An under-constrained sketch (some lines blue/grey) can shift when you extrude it. Only fully constrained sketches produce predictable 3D solids.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Marking out a 150 × 100 mm rectangle in pine">

1. **Select datum edge:** the straightest long edge of your pine off-cut. Check it with the try square.
2. **Mark width (150 mm):** place steel rule on the datum, mark at 150 mm with a pencil cross.
3. **Mark length (100 mm):** use the try square to transfer a line at 90° from the datum at the 100 mm position.
4. **Check:** measure both diagonals — if they are equal, the rectangle is square.
5. **Label waste side:** put a cross on the material outside your cut lines. You cut on the waste side of the line.
6. **Secure and cut:** hold the piece in the bench vice. Tenon saw starts on the pull stroke (pulling back creates the kerf on the correct side of the line).
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Marking out + OnShape introduction" time="90 min" grouping="Individual (technician rotation at machines)" materials={["Pine off-cut", "Marking gauge", "Try square", "Steel rule", "Pencil", "Tenon saw or acrylic scriber + sample", "Student device with browser access to onshape.com"]}>

**Workshop — marking and cutting (0–72 min):**
1. Mark out at least two desk organizer components on your chosen material(s), working from your making plan.
2. Make one straight cut using the tenon saw (pine) or the scribe-and-break method (acrylic) — supervised by the technician.
3. After the cut: check the dimension against your making plan. Note any discrepancy.

**Safety check (72–75 min):**
4. Return all tools to their correct position. Sweep sawdust into the waste bin. Check that the bench vice is released.

**OnShape — first sketch (75–90 min):**
5. Log in at onshape.com; create a new document called "Desk Organizer."
6. Start a new sketch on the Front plane; use the Rectangle tool to draw a rectangle.
7. Add dimensions: type the width and height from your making plan. Confirm all lines are black (fully constrained).
8. Screenshot your completed sketch.
</Activity>

<Support title="OnShape quick-start">

**If all lines are blue/grey:** you have not fully dimensioned the sketch. Add dimensions to any remaining blue lines using the Dimension tool (D key).

**If a line snaps to the wrong position:** press Undo (Ctrl+Z) and try again more slowly. Constraints apply automatically — if a line appears to jump, a constraint has been applied that you did not intend.
</Support>
