Last week you saw how a crank turns rotary motion into reciprocating movement. Cams do the same job but give you control over the *shape* of the motion — the door can rise slowly, stay open for a fixed period (dwell), then close. This week you choose between crank and cam for your Firefighter Barbie door, justify the choice in writing, and lock in your final mechanism design.

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Cam profile", definition: "The outline shape of the cam disc. The profile determines the follower's movement pattern (how fast it rises, how long it dwells, how it falls)." },
  { term: "Pear cam", definition: "A cam with a pointed lobe and a circular body. Produces a distinct rise (lobe pushes follower up), dwell (circular section — follower stays at height), and fall (follower drops back)." },
  { term: "Follower", definition: "The component that rests on the cam surface and moves as the cam rotates. Converts the cam's rotation into the output motion." },
  { term: "Dwell", definition: "A period in the cam cycle where the follower height does not change — the cam profile is circular at that point. Used to hold a door open for a fixed time." },
  { term: "Stroke (cam)", definition: "The total rise of the follower from its lowest to its highest position. Determined by the difference between the smallest and largest cam radius." },
]} />

<Section title="Cam profiles and follower types" icon="🔧" id="cams">

### The three cam profiles you need to know

| Profile | Shape | Motion produced | Best use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pear | Rounded lobe + circular body | Rise → dwell → fall | Door that stays open then closes |
| Snail / heart | Constantly changing radius | Gradual continuous rise, rapid fall | Constant-speed rise applications |
| Eccentric | Off-centre circle | Smooth, continuous oscillation | Gentle back-and-forth motion |

### Two follower types

- **Knife-edge follower:** Simple, cheap, follows the cam profile precisely — but wears quickly. Good for light loads, low speed.
- **Roller follower:** A small wheel at the tip rolls against the cam surface. Greatly reduces friction and wear. Recommended for the Firefighter Barbie mechanism where repeated operation is needed.
- **Flat-face follower:** A flat plate rests against the cam. Works with concave cam sections but cannot follow sharp internal profiles.

### Tracing follower motion on a pear cam

As the cam rotates through 360°:
- **Rise:** The lobe comes under the follower and pushes it upward (door opens).
- **Dwell:** The circular section of the cam is under the follower — follower stays at its highest point (door remains open).
- **Fall:** The follower drops back to the body radius (door closes, spring or gravity assisted).

### Crank vs cam — choosing for the project

| Criterion | Crank | Cam |
|---|---|---|
| Predictable stroke | Yes — exactly 2 × arm length | Depends on profile |
| Dwell (door stays open) | Not without extra linkage | Yes — pear cam provides dwell |
| Adjustability | Change arm length | Change cam profile |
| Complexity | Lower — fewer parts | Slightly higher — cam + follower |
| IGCSE exam coverage | Covered in §4.3.2 | Covered in §4.3.1 |

**For the Firefighter Barbie project:** a crank gives a defined, predictable stroke — good for a door that must open a fixed distance. A pear cam gives a dwell period — good if the door must stay open long enough for the firefighter to enter. Both are valid choices; your written justification must use these technical reasons, not "I prefer the cam" or "the crank is simpler."

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Pear cam — tracing the follower">
**Setup:** A pear cam mounted on a motor shaft, with a roller follower pressing against it from above.

**Full rotation trace:**
- 0° – 90°: Lobe rises under the follower. Follower pushes upward. Door pivots open.
- 90° – 180°: Circular dwell section. Follower stays at maximum height. Door stays open.
- 180° – 270°: Lobe passes; follower drops back to body radius. Door closes.
- 270° – 360°: Circular body section. Follower at minimum height. Door fully closed.

**Annotation on the sketch:**
- "Pear cam — mild steel — hardened surface resists wear from roller contact"
- "Roller follower — reduces friction compared to knife-edge — extends cam life"
- "Dwell section — keeps door open for 180° of rotation — approximately 1 second at typical motor speed"
</WorkedExample>

<Section title="Exam practice — annotation and rendering" icon="📝" id="annotation">

### What earns marks in annotation (direct prep for M1)

The Week 6 bonus session (M1) is a timed test of Paper 1 parts (a) and (b). Part (b) requires two annotated sketch methods. This session is your final practice before the test.

Annotation that earns marks must include one of:
- **Material** — name it and give a reason: "mild steel crank arm — strong in tension and compression"
- **Join** — name it and give a reason: "M4 bolt — removable for maintenance without tools"
- **Function** — name the feature's purpose: "connecting rod pivot — allows relative rotation as the crank sweeps"

### Simple rendering for material indication

- **Metal:** Hatching — parallel diagonal lines, evenly spaced.
- **Wood:** Wavy grain lines following the surface direction.
- **Plastic/acrylic:** Light stippling or left blank with a label.

Rendering is not compulsory on Paper 1, but it differentiates good work from excellent work and takes under 30 seconds per surface.
