<Section title="The NTC thermistor and voltage divider" icon="🔧" id="thermistor">

### NTC thermistor behaviour

NTC stands for **Negative Temperature Coefficient** — as temperature rises, resistance falls.

| Temperature | Thermistor resistance | Effect on circuit |
|---|---|---|
| Cold (room temp) | High (e.g. 10,000 Ω) | Low voltage at divider junction — transistor OFF — motor stopped |
| Warm (hand or breath) | Medium (e.g. 3,000 Ω) | Rising voltage at junction |
| Hot (fire conditions) | Low (e.g. 500 Ω) | High voltage at junction — transistor switches ON — motor runs |

### The voltage divider: how it converts resistance to voltage

Connect the thermistor in series with a fixed resistor between the supply (+9 V) and 0 V. Take the output voltage from the junction between the two components:

**Junction voltage = Vsupply × Rfixed / (Rthermistor + Rfixed)**

As temperature rises: Rthermistor decreases → denominator decreases → junction voltage increases → eventually exceeds the base threshold → transistor ON → motor runs.

**Choosing the fixed resistor:** Match it to approximately the thermistor's resistance at the trigger temperature. A 10 kΩ fixed resistor with a 10 kΩ thermistor gives exactly half the supply voltage at room temperature — well below the base threshold. As the thermistor warms and its resistance drops below the fixed resistor value, the junction voltage rises above the threshold.

### LDR comparison

An **LDR (Light Dependent Resistor)** works on the same principle, but responds to light instead of temperature. Resistance decreases as light increases. The same voltage divider and transistor circuit applies — the only difference is the sensing element. This makes Section A questions predictable: if you understand the thermistor circuit, you understand the LDR circuit.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Tracing the voltage at the divider junction">
**Circuit:** 9 V battery, 10 kΩ fixed resistor in series with a 10 kΩ NTC thermistor.

**At room temperature (thermistor = 10 kΩ):**
Junction voltage = 9 × 10,000 / (10,000 + 10,000) = 9 × 0.5 = 4.5 V

Transistor base threshold ≈ 0.7 V — but 4.5 V is much higher than 0.7 V, so why isn't the transistor already on? Because there is a **base resistor** (typically 10–47 kΩ) between the junction and the base, which limits base current so the transistor stays in the linear region rather than saturating at room temperature.

**At elevated temperature (thermistor = 1 kΩ):**
Junction voltage = 9 × 10,000 / (1,000 + 10,000) = 9 × 0.909 = 8.2 V → transistor fully saturated → motor at full speed.

In practice, the trigger point is set by the value of the base resistor and the transistor's gain. For this project, hand warmth (≈35°C) should be sufficient to trigger the motor.
</WorkedExample>

<Section title="Bonus session: workshop safety and makerspace induction" icon="🏠" id="safety">

### Safety symbols — three categories

- **Mandatory (blue circle):** You must do this action. Examples: "Eye protection must be worn," "Hearing protection required."
- **Prohibition (red circle, diagonal bar):** You must not do this. Examples: "No loose clothing," "Do not use mobile phones near machinery."
- **Warning (yellow triangle):** A hazard is present — take care. Examples: "Danger: rotating machinery," "Caution: hot surface."

**Exam note:** The category is identified by shape and colour, not by the specific symbol inside. A yellow triangle always means warning regardless of what is depicted inside it.

### PPE required in the makerspace

- **Safety glasses:** Always when using any cutting tool, drilling, or sanding.
- **Apron:** Protects clothing and provides a barrier from chips.
- **Closed-toe shoes:** Mandatory — no exceptions for sandals or open-toe footwear.

### Safety check: pass mark = 100%

You must correctly identify all safety symbols shown before you are signed off for makerspace access. This is not negotiable — it is a health and safety requirement, not an academic assessment.

</Section>

<Support title="Troubleshooting the circuit">
**Motor does not activate at all:** Check the battery connection — is the positive terminal connected to the correct rail? Check the transistor orientation — base, collector, emitter must match the circuit diagram (not interchangeable).

**Motor runs all the time (even cold):** The base resistor value may be too low. Try increasing it (e.g., from 10 kΩ to 47 kΩ) to raise the trigger threshold.

**Motor activates then stops immediately:** Check that the thermistor leads are securely in the breadboard — intermittent contact causes flickering.

**Breadboard guide:** Holes in the same horizontal row on each side of the central channel are connected. The two long rails (red and blue) run the full length of the board.
</Support>
