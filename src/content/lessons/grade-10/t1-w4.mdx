The motor in the Firefighter Barbie circuit spins. But a spinning shaft can't push a door open — it needs a mechanism to convert that rotation into the back-and-forth movement that swings the door. This week you learn the four types of motion and the slider-crank that makes the conversion. Next week you add cams. By Week 5 your mechanism design is finalised.

<Callout type="exam" title="Name motion types precisely" id="why">
  Paper 1 Section A asks you to name or identify motion types. Generic answers — "it moves back and forth" — earn zero. You must use the exact vocabulary: **rotary**, **linear**, **reciprocating**, **oscillating**. Each has a precise meaning and an exam examiner will not accept a synonym.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Rotary motion", definition: "Continuous spinning around a fixed axis. Example: motor shaft, wheel, cam rotating on a shaft." },
  { term: "Reciprocating motion", definition: "Back-and-forth movement along a straight line. Example: piston in an engine, slider in a slider-crank mechanism." },
  { term: "Oscillating motion", definition: "Back-and-forth movement along an arc (swinging). Example: pendulum, wiper blade, a door swinging on hinges." },

<Section title="The four motion types" icon="🔧" id="motion-types">

### Recognising motion types

| Motion type | Definition | Real-world example | Project example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rotary | Continuous spin around a fixed axis | Motor shaft, wheel | DC motor output shaft |
| Linear | Straight-line, one direction only | Drawer, conveyor belt | Door sliding straight out |
| Reciprocating | Back-and-forth along a straight line | Piston, hacksaw blade | Slider pushing door open and closing |
| Oscillating | Back-and-forth along an arc | Pendulum, wiper | Door swinging on hinges |

**Key distinction — reciprocating vs oscillating:** both are back-and-forth, but reciprocating is in a straight line and oscillating is along an arc. The Firefighter Barbie door **oscillates** (swings on a hinge), driven by a mechanism that converts the motor's **rotary** motion.

### The slider-crank mechanism

The slider-crank converts rotary into reciprocating:

1. The **crank arm** is fixed to the motor shaft. As the motor turns, the crank arm rotates.
2. The **crank pin** at the far end of the crank arm traces a circle.
3. The **connecting rod** links the crank pin to the **slider**.
4. As the crank arm rotates, the connecting rod pulls and pushes the slider back and forth in a straight line.

**Stroke** = the total distance the slider travels = 2 × crank-arm length.

Applied to the Firefighter Barbie door: the "slider" is replaced by a **pivot arm** attached to the door. As the crank arm rotates, the connecting rod pushes the door open, then pulls it back. The door oscillates; the connecting rod reciprocates.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Mapping the slider-crank to the Firefighter Barbie door">
**Bicycle pump (familiar context):** The handle rotates the crank arm → connecting rod pulls and pushes the piston → piston moves back and forth (reciprocating) → air is compressed.

**Firefighter Barbie door (project context):**
- Motor shaft → crank arm rotates (rotary)
- Crank pin → connecting rod links to door pivot arm
- Door pivot arm → door swings open then closed (oscillating)
- If you want the door to stay open, the crank must complete half a revolution and stop there — or you need a cam mechanism (Week 5).

**Labelling exercise:** On a given slider-crank diagram, label: (1) crank arm, (2) crank pin, (3) connecting rod, (4) slider or pivot arm, (5) fixed pivot point, (6) the motion type at each point.
</WorkedExample>
