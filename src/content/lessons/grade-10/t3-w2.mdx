The water bottle on your desk was not hand-made. It was produced in a high-speed automated process that runs thousands of cycles per hour. Today you learn the three main polymer commercial processes — how each works, what it makes, and how to describe it in a Paper 3 answer.

<Callout type="exam" title="Paper 3 Section B — polymer processes" id="why">
  Section B tests commercial processes in detail. "Name" questions score 1 mark. "Describe how it works" scores 3–5 marks and requires a numbered step sequence. "Give one advantage" scores 1–2 marks and must name the advantage with a specific reason. The cross-section sketch of the injection moulding tool is a common sub-part.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Injection moulding", definition: "Polymer granules fed from a hopper into a heated barrel; a rotating screw melts and pushes the material; molten polymer is injected under high pressure into a cooled steel mould; the mould cools the polymer until solid; ejector pins push the finished part out. Used for thermoplastics. Products: bottle caps, phone cases, pen bodies. Mould cost: >$10,000 — only economical at high volume." },
  { term: "Extrusion blow moulding", definition: "Molten polymer is extruded as a hollow tube (the parison) between open mould halves; the mould closes, pinching the bottom of the parison; compressed air is blown inside, inflating the parison against the mould walls; the mould cools; the product is ejected; any flash (excess polymer at the pinch) is trimmed. Used for HDPE and PE bottles." },
  { term: "Injection stretch blow moulding", definition: "Used specifically for PET drinks bottles. A preform (a thick-walled test-tube shape) is injection-moulded first; the preform is heated and stretched along its length; compressed air blows it outward into the bottle shape. This biaxial stretching makes PET stronger and clearer than plain blow moulding." },
  { term: "Vacuum forming", definition: "A flat sheet of thermoplastic is clamped over a mould and heated until soft; a vacuum pump removes the air beneath the sheet; atmospheric pressure pushes the softened sheet onto the mould surface; the mould cools; the formed shape is removed and trimmed. Suitable for open-topped shapes (packaging trays). NOT suitable for sealed hollow containers (water bottles) because it can only form open-top shapes — it cannot produce a bottle with an enclosed body and a separate neck." },
]} />

<Section title="Injection moulding — cross-section components" icon="🔧" id="injection">

A labelled cross-section of the injection moulding tool typically shows:

1. **Hopper** — funnel where polymer granules are loaded
2. **Heated barrel** — the cylinder where the polymer melts
3. **Screw** — rotates to mix and push molten polymer forward
4. **Nozzle** — where molten polymer exits the barrel and enters the mould
5. **Sprue** — the channel from nozzle into the mould cavity
6. **Mould cavity** — the negative of the product shape (often two halves: fixed + moving)
7. **Ejector pins** — push the finished part out when the mould opens
8. **Cooling channels** — carry water through the mould to solidify the polymer quickly

### Injection vs blow moulding — side-by-side

| Feature | Injection moulding | Extrusion blow moulding |
|---------|-------------------|------------------------|
| Starting form | Granules → melt → inject | Granules → melt → extrude as parison |
| Shaping | Filled into solid mould | Inflated with air inside hollow mould |
| Product type | Solid or hollow-to-one-end | Hollow, enclosed container |
| Products | Caps, cases, brackets | Shampoo bottles, milk jugs |
| Wall thickness | Controlled by mould geometry | Controlled by parison thickness + blow pressure |

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Paper 3 Section B — describing injection moulding">

**Question:** "Describe how injection moulding is used to produce a water bottle cap in large quantities. [4 marks]"

**Answer:**
"1. Polypropylene granules are fed from a hopper into a heated barrel, where a rotating screw melts the polymer and pushes it forward. [1 mark]
2. The molten PP is injected under high pressure into a cooled steel mould in the shape of the bottle cap. [1 mark]
3. The mould is kept cool by water channels; the PP solidifies into the cap shape. [1 mark]
4. The mould opens and ejector pins push the finished cap out; the cycle repeats — up to 50,000 cycles per hour. [1 mark]"

**Key: one step = one mark. Include the material state at each step.**
</WorkedExample>

**M**elt → **P**arison → **C**lose → **B**low → **C**ool → **E**ject → **T**rim

(Melt, Parison, Close mould, Blow air, Cool, Eject, Trim flash)
