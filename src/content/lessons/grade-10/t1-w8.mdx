The circuit is more than wires and components — it is a set of precise decisions. Too much current burns the LED. Too little base current and the transistor never switches on. This week you learn the calculation behind each decision and draw the transistor switch circuit that is the brain of the Firefighter Barbie system.

<Section title="Ohm's Law and the current-limiting resistor" icon="🔧" id="ohms-law">

### Ohm's Law: V = I × R

Three forms — one formula, three rearrangements:

- **V = I × R** (find voltage if you know current and resistance)
- **I = V / R** (find current if you know voltage and resistance)
- **R = V / I** (find resistance if you know voltage and current) ← most used in this project

### Calculating a current-limiting resistor for an LED

An LED has two critical values:
- **Forward voltage (VLED):** the voltage it drops when operating (typically 2 V for red/green, 3.5 V for blue/white).
- **Forward current (ILED):** the current it needs to operate at full brightness (typically 20 mA = 0.020 A).

The resistor must drop the remaining voltage above the LED's forward voltage:

**R = (Vsupply − VLED) / ILED**

After calculating R, always choose the **nearest standard resistor value above** the calculated value. Standard values follow the E12 or E24 series (common values: 100, 120, 150, 180, 220, 270, 330, 390, 470, 560, 680, 820, 1000 Ω...).

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Calculating R for an LED in the Firefighter Barbie circuit">
**Given:** Supply voltage = 9 V. LED forward voltage = 2 V. LED current = 20 mA.

**Step 1 — Write the formula:**
R = (Vsupply − VLED) / ILED

**Step 2 — Convert mA to A:**
20 mA = 0.020 A

**Step 3 — Substitute:**
R = (9 − 2) / 0.020 = 7 / 0.020 = 350 Ω

**Step 4 — Choose nearest standard value above 350 Ω:**
From the E12 series: 390 Ω is the next value above 350 Ω.

**Answer:** Use a 390 Ω resistor. This keeps the LED current just below 20 mA (safe), with no risk of burning the LED.
</WorkedExample>

<Section title="The NPN transistor as a switch" icon="🔧" id="transistor">

### The three terminals

- **Base (B):** The control input. A small current into the base switches the transistor on.
- **Collector (C):** Connected to the positive supply via the output device (motor or LED). When the transistor is on, current flows from collector to emitter through the output device.
- **Emitter (E):** Connected to 0 V (the negative rail). Current exits the transistor here.

### Switching action

- **Transistor OFF (base current = 0):** No path from collector to emitter. Output device (motor) receives no current. Motor stops.
- **Transistor ON (base current flows):** Transistor conducts from collector to emitter. Current flows through the motor. Motor runs.

**Key point:** A very small base current (microamps) controls a much larger collector current (milliamps or more). The transistor amplifies the control signal.

### Applied to Firefighter Barbie

The thermistor voltage divider (covered in Week 9) provides the base voltage. When temperature rises:
- Thermistor resistance falls → junction voltage rises → reaches base threshold → transistor switches on → motor runs → crank opens the door.
