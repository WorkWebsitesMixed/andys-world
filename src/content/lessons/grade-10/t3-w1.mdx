Almost everything you own was made at scale. The water bottle on your desk, your phone, your chair — each was produced using processes designed to run in the thousands or millions. This term you will analyse one of those products, understand how it was made, and redesign part of it. Paper 3 Section B tests exactly this knowledge.

<Callout type="exam" title="Paper 3 Section B — new territory" id="why">
  Paper 3 Section B is different from Section A. In Section A you explain material properties. In Section B you describe a commercial manufacturing process in sequence — how it works, step by step, for a named material. The four question types are: name the process, describe how it works, give an advantage, state a quality control check. Today you learn the format so you know what you are answering towards in Weeks 2–9.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "One-off (bespoke) production", definition: "A single unique item made to individual requirements. High unit cost; high skill level; long production time. Examples: a custom prosthetic limb, an architect's presentation model, a hand-built racing car. Each product is different from the last." },
  { term: "Batch production", definition: "A defined quantity of identical products made, then the production line is reconfigured for a different product. Flexible; moderate unit cost. Examples: a seasonal chocolate box, school furniture, printed textbooks. Batch production is common when demand is seasonal or the product range changes frequently." },
  { term: "Mass (line) production", definition: "A continuous flow of identical products, often on a moving assembly line; very high volume; low unit cost. Examples: plastic water bottles, smartphones, cars. The production line runs continuously without reconfiguration." },
  { term: "Continuous flow production", definition: "24-hour, uninterrupted production that cannot be paused mid-process without destroying the product or damaging equipment. Examples: steel strip rolling, paper manufacture, cement production, glass making. The product is not discrete items but a continuous output that is cut or filled later." },
  { term: "CAD/CAM", definition: "Computer-Aided Design produces the digital model. Computer-Aided Manufacture converts it to machine instructions (G-code) that a CNC machine executes. Advantages: speed, repeatability, ability to produce complex geometries, reduced material waste. CAD/CAM is what makes mass production at high quality and low cost possible." },
]} />

<Section title="Four scales of production" icon="🏭" id="scales">

| Scale | Quantity | Unit cost | Flexibility | Example |
|-------|----------|-----------|-------------|---------|
| One-off | 1 | Very high | Maximum | Custom wheelchair, architect's model |
| Batch | Tens–thousands | Moderate | High | Seasonal chocolate box, school chairs |
| Mass / line | Thousands–millions | Low | Low | Plastic water bottle, smartphone |
| Continuous flow | Uninterrupted run | Lowest | None | Steel strip, paper, cement |

### Jigs and fixtures — why they matter in batch and mass production

A **jig** guides a tool (e.g. a drill jig locates the drill at exactly the right position every time). A **fixture** holds a workpiece in a fixed position during machining. Both eliminate the need for an operator to measure each time — the tool goes to the correct position automatically.

Without jigs and fixtures, every item would need individual measurement, increasing labour time and variability between items.

<WorkedExample title="How CAD/CAM enables mass production of a water bottle">

**Step 1 — CAD:** An engineer designs the bottle body in 3D CAD software. Every dimension is parametric — the wall thickness (0.2 mm), the neck diameter, the base profile.

**Step 2 — CAM:** The CAD file is exported and converted to G-code. For a blow-moulded PET bottle, this controls the extrusion of the parison (the hot plastic tube), the mould closure, and the blow pressure sequence.

**Step 3 — CNC / automated machine:** The blow moulding machine runs the G-code, producing up to 50,000 identical bottles per hour with no human measurement at each cycle.

**What CAD/CAM eliminates:** human measurement error between units; variability in wall thickness; need for skilled operators at every step.
</WorkedExample>

</Activity>

<Support title="How to tackle a 'describe how it works' question">

Use numbered steps. Each step should describe one action and the material state after that action. Example for injection moulding: "1. Polymer granules fed from hopper into heated barrel. 2. Rotating screw melts the polymer and pushes it forward. 3. Molten polymer injected under pressure into a cooled steel mould. 4. Mould cooled; polymer solidifies. 5. Mould opens; ejector pins push the finished part out."

One step = approximately one mark.
</Support>
