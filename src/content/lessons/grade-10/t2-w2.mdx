Every material choice you make in the desk organizer project must be justified by properties. Paper 3 Section A tests exactly that — you must name the property and link it to a function. Today you build the vocabulary that earns those marks.

<Callout type="exam" title="Paper 3 Section A technique" id="why">
  A common Paper 3 error: writing a material name without the property. "Mild steel is used for the frame" scores nothing. "Mild steel has high tensile strength, which means it can support heavy loads without stretching or breaking" scores the mark. Every answer needs: **property name → linked reason**.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Strength", definition: "The ability of a material to resist a load without breaking. Types: tensile (pulling force), compressive (pushing force), shear (sliding force), bending (transverse force). A material can be strong in one type and weak in another — mild steel is strong in tension and compression but can be sheared." },
  { term: "Hardness", definition: "The ability to resist scratching or indentation. Tested by the Vickers or Brinell test (pushing an indenter into the surface). Glass is hard; lead is soft. A hard material is not necessarily strong — glass is hard but brittle." },
  { term: "Toughness", definition: "The ability to absorb impact energy without fracturing. Tested by the Izod or Charpy impact test. Polycarbonate is tough; cast iron is brittle (low toughness). Hardness and toughness are often opposites: harder materials tend to be more brittle." },
  { term: "Ductility", definition: "The ability to be drawn into a wire without breaking. Copper is highly ductile; cast iron is not ductile at all. Ductility is a tensile property — the material must stretch under tension without snapping." },
  { term: "Density", definition: "Mass per unit volume (kg/m³). A dense material is heavy for its size; a low-density material is light. Aluminium is less dense than mild steel, making it preferred where weight is important." },
]} />

<Section title="Properties reference — four key materials" icon="📊" id="properties">

| Property | Pine (timber) | Mild steel (metal) | Acrylic/PMMA (polymer) | Aluminium (metal) |
|----------|--------------|-------------------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Tensile strength | Moderate (parallel to grain) | High | Moderate | High (for its density) |
| Hardness | Low–moderate | High | Low | Moderate |
| Toughness | Moderate | High | Low (brittle) | High |
| Ductility | None (wood is not ductile) | High | None | High |
| Density | Low (≈ 500 kg/m³) | High (≈ 7,800 kg/m³) | Moderate (≈ 1,180 kg/m³) | Low-moderate (≈ 2,700 kg/m³) |
| Aesthetic | Natural grain, warm | Industrial, can be polished | Translucent, can be coloured | Metallic, smooth |

### Physical and aesthetic properties

**Thermal conductivity:** how readily heat passes through. Metals conduct heat well (feel cold to touch); wood and polymers are poor conductors (thermal insulators).

**Electrical conductivity:** metals conduct electricity; wood and most polymers are insulators. Important for electrical enclosures.

**Aesthetic properties:** surface quality, grain pattern, colour, translucency. Acrylic offers translucency — useful when you want to see contents; pine offers warm natural grain.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Answering a Paper 3 Section A question">

**Question:** "Explain why mild steel is a suitable material for the frame of a shelving unit. [2 marks]"

**Weak answer (0 marks):** "Mild steel is suitable because it is strong and durable."

**Strong answer (2 marks):** "Mild steel has high compressive and tensile strength, which means it can support heavy loads placed on the shelves without bending or breaking. [1 mark for property + 1 mark for function-linked reason]"

**The rule:** property name + "which means it" + function-linked consequence.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Properties exercises" time="90 min" grouping="Individual / pairs at testing station" materials={["Material sample set (pine off-cut, mild steel strip, 3 mm acrylic, aluminium strip)", "Scratch test kit", "Printed properties table sheet", "Paper 3 question sheet"]}>

**Starter card-match (0–10 min):**
1. Match six property names to six definitions on the card set.

**Properties table (10–55 min):**
2. Complete the properties table for all four materials (pine, mild steel, acrylic, aluminium) — five rows: tensile strength, hardness, toughness, ductility, density.
3. At the testing station: rank all four samples from hardest to softest using the scratch test. Record your ranking.

**Exam questions (55–80 min):**
4. Answer two Paper 3 Section A questions (one on wood properties, one on metals). Use the format: property name + functional reason.
5. Peer-mark: swap with a partner. Circle any answer that names a property without a functional reason.

**Reflection (80–90 min):**
6. Write which material property matters most for the structure of your desk organizer, and which matters most for the panels. Are they the same property?
</Activity>

<Support title="Property sentence frame">

"[Material] is suitable because it has [named property], which means it [function-linked consequence]."

Example: "Pine is suitable for the frame because it has moderate compressive strength, which means it can support the weight of contents in the compartments without crushing."
</Support>
