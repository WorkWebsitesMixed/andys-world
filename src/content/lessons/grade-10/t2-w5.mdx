The difference between a temporary and a permanent joint is not just how it looks — it determines whether the product can be disassembled for repair, which is directly a sustainability decision. Choose your joints with a reason.

<Callout type="exam" title="Paper 3 Section A — joining" id="why">
  Paper 3 joining questions always ask for justification: "Why is PVA not suitable for joining acrylic to pine?" or "Give one advantage of a temporary joint over a permanent joint." Today you practise giving technical justifications — not just naming the join.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Temporary joint", definition: "A joint that can be assembled and disassembled without damaging the components. Examples: machine screws and nuts, wood screws (can be removed), knock-down (KD) fittings (cam-lock and barrel nut). Temporary joints allow repair and disassembly — relevant to Repair and Reuse in the 6Rs." },
  { term: "Permanent joint — adhesive", definition: "A joint where components are bonded with adhesive and cannot be separated without damage. PVA (polyvinyl acetate) bonds wood-to-wood only. Contact adhesive bonds most surfaces including wood-to-acrylic and acrylic-to-acrylic. Epoxy resin bonds dissimilar materials. Never use PVA on polymer surfaces — it does not form a bond with acrylic." },
  { term: "Knock-down fitting", definition: "A temporary joining system designed for flat-pack furniture. A barrel nut is inserted in one component; a cam-lock in the other. A bolt connects them; rotating the cam locks the bolt. Fully reversible, repeatable, and requires no adhesive. Common in MDF flatpack furniture." },
  { term: "Extrude (CAD)", definition: "The operation that converts a 2D sketch face into a 3D solid by specifying a depth. In OnShape: select the sketch face → Extrude → enter depth in mm. The result is a 3D solid with the profile of the sketch and the depth you specified." },
]} />

<Section title="Joining method selection" icon="🔧" id="joining">

| Joint type | Bond type | Works on | Key advantage | Key limitation |
|-----------|-----------|---------|--------------|----------------|
| Wood screws | Temporary | Wood to wood | Can be removed; strong in shear | Poor in end grain without pilot hole |
| Machine screw + nut | Temporary | Any (through-holes needed) | Very strong; fully reversible | Requires aligned through-holes |
| KD cam-lock fitting | Temporary | MDF/panel boards | Flat-pack; repeatable | Not suitable for structural loads |
| PVA glue | Permanent | Wood to wood only | Strong wood bond; low cost | Does not bond acrylic or metal |
| Contact adhesive | Permanent | Most surfaces | Bonds dissimilar materials (wood + acrylic) | Immediate bond on contact — no repositioning |
| Epoxy resin | Permanent | Dissimilar materials | Strongest; works on any pair | Long cure time; expensive |

### Matching the join to the requirement

For the desk organizer:
- **Frame corners (pine to pine):** PVA + wood screws (strong permanent bond + mechanical backup)
- **Acrylic panel to pine frame:** contact adhesive (only adhesive that bonds both; permanent = no gap)
- **Dividers (removable):** wood screws only (allows future compartment reconfiguration)

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Justifying a joining choice in the folder">

**Component:** acrylic side panel bonded to pine frame.

**Option A — countersunk wood screw:** drills through the acrylic; risk of cracking acrylic at the pilot hole; visible fixings.

**Option B — contact adhesive:** no holes in acrylic; immediate bond; invisible from outside.

**Chosen: Option B (contact adhesive)**

"Contact adhesive is chosen because it bonds acrylic to pine without drilling holes in the acrylic panel (which risks cracking). The bond resists shear along the panel length. PVA was rejected because it does not form an adhesive bond with the acrylic surface."

This is the format for a folder justification: chosen join + technical reason + rejected alternative + reason for rejection.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Join sketches + development sketches + OnShape extrude" time="90 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["Join samples set (screw, bolt, KD fitting, PVA, contact adhesive, epoxy)", "A4 folder pages", "Student device"]}>

**Join sketches (0–45 min):**
1. Sketch and annotate two joining methods for your desk organizer:
   - One wood-to-wood join (label: materials being joined, join type, assembly direction)
   - One acrylic-to-frame join (label: materials, join type, why this adhesive not another)
2. Add a justification sentence for each: "I chose ___ because ___ [technical reason]. I rejected ___ because ___."

**Development sketches (45–65 min):**
3. Produce two development sketches showing two alternative frame constructions — each annotated with material, join type, and an approximate dimension. These are design thinking sketches, not finished drawings.
4. Peer feedback (65–70 min): a partner reads both development sketches and gives one comment: "The clearer of the two is ___ because ___."

**OnShape — extrude (70–90 min):**
5. Open your W4 OnShape document. Select the sketch face.
6. Extrude to the thickness of the component (e.g. 18 mm for a pine base panel).
7. Apply at least one Fillet to a top edge (radius: 3 mm).
8. Orbit the view; screenshot for the folder.
</Activity>

<Support title="Contact adhesive — safe use">

Contact adhesive contains solvent. **Ventilate the room before using.** Apply to both surfaces, allow to dry until tacky (not wet — approximately 5–10 minutes), then press together only once. The bond is immediate on contact and cannot be repositioned. If you press too early (when still wet), the bond is weak.
</Support>
