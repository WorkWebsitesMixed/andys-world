The product is assembled. Now it becomes a finished object — sanded, sealed, and tested against the specification you wrote in Week 3. A product that looks finished but has not been tested is not complete. Test first, then evaluate.

<Callout type="exam" title="Paper 3 Section A — finishing + testing" id="why">
  Paper 3 Section A asks about finishing processes (sanding, painting, varnishing) and testing. The revision drill at the end of this session is the most focused Paper 3 Section A practice before M3 — use it seriously.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Sanding sequence", definition: "Using progressively finer abrasive papers in sequence: coarse (80–120 grit) removes surface defects, medium (180–240 grit) smooths the surface, fine (320+ grit) prepares for finishing. Always sand with the grain on wood — across the grain creates scratches that show under the finish." },
  { term: "Surface finish", definition: "A coating or treatment applied to the surface of a material to improve aesthetics, durability, or resistance to moisture. For wood: primer + undercoat + topcoat (paint), or wax/varnish. For acrylic: buffing with metal polish gives a clear, sharp edge." },
  { term: "Specification testing", definition: "Comparing the completed product to each specification point with a named test method and a measured result. A point is passed only if the evidence is measurable and clearly linked. 'It looks right' is not evidence; '149 mm measured with a steel rule against a 150 mm specification' is evidence." },
  { term: "Paper 3 Section A timing", definition: "Approximately 1–1.5 minutes per mark. A 2-mark question should take 2–3 minutes maximum. Over-writing on low-mark questions uses time that is needed for higher-mark questions later in the paper. Write the key point first; add detail only if time allows." },
]} />

<Section title="Finishing sequence" icon="🎨" id="finishing">

### Wood — paint or varnish

| Stage | Grit / product | Purpose |
|-------|---------------|---------|
| 1 | 120 grit | Remove saw marks, rough patches, squeeze-out |
| 2 | 240 grit | Smooth for first coat |
| Apply coat 1 | Primer or varnish | Seal wood; raise grain |
| De-nib | 240 grit (very light) | Remove raised grain fibres between coats |
| Apply coat 2 | Undercoat / varnish | Build colour and coverage |
| Final sand | 320 grit or wire wool | Ultra-smooth surface |
| Apply coat 3 | Topcoat / final varnish | Durable, attractive finish |

**Always sand with the grain.** Cross-grain sanding leaves scratches that are visible under any finish.

### Acrylic edge — polishing sequence

1. File to remove any saw marks (smooth file, one direction).
2. Sand with 120 grit wet-and-dry.
3. Sand with 240 grit wet-and-dry.
4. Sand with 400 grit wet-and-dry.
5. Apply a small amount of metal polish to a soft cloth; rub in circular motion until the edge is clear and smooth.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Testing a specification point correctly">

**Specification point:** "The organizer must fit within a 300 × 200 mm footprint so it does not obstruct the desk working area."

**Weak test record:** "The organizer is about the right size."

**Strong test record:** "Test method: measure overall length and width with a steel rule. Result: length = 276 mm (within 300 mm limit — PASS); width = 198 mm (within 200 mm limit — PASS). Evidence: two measurements taken and recorded."

The strong version names the test method, gives actual measured values, compares them to the specification criterion, and states PASS or FAIL.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Finishing + specification testing + Paper 3 drill" time="90 min" grouping="Individual (technician rotation for finishing)" materials={["Sandpaper 120, 240, 400 grit", "Varnish or primer + paint", "Brushes", "Metal polish + cloth", "Steel rule", "Paper 3 drill sheet"]}>

**Demo (0–15 min):**
1. Watch teacher demonstrate sanding sequence on a pine off-cut (120→240 grit) and polish an acrylic edge sample. Photograph the before and after.

**Finishing (15–65 min):**
2. Sand a pine sample (120→240 grit) and polish an acrylic edge sample.
3. Apply final surface finish to the desk organizer: sand to 240 grit, then apply one coat of varnish or primer. Polish all acrylic edges to clear.
4. Photograph the organizer at this stage.

**Specification testing (65–75 min):**
5. Complete a specification test table for at least four specification points:
   - Column 1: specification point (copied from your W3 spec)
   - Column 2: test method
   - Column 3: result (with measurement)
   - Column 4: PASS or FAIL

**Paper 3 drill (75–90 min):**
6. Timed drill: 6 items covering all term content (properties, selection, joining, finishing, sustainability). Allow approximately 1.5 min per mark.
7. Self-mark. Write one revision target on your revision card: "Before M3, I need to review ___."
</Activity>

<Support title="If a specification point fails the test">

Write it honestly: "The organizer base is 283 mm wide — 3 mm outside the 280 mm specification. This is due to a marking out error. In a second iteration I would use a marking gauge rather than a pencil to mark the width line."

A honest failed test + a specific correction is worth more in the folder than a dishonest pass with no evidence.
</Support>
