<Section title="What to write in each section" icon="💻" id="content">

Use this as your in-session reference. Your teacher has provided: (1) a one-page experiment summary with data and observations, (2) lab photos uploaded to Overleaf, (3) a starter `.bib` file with three references.

### Introduction

- Context: why potassium chlorate decomposition matters (link to the curriculum theory you've covered with the Chemistry teacher).
- Theory: the balanced equation — use `\ce{}`.
- Aim: one sentence. ("This experiment investigates…")
- At least one `\cite{}` citation to a source in your `.bib` file.

### Materials and Methods

- List your apparatus and chemicals.
- Describe the procedure in past tense, passive voice.
- Insert the apparatus photo as a figure with a caption and cross-reference.

### Results

- Present the data table (use `booktabs`).
- Insert the second lab photo as a figure.
- Cross-reference both table and figure in the text.
- No interpretation — just data and observations.

### Discussion

- Interpret your results: what do they mean?
- Use `\ce{}` for every equation.
- Connect to theory: does your data confirm the expected outcome?
- Explain any anomalies.

### Conclusions

- One conclusion per main finding, linked to the aim.
- State whether the aim was achieved.
- Three to five sentences — not a summary of Discussion.

### References

- `\printbibliography` generates this automatically.
- Check: every source you cited appears here, and every source here is cited in the body.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="From a rough note to a Discussion sentence">
**Rough note from your observation:** "mass went down — oxygen came out"

**First draft:** "The mass decreased because oxygen was released."

**Improved (mark-level) — what to write in your `.tex` source:**

```latex
The steady decrease in mass observed in Table~\ref{tab:mass} is consistent
with the release of gaseous oxygen during the decomposition of potassium
chlorate, as described by the equation \ce{2KClO3 -> 2KCl + 3O2}.
The oxygen escaped from the open test tube, reducing the total mass of the system.
```

This earns marks by: (1) cross-referencing the data table with `\ref{}`, (2) using `\ce{}` for the balanced equation, and (3) explaining the mechanism (open system → gas escapes → mass decreases).
</WorkedExample>

<Support title="Common writing blocks and how to break them">
**Stuck on Discussion:** re-read your Results table. Pick one row of data. Ask "why did this value change?" That question is your Discussion sentence starter.

**Can't write the aim:** try this frame: "This experiment investigates [the reaction/property] of [substance] by [method], in order to [scientific purpose]."

**Figure not showing:** check (1) the filename in `\includegraphics{}` exactly matches the file in Overleaf (including extension), (2) the file is uploaded to the project (not just on your computer), (3) you have `\usepackage{graphicx}` and `\usepackage{float}` in the preamble.

**Bibliography empty:** check (1) `\addbibresource{refs.bib}` is in the preamble, (2) `\printbibliography` is before `\end{document}`, (3) you've compiled twice — biblatex needs two passes.
</Support>

<Section title="Homework & extension" icon="🏠" id="homework">

**If any section is incomplete:** finish it before Week 12. Arriving at peer review with an empty section means your partner cannot review that section — and you lose half the value of the session.

**All sections complete?** Reread your Discussion. For every sentence: can you trace it back to a row in your Results table or a figure? If a sentence in Discussion has no data behind it, either it belongs in Introduction (theory) or it needs to be cut.
