This is the last structured preparation session before the IGCSE exams. Your teacher has tallied the deduction cards from Weeks 3 and 6 and knows exactly where the class lost the most marks. Today you close those gaps — as a class for the top two, and individually for everything else on your personal card.

<Callout type="exam" title="Why this matters" id="why">
  Generic revision is the least efficient use of time before an exam. This session is data-driven: you work only on the items that actually cost you marks. A student who lost 4 marks on VLOOKUP and 3 on mail merge should spend 100% of today's time on those two things — not re-reading a chapter on networks they already understand.
</Callout>

<Section title="Practical: targeted consolidation" icon="💻" id="practical">

### Whole-class: the top two class-wide gaps

Your teacher will demonstrate the two items that appeared on the most deduction cards. These are the only items modelled whole-class today. Pay attention even if neither is on your personal card — they're the highest-probability mark losses in the real exam.

### Individual work: your personal deduction profile

Work through past paper questions targeting your own starred items. For each:

1. Attempt the question **from memory** — no prompt card yet.
2. If you can't start after 2 minutes, use the relevant prompt card.
3. Check against the mark scheme. Ask: did you lose the mark for the same reason as before, or a different reason?
4. Write one sentence: *"I lost the mark because ___, and next time I will ___."*

**Developing track:** after each question, write in one sentence what you did differently compared to the timed paper.

**Proficient track:** no prompt cards. Time yourself: aim for the mark allocation × 1.5 minutes per item.

### Whole-class debrief: one change for the real exam

At the end of the session, each student states — out loud — one specific thing they will do differently in the real exam. Not "try harder" or "be more careful" — something concrete:

- *"I will press F4 to add $ signs instead of typing them manually."*
- *"I will click Apply to All in the PowerPoint footer dialog."*
- *"I will write the VLOOKUP skeleton before filling in the arguments."*

### MCQ drill: how to read a question

Paper 1 MCQs use precise language. The difference between a correct and a wrong answer is often one word:

- "RAM is **volatile**" (correct) vs "RAM is **permanent**" (wrong).
- "HTTP **governs the transfer of web pages**" vs "HTTP **sends emails**" (wrong — that's SMTP).

For every MCQ you get wrong in the drill: don't just note the right answer — write **why** each wrong option is wrong. This forces you to distinguish, not just memorise.

### Your Paper 1 revision list

After the MCQ drill, list the topics where you scored below 60%. This is your independent study agenda before the exam. Be specific: "Networks — I confuse IMAP and POP3" is more useful than "Networks".

<WorkedExample title="Mark-scheme precision in action">
**Question:** "Explain what is meant by encryption."

Zero-mark answer: *"Encryption keeps data safe."*

One-mark answer: *"Encryption converts data into a coded format so that it cannot be read without a key."*

Two-mark answer: *"Encryption converts data into a coded format using an algorithm. Only a recipient with the correct decryption key can decode and read the data, so even if the data is intercepted during transmission it cannot be understood."*

The two-mark answer earns both marks by: (1) stating what encryption does to the data (converts/codes it), and (2) explaining the mechanism (requires a key to reverse; protects intercepted data). "Keeps data safe" earns nothing because it describes the goal, not the mechanism.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Personal gap reattempt" time="50 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["Laptop", "Your deduction card", "Past paper questions targeting your gaps", "Prompt cards (after 2 minutes if needed)"]}>
1. Look at your deduction card. Rank your starred items by how many marks they cost.
2. Start with the highest-cost item. Attempt a past paper question targeting that exact skill.
3. Check against the mark scheme. Write your "I lost it because / next time I will" sentence.
4. Move to the next item. Repeat until time is called.
5. In the final 10 minutes: your single highest-priority item, one more time, under self-imposed time pressure.
</Activity>

<Support title="If an item keeps defeating you">
If you've attempted the same technique three times and still can't get the mark:

1. Go back to the prompt card and read the steps **aloud**.
2. Do the steps **without looking at the question** — just follow the card.
3. Now try with the question.

If it still doesn't work, write a specific question to ask your teacher: not "I don't understand mail merge" but "I don't understand why my merge fields show literally instead of the data."
</Support>
