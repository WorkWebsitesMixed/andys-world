Part (c) is worth 12 marks — the single largest chunk of <ExamLink to="paper1">Paper 1</ExamLink> marks. It asks for three clearly different, annotated ideas for a given context: not variations of one idea, not rough thumbnails, but three distinct structural and functional approaches. Today you do it under timed exam conditions, then analyse what you produced.

<Callout type="exam" title="Why this matters" id="why">
  Part (c) of <ExamLink to="paper1">Paper 1</ExamLink> is **12 marks out of 50** — the highest-scoring part of the whole paper. In the real exam you have roughly 18 minutes for it. Today's graded submission (M1, 20% of the term) gives you real exam feedback on this skill while there is still time to improve it before November.
</Callout>

<Section title="What part (c) rewards" icon="✏️" id="partc">

### The three-idea rule

Each idea must be **visibly different** — if a student sitting next to you looked at your three sketches and could not tell them apart at a glance, two of them are not conceptually different.

Use structural categories as a starting point:
- **Shell idea:** the form is one continuous surface or sheet
- **Frame idea:** rigid members connected at joints
- **Freestanding vs wall-mounted vs suspended:** three different interaction types

### Annotation that earns marks

Every sketch needs at least three annotations. The strongest annotations answer one of:
- **Material:** "18 mm birch ply — stiff in bending"
- **Join:** "halving joint at corners for rigidity without hardware"
- **Function:** "circuit board housed in base cavity; LED faces user"
- **Dimension:** "shelf depth 120 mm to suit A4 folders upright"

Short, precise, technical — one line per annotation.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Three different ideas for a plant-collector display stand">

**Idea 1 — Tiered frame:** A stepped frame of three horizontal shelves on a vertical ladder, 18 mm birch ply. Annotation: "Halving joints at each rung — rigid without hardware. Load: 3 kg across three shelves."

**Idea 2 — Cantilevered wall bracket:** A single swept-arm bracket cut from 9 mm acrylic sheet, wall-mounted. Annotation: "Cantilever arm 250 mm — section tapered to put material where bending is greatest. Toggle switch at bracket root."

**Idea 3 — Column with rotating tray:** A circular base with a central pillar and a rotating tray at the top. Annotation: "Pillar: 50 mm diameter dowel. Tray: lazy-susan bearing. Tilt switch — light activates as tray spins."

**Why these are conceptually different:** different structural principle (ladder frame vs cantilever vs column), different user interaction (place vs wall-mount vs rotate), different circuit placement.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Graded M1 + improvement practice" time="90 min" grouping="Individual — exam conditions for first 30 min" materials={["Part (c) answer booklet", "Pencils — no ruler needed", "Mark scheme extract issued after submission"]}>

**Graded M1 submission (0–30 min, exam conditions):**
1. Teacher issues the context. Settle and read the brief carefully.
2. Produce three clearly different, annotated idea sketches in 25 minutes.
3. Scripts collected at 30 minutes.

**Self-marking and improvement (30–75 min):**
4. Teacher issues the mark scheme. Self-mark: circle every annotation that would earn a mark; cross through any sketch that looks too similar to another.
5. Pick your weakest idea and produce an improved version: more distinct structure, at least three annotations.
6. Write an evaluation of which idea is strongest (rehearses part d): one idea, two reasons linked to the context.

**Term 2 goal-setting (75–90 min):**
7. On a sticky note: "By the end of Term 2 my part (c) will be stronger because I will ____." Put it in your folder.
</Activity>

<Support title="Struggling to generate three different ideas?">

**Try a forced-categories prompt:** (1) What if it was mounted on the wall? (2) What if it was freestanding on the floor? (3) What if it hung from above? Three starting points that are structurally different by definition.

**Ideas look too similar?** Check: do all three use the same structural principle (all frames, all shelves)? If so, force one to use a different principle — cantilever instead of supported shelf, hanging instead of standing.

**Annotations feel too vague?** Use this sentence frame for every leader line: "[material or feature] — [why it is appropriate for this user/context]." If you cannot finish the sentence, the annotation is not yet worth marks.
</Support>
