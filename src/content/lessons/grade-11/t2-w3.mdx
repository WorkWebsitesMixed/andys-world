You have read about material properties in T1. Today you test them. The three practical tests carried out in this session give you primary research evidence that can be cited in your C2 justifications and in Paper 4 Section A answers. Every result you record becomes a data point that replaces a generic claim.

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Stiffness (Young's modulus)", definition: "A material's resistance to elastic deformation under load. Measured in GPa. A stiffer material deflects less for the same applied force. For a shelf, stiffness (not just strength) is often the controlling property — a stiff shelf does not sag." },
  { term: "Hardness", definition: "A material's resistance to surface indentation or scratching. Tested comparatively using the scratch test (harder material scratches softer). High hardness means the surface resists daily wear from stored items." },
  { term: "Machinability", definition: "How easily a material can be cut, drilled, or shaped using available tools. Factors include grain direction (wood), brittleness (ceramics), and tool requirements. High machinability means clean cuts and short processing time with school equipment." },
  { term: "Ductility", definition: "A material's ability to be deformed plastically without fracturing. Mild steel is ductile — it bends before breaking. Cast iron is brittle — it fractures without significant deformation. Ductility matters for workshop forming processes." },
]} />

<Section title="Materials testing — three methods" icon="🔬" id="testing">

### Test 1: Cantilever bending (stiffness comparison)

**Method:** Cut strips of each test material to identical dimensions (200 mm x 30 mm). Clamp each strip with 150 mm overhanging. Apply a standard 100 g (1 N) load at the free end. Measure the deflection at the free end with a ruler.

**Result:** Lower deflection = higher stiffness. Record deflection in mm for each material.

**Materials to test:** 18 mm MDF, 18 mm pine, 3 mm acrylic, 3 mm mild steel strip, 3 mm aluminium strip.

### Test 2: Surface hardness (scratch comparison)

**Method:** Each material gets a scratch test using three tools in increasing hardness order: (A) fingernail, (B) copper coin, (C) steel file. Record which tool leaves a scratch.

**Result:** Higher scratch resistance = higher hardness. Record as: resists A only, resists A+B, resists A+B+C.

### Test 3: Machinability (cut quality)

**Method:** Using a hand saw or scroll saw, cut across each material. Observe: does the material split along grain lines? Does the cut face require sanding? Any burr or tear-out? Rate each material 1 (poor) to 3 (excellent) for clean cut quality.

</Section>

<Section title="Material selection matrix" icon="📊" id="matrix">

### The weighted matrix method

1. List the evaluation criteria relevant to your specification.
2. Assign a **weight** (1–5) to each criterion based on its importance.
3. Score each material on each criterion (1–5: 1 = poor, 5 = excellent).
4. Multiply score by weight for each cell.
5. Sum the weighted scores — highest total wins.

| Criterion | Weight | MDF | Pine | Aluminium |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Stiffness for shelf | 5 | 3 (15) | 2 (10) | 5 (25) |
| Surface hardness | 3 | 3 (9) | 2 (6) | 4 (12) |
| Machinability in school | 4 | 5 (20) | 4 (16) | 3 (12) |
| Cost | 2 | 4 (8) | 3 (6) | 2 (4) |
| Sustainability | 3 | 2 (6) | 4 (12) | 3 (9) |
| **Total** | | **58** | **50** | **62** |

Aluminium scores highest here. The matrix allows specific justification: "Aluminium was selected over MDF primarily due to its higher stiffness (score 5 vs 3), weighted most heavily because the product must resist 5 kg without 2 mm deflection."

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Full material justification for a shelf: MDF vs aluminium">

**From test results:**
- MDF cantilever deflection at 100 g: 12 mm. Aluminium deflection at 100 g: 3 mm.
- MDF surface hardness: resists fingernail only. Aluminium: resists file (A+B+C).
- MDF cut quality: 3/3. Aluminium cut quality: 2/3.

**Justification statement:**
"Aluminium was selected for the shelf surface based on testing evidence. The cantilever deflection test recorded 3 mm for aluminium versus 12 mm for MDF — a factor of 4 improvement in stiffness. The specification requires less than 2 mm deflection at 5 kg (Spec point 1), and modelling indicates aluminium will meet this requirement where 18 mm MDF would not. The lower machinability score for aluminium (2/3 versus MDF 3/3) is accepted because the workshop has a band saw appropriate for aluminium, and the stiffness advantage outweighs the machinability compromise."
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Three materials tests and selection matrix" time="90 min" grouping="Pairs for testing, individual for write-up" materials={["Test strips: MDF, pine, acrylic, mild steel, aluminium (prepared by teacher)", "100 g standard weight", "Ruler (mm)", "Clamp or bench vice", "Copper coin, steel file for scratch test"]}>

**Testing phase (0–45 min):**
1. Conduct the three tests in rotation as pairs:
   - Bending test: record deflection in mm for each material strip.
   - Scratch test: record scratch resistance level for each material.
   - Cut quality: observe cut or record from teacher demo.
2. Record all results in a data table before moving to the next test.

**Matrix completion (45–65 min):**
3. Individually, build a weighted matrix for your Component 2 material decision. Choose criteria relevant to your specification. Assign weights. Score each candidate material using test data where available, textbook data otherwise.

**Justification writing (65–85 min):**
4. Write a 150-word material justification. Must include:
   - At least one numerical result from today's tests.
   - The specification point number addressed.
   - One acknowledged trade-off.

**Folder integration (85–90 min):**
5. Note which folder section (C2 or C4) this justification belongs to. Flag for insertion into your project folder.
</Activity>

<Support title="Testing and matrix help">

**Deflection results inconsistent?** Ensure the clamp position is the same for each strip (150 mm overhang). Apply the load at the same point (free end tip).

**Matrix results not matching intuition?** Review your weights. The stiffness weight should be high if the shelf must carry 5 kg. Adjust weights to reflect what your specification actually demands.

**Justification still generic?** Replace every claim with a number: "higher stiffness" → "4x less deflection at 100 g (3 mm vs 12 mm)". If no test number exists, find a textbook value: Young's modulus for aluminium is approximately 70 GPa vs 7 GPa for MDF.
</Support>
