<Section title="The lesson" icon="📋" id="lesson">

### 1. Three families of structure

Almost any structure you can name belongs to one of three families — or is a
combination of them:

- **Shell** — strength comes from the **outer surface**. A plastic bottle is
  surprisingly strong for its weight because of its thin curved skin.
- **Frame** — strength comes from **rigid bars (members) joined together**, like a
  roof truss or scaffolding.
- **Mass** — strength comes from **sheer bulk and weight**, like a dam or a
  thick stone wall.

### 2. Natural or man-made (a quick recap)

Remember last week: every structure is also either **natural** (a spider's web, an
eggshell) or **man-made** (a bridge, a crane). So each structure gets *two* labels
— its family *and* whether it's natural or man-made.

### 3. What keeps a frame rigid? Triangulation

Here's the key idea for your project. A **square** frame is *not* rigid: push it
sideways and it leans over into a parallelogram — it **racks**. That's because a
four-sided shape can change its angles while its sides stay the same length.

A **triangle can't do that.** It's the only polygon that can't be pushed out of
shape unless you change the length of one of its sides. So if you add a
**diagonal member** to a square, you split it into two triangles and it becomes
rigid. That's **triangulation** — and you'll see it everywhere once you look:
gates, bridges, shelves, bike frames.

<WorkedExample title="Worked example — classify and explain a stepladder">
1. **What is it made of?** Separate bars joined together — so it's a **frame** structure (not a shell or a mass).
2. **Natural or man-made?** Man-made.
3. **Could it rack?** On its own, the four-sided shapes between the bars could fold flat.
4. **What stops it?** The A-shape and the spreader form **triangles** — triangulation keeps it rigid and safe to stand on.

→ A stepladder is a **man-made frame structure, kept rigid by triangulation.**
</WorkedExample>
