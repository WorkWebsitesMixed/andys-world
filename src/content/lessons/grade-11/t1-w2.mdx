Good sketching is not about talent — it is about the right habits applied consistently. Today your drawing colleague demonstrates those habits live, and you practise until the technique is in your muscle memory. The crating method works for any 3D object, and once it clicks it becomes your fastest way to communicate a structural idea on paper.

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Crating", definition: "A sketching technique where a 3D object is first enclosed in a rectangular bounding box (the 'crate'), then the object's form is cut out of that box. Starting with the box gives correct proportions before any detail is drawn." },
  { term: "Thick/thin line", definition: "A convention used in freehand 3D sketches: thick lines mark visible, front-facing edges and silhouettes; thin lines mark construction edges, receding edges, and the crate itself. The contrast between line weights creates the illusion of depth." },
  { term: "Line confidence", definition: "Drawing a line in a single decisive stroke rather than a series of short overlapping strokes. Feathered lines (multiple scratchy strokes) look hesitant and make sketches harder to read. Confident lines communicate clearly." },
  { term: "Ellipse", definition: "The shape a circle makes when viewed at an angle. In 3D sketching, circular holes, cylinder ends, and rounded edges must be drawn as ellipses rather than as circles, otherwise the sketch loses its 3D quality." },
]} />

<Section title="The crating method" icon="✏️" id="crating">

### Step 1: Draw the bounding box first

Every 3D object fits inside a rectangular box. Before drawing any detail, sketch the box that would just contain your object — this is the crate. Use **thin, light lines** for the crate itself.

Proportion is the hardest part: the crate must have the correct ratio of width, height, and depth. If a shelf bracket is 200 mm wide, 150 mm tall, and 50 mm deep, your crate must reflect those proportions — wide and shallow, not square.

### Step 2: Cut the form out of the crate

Once the crate is drawn, sketch the actual form inside it. An L-shaped bracket uses the top-right corner of the crate; a cylinder uses the full height but only the central width. Remove or lighten the crate lines that fall inside the object.

### Step 3: Apply thick/thin line weighting

- **Thick lines:** the visible front edges, corners facing the viewer, and the silhouette outline of the object.
- **Thin lines:** the construction edges, receding edges going away from the viewer, and any remaining crate lines used as guides.

The contrast between thick and thin replaces shading — it does the same job in 10 seconds instead of 10 minutes.

### Ellipses on 3D faces

A circular hole on the front face of a block appears as a slightly flattened ellipse, not a circle. The flattening depends on the viewing angle: faces viewed nearly head-on have nearly circular ellipses; faces at a steep angle have very flat ellipses.

Rule of thumb: your ellipses on a standard 3D sketch should be roughly 30–40% compressed (if the circle's diameter is 40 mm, the minor axis of the ellipse is about 12–16 mm).

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Crating a shelf bracket in three stages">

**Stage 1 — Bounding box:** Draw a thin-line box roughly 3:1 width-to-height ratio, shallow depth (about 1/4 of the width). This is the maximum space the bracket occupies.

**Stage 2 — Cut the L-shape:** The bracket is an L-shape. Lightly sketch the horizontal shelf portion (top 1/3 of the crate) and the vertical support (right-hand 1/4 of the crate). Erase or fade the crate lines inside the bracket's solid area.

**Stage 3 — Line weighting:** Go over every visible front edge with a thick, confident stroke. The top edge of the shelf, the front face of the vertical support, the bottom corner. Leave the receding edges thin. The bracket instantly looks 3D.

**Common mistakes to fix:**
- Crate lines the same weight as the object lines (no depth contrast).
- Circular mounting holes drawn as circles not ellipses.
- Feathered lines along the top edge (redraw in one stroke).
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Warm-up and crating practice" time="90 min" grouping="Individual (drawing colleague demonstrates first 30 min)" materials={["A3 cartridge paper", "HB and 2B pencils (or one mechanical pencil)", "No ruler — freehand only"]}>

**Drawing colleague demo (0–30 min):** Watch the demonstration. Take note of:
- The speed of each stroke — confident, not cautious.
- How the bounding box goes down before anything else.
- Where thick lines appear vs thin lines.

**Warm-up (30–45 min):**
1. Draw 20 single-stroke horizontal lines across the page — no feathering.
2. Draw 10 ellipses at different sizes — aim for smooth, closed curves.
3. Draw 5 bounding boxes of different proportions (tall and narrow, wide and shallow, cubic).

**Main practice (45–85 min):**
4. Crate a mug: cylindrical body with a D-handle. Start with the bounding cylinder (box containing a cylinder), add the handle, apply line weights.
5. Crate a simple phone stand: a wedge shape with a slot. Box first, cut the wedge, add the slot as an ellipse.
6. Crate your own structural product idea (from Week 1 brief). Keep it simple — no detail, just the major form.

**Self-review (85–90 min):**
7. On your three sketches, tick where you have applied thick line correctly, circle any feathered lines, and write one target for next session.
</Activity>

<Support title="Getting crating right">

**Proportions feel wrong?** Before drawing, hold up your pencil at arm's length and measure the real proportions of a box on your desk. Width : height : depth. Transfer those ratios to your sketch. Proportion is a skill — practise with objects you can see.

**Lines keep feathering?** Speed is the cure. A slow line is a feathered line. Draw faster than feels comfortable — the line will be more confident even if it is slightly less accurate.

**Ellipses come out as flat lines or full circles?** Remember: ellipse, not circle, not dash. Practise drawing ellipses around a point (imagine a clock face, draw an oval around it).
</Support>
