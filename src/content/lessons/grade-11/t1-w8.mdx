<Section title="Research strategy" icon="🔍" id="research">

### Primary research methods

**User interview / questionnaire:** The most direct source. Ask your user:
- What specific items do they need to store? How many? What mass?
- What dimensions are available? (Measure the space on your user's desk, wall, or shelf.)
- What aesthetic preferences do they have? (Colour, material, style.)
- What safety concerns do they have? (Stability, sharp edges, electrical safety.)

Record responses verbatim where possible — direct quotes strengthen justifications.

**Measurement of existing products:** Measure a similar product on the market. Record dimensions (length, width, height, shelf thickness, load capacity from label). These become specification constraints.

### Secondary research methods

**Manufacturer specifications:** Find a product that partially solves the same problem. Note its dimensions, load rating, and materials. These give realistic benchmarks for your own specification.

**Safety standards:** For a product with an LED circuit, check that the supply voltage is below 50 V DC (safe low voltage). Mechanical safety: no sharp projecting edges, stable base.

### ACCESS FM analysis

Apply ACCESS FM to one existing product similar to your brief:

| Letter | Question | Example finding for a desk shelf |
|---|---|---|
| Aesthetics | Does it look appropriate for the context? | Natural wood finish — suits a study environment |
| Cost | What does it cost? | £35 retail — target similar price point |
| Customer | Who uses it? | 16–25, student, desk user |
| Environment | What is it made of? End-of-life? | MDF + steel brackets — MDF not recyclable |
| Safety | Any hazards? | Sharp bracket edges — should be smoothed |
| Size | Dimensions? | 600 mm wide x 200 mm deep x 150 mm tall |
| Function | What does it do? | Holds books upright, no LED integrated |
| Materials | Main materials? | MDF shelf, mild steel brackets, painted finish |

</Section>

<Section title="Writing a measurable, justified specification" icon="📋" id="specification">

### The three-part specification point

Every specification point needs three components:

1. **Criterion:** what the product must do or be.
2. **Measure:** how the criterion will be tested (specific, not vague).
3. **Justification:** why this criterion is required (linked to research).

**Template:** "The product must [criterion] as tested by [test method], because [research finding]."

### Examples: poor vs strong specification points

| Poor (unacceptable) | Strong (mark-worthy) |
|---|---|
| "It must look good." | "The product must have a surface finish with no visible machining marks, as tested by visual inspection at 300 mm, because the user stated they would place the product on a visible desk." |
| "It must be strong enough." | "The product must support a static vertical load of at least 5 kg for 24 hours without permanent deformation, as tested by applying a 5 kg deadweight, because the user stores textbooks with a measured total mass of 4.3 kg." |
| "It must be safe." | "All exposed edges must be rounded or chamfered to a radius of at least 1 mm, as tested by running a finger along all edges, because the product will be used in daily contact and sharp edges pose a laceration risk." |

### Six areas to cover

Your six specification points should span: load/structural performance, dimensions (size), aesthetics/finish, materials (sustainability), safety, and electronics (circuit function).

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Converting three research findings into specification points">

**Finding 1 (from user interview):** "My desk space is 600 mm wide and I want the shelf to fit along the back without overhanging."
Specification point: "The product width must not exceed 590 mm, as measured with a steel rule, because the user's available desk depth is 600 mm and the product must not overhang the desk edge."

**Finding 2 (from measurement of existing product):** "The textbook stack measured 4.3 kg on kitchen scales."
Specification point: "The product must support a minimum vertical load of 5 kg without measurable deflection (less than 2 mm at the midspan), as tested by applying a calibrated 5 kg deadweight for 5 minutes, because the user's textbook collection (measured at 4.3 kg) must be stored safely with a safety margin."

**Finding 3 (from secondary research — safety standard):** "LED circuits operating below 50 V DC are classified as Safe Extra-Low Voltage (SELV)."
Specification point: "The electrical circuit must operate at 12 V DC or below, as verified by checking the power supply specification, because any voltage below 50 V DC meets the SELV standard and presents no risk of electric shock to the user."
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Research review and specification writing" time="90 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["Your C1 design brief from Week 1", "Research notes from home task", "Ruler (for measuring existing product if brought)", "Pencil"]}>

**Research consolidation (0–25 min):**
1. Review your primary research (interview notes from Week 1 and any additional home research). Highlight five specific findings that can be turned into specification points.
2. Complete a mini ACCESS FM analysis for one existing product related to your brief (use a product you found in the Week 1 extension, or choose one from the teacher-provided examples).

**Specification writing (25–70 min):**
3. Write a minimum of six specification points. Use the three-part template: criterion + test method + justification. Cover at least: load, dimensions, aesthetics, material/sustainability, safety, and circuit.
4. For each point: underline the measure (the testable part) and circle the justification (the research link). If you cannot underline or circle, the point is incomplete.

**Brief finalisation (70–80 min):**
5. Review your C1 design brief from Week 1. Update any function points that have been refined by research. Ensure the brief names a specific user, states at least three function points, and includes one user-specified constraint.

**Submission check (80–90 min):**
6. Checklist before submission: C1 brief has a named user? Three or more function points? Spec has six or more points? Each point has a test method? Each point has a justification? If any item is missing, fix it now.
</Activity>

<Support title="Specification troubleshooting">

**"I cannot think of a test for this point."** If you cannot describe a test, the criterion is not yet measurable. Add a number, a dimension, or a condition. "It must look professional" → "It must have a consistent painted finish with no brush marks visible at 500 mm distance, as assessed by visual inspection."

**"I do not have enough research for justifications."** Use the measurements from an existing product as secondary research: "Measurement of a comparable product found on the market showed a width of 500 mm — this defines the upper size boundary for this product."

**"My specification is just restating the brief."** The specification refines the brief with numbers. If the brief says "it must hold textbooks," the spec says "it must support 5 kg vertically at the shelf midspan with less than 2 mm deflection."
</Support>
