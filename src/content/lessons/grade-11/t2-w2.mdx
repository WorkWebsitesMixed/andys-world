The ideas you generated at the end of Term 1 were a starting point, not an end point. Today you develop them, stress-test them against your specification, and select the one to take forward into development and making. This session closes <ExamLink to="project">Component 2</ExamLink> criterion C3 — worth 20 marks.

<Callout type="exam" title="Why this matters" id="why">
  C3 (idea generation) is worth **20 marks out of 100** in <ExamLink to="project">Component 2</ExamLink> — the single largest design criterion. Marks come from ideas that are **genuinely different** from each other, **annotated against the specification**, and followed by an **objective selection**. A matrix that just picks the idea you already liked scores near zero; one that weighs ideas against measurable specification points scores well.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Developing an idea", definition: "Adding detail and resolution to a sketch concept — deciding how the structure is joined, what material the main frame uses, how dimensions relate to the specification. Developing is different from generating: you are resolving questions, not generating more options." },
  { term: "Evaluation matrix", definition: "A table that scores each design idea against a set of weighted specification points. Each cell gets a score (e.g. 0–3); the scores are totalled; the idea with the highest weighted total is the rational choice. The matrix makes the selection process visible and objective." },
  { term: "Justification", definition: "Explaining why the chosen idea best meets the specification — specifically, which spec points it satisfies better than the alternatives. A justification goes beyond 'I like it best': it connects the choice to evidence from the matrix." },
  { term: "Design fixation", definition: "Unconsciously favouring one idea from the start and going through the motions of evaluation without genuinely considering the alternatives. Spotted by examiners when all alternatives score unusually low." },
]} />

<Section title="Developing and selecting ideas" icon="💡" id="developing">

### From idea to developed proposal

An idea sketch from Term 1 may only show the overall form. Development adds the missing information: what material is the main structure? What joining method connects the members? Where does the switch sit, and can the user reach it easily? What are the key dimensions, and do they fit within 400 × 400 mm?

Work through each of your Term 1 ideas and add one development page per idea: a refined sketch plus brief notes answering those questions.

### Building the evaluation matrix

Set up a table with your ideas as columns and your specification points as rows.

1. Choose 4–6 specification points that most differentiate the ideas.
2. Assign a weight to each point (total weights = 10 is easy). Weight higher the points that matter most to your user.
3. Score each idea 0–3 on each point (0 = fails, 1 = partial, 2 = meets, 3 = exceeds).
4. Multiply score × weight; sum each column. The highest total is the rational choice.

### Writing the justification

Three sentences is enough: (1) which idea scored highest and on which key spec points; (2) why that matters for your specific user; (3) what the main weakness of the chosen idea is and how you will address it in development.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Evaluation matrix for a cyclist gear organiser">

Three ideas: (A) wall-mounted pegboard frame, (B) freestanding tower frame, (C) low suspended hanging frame.

| Spec point | Weight | A score | B score | C score |
|------------|--------|---------|---------|---------|
| Holds 4 kg | 3 | 3×3=9 | 3×3=9 | 2×3=6 |
| Fits 400 mm width | 2 | 3×2=6 | 2×2=4 | 3×2=6 |
| Circuit easily accessible | 2 | 3×2=6 | 2×2=4 | 1×2=2 |
| Easy to mount/unmount gear | 3 | 2×3=6 | 3×3=9 | 1×3=3 |
| **Total** | | **27** | **26** | **17** |

**Justification:** Idea A (wall-mounted pegboard frame) scores highest overall, performing best on load-bearing and circuit accessibility. It suits the user's wall-mounted preference and keeps gear at eye level. Its main weakness is that it requires wall fixings — I will address this in development by designing the bracket attachment to suit plasterboard anchors.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Develop, evaluate, select" time="90 min" grouping="Individual (drawing colleague circulates for sketch quality)" materials={["Term 1 idea pages", "Specification from T1 W8", "Evaluation matrix template"]}>

**Develop each idea (0–30 min):**
1. For each of your three Term 1 ideas, add a development page: refine the form and add notes on material, joining method, dimensions, and switch placement.

**Build the evaluation matrix (30–60 min):**
2. Set up the matrix: ideas as columns, 4–6 specification points as rows, weights assigned.
3. Score each idea on each point (0–3), multiply by weight, total each column.

**Select and justify (60–80 min):**
4. Write a three-sentence justification: which idea, why it scores highest on the key points, and what one weakness you will address in development.

**Tidy and file (80–90 min):**
5. Order your C3 evidence: all idea sketches (Term 1) → development pages → matrix → justification. This is your C3 folder submission.
</Activity>

<Support title="Matrix feels arbitrary?">

**All ideas scoring the same?** You may be scoring on points that do not differentiate the ideas. Choose spec points where the ideas genuinely behave differently — load capacity, dimensions, user access, circuit integration.

**Can't decide on weights?** Ask: which of these spec points, if a product failed it, would most let down your user? That point gets the highest weight.

**Justification feels circular?** Don't just repeat the matrix totals. Explain what the numbers mean for the user: "Idea A scores highest on load capacity because its triangulated frame distributes weight to four fixing points, which matters for my user who needs to hang heavy equipment."
</Support>

<Section title="Homework" icon="🏠" id="homework">

**Home task:** Tidy your chosen-design page. Then list everything you must **test** during development: which spec points are measurable (load, dimensions, circuit function) and how you would set up each test. Bring this list to Week 5.

<Extension title="Hybrid design">
Create a fourth 'hybrid' idea that deliberately combines the strongest feature from each of your three ideas. Run it through your existing evaluation matrix. Does it score higher than your chosen idea? If so, does it reveal a weakness in the way you weighted your matrix, or is it genuinely a better design?
</Extension>
