Term 2 opens with your selected idea from T1 W10 in hand. Development is not the same as redrawing the same idea more neatly. Each development move must answer a specific question: what was wrong or uncertain in C3, and how does this change fix it? By the end of this session you have a C4 folder section with at least four documented development moves.

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Development move", definition: "A single, deliberate change to the design that can be explained and justified. A development move must: identify a problem or uncertainty in the previous version, show the proposed change, and explain how the change improves the product against the specification. 'I made it bigger' is not a development move. 'I increased the shelf depth from 150 mm to 210 mm because the user's A4 textbook is 210 mm deep and the previous version would not have contained it' is a development move." },
  { term: "Iterative design", definition: "A design process where successive versions of a product are tested and refined based on feedback, testing, or analysis. Each iteration builds on the previous one. The IGCSE coursework process is explicitly iterative: C3 → C4 → C5 → C6 → C7." },
  { term: "Design rationale", definition: "The reasoning behind a design decision. A design rationale for a joint choice includes: the forces acting on the joint, the material being joined, the manufacturing process available, and why this joint type handles those conditions better than alternatives." },
  { term: "Circuit integration", definition: "The process of incorporating the electronic circuit into the structural product so that the circuit is hidden, protected, and operable. Development of circuit integration decisions includes: where the switch is positioned (user access), where the LED is positioned (visibility), how the wires are routed (hidden within the structure), and how the battery is accessed (for replacement)." },

<Section title="Four types of development move" icon="🔄" id="moves">

### Move 1: Material refinement

Look at the material stated in C3. Ask: is it the best choice for this specific product, for this specific user, given the school workshop constraints?

A material refinement move involves:
- Identifying the reason the original material is problematic (too heavy, not available, too expensive, poor machinability).
- Proposing an alternative with specific material justification (not just "better" — which property is improved?).
- Sketching the revised component in the new material with annotations.

### Move 2: Joint refinement

Look at every joint in C3. For each joint, ask: what force acts on this joint? Does the joint type resist that force effectively?

Common joint refinements:
- A butt joint was shown at a corner under bending — replace with a bridle joint or reinforce with a dowel.
- A mortise and tenon was shown in a location where quick disassembly is required — replace with a KD fitting.
- An adhesive bond was shown at a joint under tension — reinforce with a mechanical fastener.

### Move 3: Dimension change

Go back to the specification. For each dimension in C3, check it against the spec. If a dimension does not meet a spec point, this is a required change — not optional.

Dimension changes include:
- Shelf depth increased to match the user's A4 textbook depth.
- Bracket wall thickness increased to meet the load calculation (FoS must exceed 2.0).
- Circuit housing enlarged to accommodate the battery clip and switch.

### Move 4: Circuit integration

This move documents where and how the circuit components fit into the structure. It must show:
- Switch position (where the user operates it — accessible without removing objects from the shelf).
- LED position (where light is needed — aimed at the working surface).
- Wire routing (hidden within a channel, channel dimensions stated).
- Battery access (a removable panel or accessible compartment for battery replacement).

</Section>

<Section title="Drawing development moves" icon="✏️" id="drawing">

### Before-and-after format

Each development move should be presented as:
1. **Before:** a small sketch of the original C3 detail.
2. **Change label:** one sentence identifying the problem and the proposed solution.
3. **After:** a revised sketch showing the change.
4. **Justification:** 1–2 sentences linking the change to the specification.

This format is consistent with the Paper 1 part (d) format — practising it now means part (d) in the exam is familiar.

### Scale and annotation

Development sketches do not need to be full product drawings. A detail of a single joint, a cross-section through the shelf, or a plan view of the circuit placement is sufficient. Annotate each sketch: material, dimension, function, or force label as appropriate.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Four documented development moves for a desk organiser">

**Move 1 — Material:** C3 used 12 mm pine for the vertical back panel. Pine has visible grain and may split along grain lines when drilled. Changed to 12 mm MDF — no grain direction, predictable drilling without splitting, smooth laser-cut finish. Meets Spec point 4 (consistent finish visible at 300 mm).

**Move 2 — Joint:** C3 showed a butt joint at the shelf-to-back panel connection. Under a 5 kg vertical load, the butt joint is in shear at the nail points — low strength. Changed to a housed joint (dado joint): a 12 mm channel routed in the back panel receives the shelf, distributing the shear load over the full shelf thickness. Meets Spec point 1 (5 kg load without 2 mm deflection).

**Move 3 — Dimension:** C3 shelf depth was 150 mm. User's A4 textbook is 210 mm deep — the original shelf would not contain it. Increased shelf depth to 220 mm (210 mm textbook plus 10 mm clearance at front). Meets Spec point 3 (product dimensions match user's storage items).

**Move 4 — Circuit integration:** Added a circuit housing diagram. Toggle switch on the right-hand face, 30 mm from the front edge (accessible without removing items). Red LED on the top face, angled 15° toward the desk surface (illuminates working surface). Wire channel: 8 mm wide x 5 mm deep routed in the underside of the shelf. Battery compartment: a removable MDF panel on the rear face secured with two M3 screws.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="C4 development documentation" time="90 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["Your C3 sketches from T1 W10", "Your specification from T1 W8", "A3 layout paper", "Pencil and 30-60-90 set square"]}>

**Review and plan (0–15 min):**
1. Place your C3 sketches and specification side by side.
2. For each specification point: does C3 meet it? Circle any point that is not clearly met.
3. List four development moves (one from each category: material, joint, dimension, circuit integration). Write the move title and the specification point it addresses before drawing anything.

**Development sketches (15–75 min):**
4. Complete four before-and-after development move sketches. Each must have: (a) a before sketch, (b) a change label naming the problem and the fix, (c) an after sketch with relevant annotations, (d) one or two sentences of justification linking to the specification point number.

**Circuit integration drawing (75–85 min):**
5. Draw a plan view (top-down) of the product with circuit components placed: switch, LED, battery, and wire channel. Label each component and state its position relative to the nearest edge (e.g. "switch: 30 mm from right face, 20 mm from front face").

**Self-check (85–90 min):**
6. For each development move: can you identify the problem it solves and the specification point it addresses? If not, add the link before finishing.
</Activity>

<Support title="Development move blockers">

**"I do not know what to change."** Start with the specification. Find the spec point with the most specific number (the load, the dimension, the electrical voltage). Now check: does your C3 explicitly meet this number? If yes, show it. If uncertain, that is a development move — add the calculation or the measurement that proves it.

**"All my ideas look the same as C3."** Look at the joint, not the overall form. Even if the outer shape stays the same, the internal joints and the circuit integration are always development territory. The switch position and LED angle are concrete decisions that can be shown clearly.

**"I do not know where to put the circuit."** Start with the user: which hand do they use? Which direction does the product face? The switch goes where the user's hand naturally reaches. Then route wires in channels away from load-bearing areas.
</Support>
