Term 2 opens by returning your Term 1 Paper 1 mock. The first twenty minutes are diagnostic: what went well, what did not, and what to fix. After that you practise the most trainable part of the paper — part (a) function points — until you can produce four strong ones inside six minutes.

<Callout type="exam" title="Why this matters" id="why">
  <ExamLink to="paper1">**Paper 1**</ExamLink> is worth **25% of your IGCSE** and follows the same seven-part template every time. Part (a) is always the first 4–6 marks and it is entirely about reading what a product **does** — not what it looks like. Nail part (a) and you start every exam with early confidence.
</Callout>

<Section title="The Paper 1 template" icon="📝" id="template">

### Seven parts, every time

| Part | What it asks | Typical marks |
|------|--------------|---------------|
| (a) | Function points — what must the product do? | 4–6 |
| (b) | Two different sketched approaches | 8 |
| (c) | Three annotated design ideas | 12 |
| (d) | Evaluate and justify one idea | 6 |
| (e) | Construction / joining detail | 6 |
| (f) | Sustainability point | 4 |
| (g) | Modification or improvement | 4 |

**Total: 50 marks, 1 hour 15 minutes.** Roughly 1.5 minutes per mark. Part (a) at 4–6 marks = 6–9 minutes — stick to that limit so you have enough time for the mark-rich parts (c) and (e).

### What makes a strong function point?

A function point answers: *what must this product do?* It must be **specific** (naming a quantity, action, or context), **testable** (you could check whether it passes or fails), and **user-linked** (connected to what the user needs). Avoid describing form or materials — those belong in later parts.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Upgrading weak function points for a cyclist gear organiser">

| # | Weak version | Strong version |
|---|-------------|----------------|
| 1 | Must be useful | Must hold a full-face helmet securely without it rolling off |
| 2 | Must be strong | Must support a total static load of at least 4 kg without bending |
| 3 | Must have space | Must provide separate storage for at least three item types so the cyclist sees everything at a glance |
| 4 | Must have a light | Must include a switch-operated LED the cyclist can turn on when loading gear in a dim garage |

**The upgrade rule:** Read your function point and ask "could this apply to a kitchen shelf?" If yes, it is too generic — add a user or context detail.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Post-mock review + timed part (a)" time="90 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["Returned Term 1 mock script", "New context sheet from teacher", "Mark scheme extract"]}>

**Post-mock review (0–20 min):**
1. Collect your returned Term 1 M3 mock script.
2. On the script, mark ✓ one thing you did correctly and ✗ one answer where you lost marks you should not have.
3. For the ✗ answer write one sentence: "Next time I will ____."

**Timed part (a) (20–50 min):**
4. Teacher issues a new context (a structural product brief you have not seen before).
5. Set a 6-minute timer. Write four function points. Stop at 6 minutes.
6. Self-mark against the criteria: each point must be specific and testable to score.

**Review and target-setting (50–90 min):**
7. Discuss common patterns from the Term 1 mock: which errors appeared most often?
8. Write your one Paper 1 improvement target for this term and put it in your folder.
</Activity>

<Support title="Struggling with function points?">

**Can't think of four different points?** Use these four categories: (1) what does it hold or support? (2) how does the user access or operate it? (3) what safety or stability condition must it meet? (4) what does the circuit feature do for the user?

**Points feel too similar?** Each point should describe a different type of function. If two points both start "It must hold…", one of them is a repeat.

**Vague language sneaking in?** After writing each point, test it: can I check this with a ruler, a set of weights, or a stopwatch? If not, add a quantity or a condition.
</Support>
