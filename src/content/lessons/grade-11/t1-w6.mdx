Your Component 2 project requires a switch-operated light circuit integrated into the structural product. This session covers every piece of theory you need to design, draw, and build it — from circuit symbols through IPO labelling to the resistor calculation that protects the LED.

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "IPO (Input–Process–Output)", definition: "A model for describing any electronic system. Input: the component that senses or controls (the switch). Process: the component that manages the electrical flow (resistor or transistor). Output: the component that produces a visible or physical effect (LED, lamp, motor)." },
  { term: "LED (Light-Emitting Diode)", definition: "A semiconductor component that emits light when current flows through it in the forward direction. LEDs have a forward voltage (typically 2 V for red/green, 3.0–3.5 V for blue/white) and a maximum forward current (typically 20 mA)." },
  { term: "Current-limiting resistor", definition: "A resistor placed in series with an LED to restrict the current to the LED's safe operating level. Without this resistor, the LED would draw excess current and burn out. R = (Vsupply minus V_LED) divided by I_LED." },
  { term: "Switch type", definition: "The physical mechanism that opens and closes a circuit. SPST toggle (latching, stays in position), push-to-make (momentary, returns when released), tilt switch (gravity-operated, closes when tilted), reed switch (closes in a magnetic field)." },
  { term: "Series circuit", definition: "Components connected end-to-end in a single loop. The same current flows through every component. The total voltage is shared among components. If any component fails open, the whole circuit stops." },
]} />

<Section title="Circuit symbols and the IPO model" icon="⚡" id="symbols">

### The seven symbols you must be able to draw

| Component | Symbol features | Function |
|---|---|---|
| Cell (single) | Long thin line (positive), short thick line (negative) | Provides EMF (electromotive force) |
| Battery (multi-cell) | Multiple cell symbols in series | Higher voltage supply |
| Resistor | Rectangle | Limits current flow |
| LED | Diode triangle (direction of current flow) with two outward arrows | Emits light when forward current flows |
| Switch (SPST open) | Line with a gap and a pivoting lever | Breaks the circuit to stop current |
| Toggle switch | Same as above with latching symbol | Stays in the open or closed position |
| Push-to-make | Switch symbol with a dashed line showing spring return | Closes only while pressed |

Draw each symbol neatly and consistently. In the exam, a symbol that is too small or distorted will not be credited.

### Conventional current direction

Current is shown flowing from the positive terminal of the cell (+), through the circuit, and back to the negative terminal (–). Components must be connected in the correct direction — the LED has a positive (anode) and negative (cathode) terminal and only conducts when connected the right way.

### IPO for the project circuit

- **Input:** Switch (the student or user decides when the light is on — they trigger the system)
- **Process:** Resistor (limits the current to protect the LED — makes the electrical decision)
- **Output:** LED (produces light — the visible result that meets the user need)

</Section>

<Section title="Switch types" icon="🔌" id="switches">

### Choosing the right switch for your project

| Switch type | How it works | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| SPST toggle | Flips between on and off, stays in position | A desk organiser where the user wants the light on for extended periods |
| Push-to-make | Closes while pressed, springs open when released | A product where the light should activate only when the product is in use |
| Tilt switch | Contains a ball bearing — closes when tilted past an angle | Products where the light should activate automatically when the item is picked up or repositioned |
| Reed switch | Closes in the presence of a magnetic field | Products where the light activates when a door is opened (magnet on door, reed switch in frame) |

For your project, you must choose a switch type and justify it in your specification. The justification should relate to the user: "A tilt switch was chosen because the product is a portable charging stand — when lifted, the tilt switch closes and the LED confirms the product is ready."

</Section>

<Section title="The current-limiting resistor calculation" icon="🔢" id="resistor">

### Why the resistor is essential

An LED is not a resistor — it does not naturally limit its own current. If connected directly to a battery, it draws as much current as the battery can supply, far exceeding its 20 mA maximum, and burns out in seconds.

### The formula

R = (Vsupply - V_LED) / I_LED

Where:
- Vsupply = battery voltage (typically 9 V for this project)
- V_LED = LED forward voltage (2 V for red/green/yellow; 3.0 V for blue/white)
- I_LED = LED operating current in **amperes** (20 mA = 0.020 A)

Always convert mA to A before substituting. The result R is in ohms.

After calculating R, choose the nearest standard resistor value **above** the calculated value (never below — you must not exceed the LED's current rating).

Common standard resistor values (E12 series): 100, 120, 150, 180, 220, 270, 330, 390, 470, 560, 680, 820, 1000 Ω.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Full resistor calculation for a red LED on a 9 V supply">

**Given:** Supply = 9 V. Red LED forward voltage = 2 V. LED operating current = 20 mA.

**Step 1 — Write the formula:**
R = (Vsupply - V_LED) / I_LED

**Step 2 — Convert mA to A:**
20 mA = 0.020 A

**Step 3 — Substitute:**
R = (9 - 2) / 0.020 = 7 / 0.020 = 350 Ω

**Step 4 — Choose the nearest standard value above 350 Ω:**
From the E12 series: 390 Ω is the next value above 350 Ω.

**Answer:** Use a 390 Ω resistor. This keeps LED current below 20 mA (safe), with a small margin.

**Verification:** Actual current with 390 Ω: I = (9 - 2) / 390 = 7 / 390 = 0.0179 A = 17.9 mA. Below 20 mA — LED is protected.
</WorkedExample>

1. **(0–20 min) Draw the circuit.** On A4 paper, draw the complete switch-operated LED circuit using correct symbols: battery → switch → resistor → LED → back to battery. Label: + and – terminals on battery, component names, IPO labels (I, P, O), current direction arrows.

2. **(20–35 min) Resistor calculation.** Complete three calculations:
   - Red LED, 9 V supply: forward voltage 2 V, 20 mA.
   - Blue LED, 9 V supply: forward voltage 3.0 V, 20 mA.
   - Green LED, 6 V supply: forward voltage 2.1 V, 15 mA.
   Show all steps: formula, unit conversion, substitution, calculation, standard value selection.

3. **(35–65 min) Build on breadboard.** Using the circuit you drew:
   - Connect battery to breadboard power rails.
   - Place the toggle switch in the circuit.
   - Connect the calculated resistor in series.
   - Connect the LED (note anode/cathode direction).
   - Test: does the LED light when the switch is toggled?

4. **(65–80 min) Switch type comparison.** Replace the toggle switch with a push-to-make switch. Test. Then try a tilt switch if available. For each: note how the user experience changes and which switch type best suits your project brief.

5. **(80–90 min) Circuit annotation.** Add your switch choice and justification to your C1 brief. State which switch type you will use and why it suits your target user.
</Activity>

<Support title="Building and troubleshooting the breadboard circuit">

**Breadboard layout reminder:** Holes in the same horizontal row on each half of the board are connected. The two long rails (marked red + and blue –) run the full board length — use these for the battery positive and negative connections.

**LED not lighting?** Check: (1) LED polarity — the longer leg is the anode (positive); (2) switch correctly positioned in the circuit (gap in the circuit line); (3) resistor value correct; (4) battery connection secure; (5) all connections in the same numbered column or power rail.

**LED burning out?** Check your resistor value. If you used a resistor much lower than the calculated value, the LED drew too much current. Replace the LED and use the correct resistor.
</Support>
