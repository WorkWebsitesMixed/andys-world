Concrete is everywhere — in the floor you walked on to get here, in the beams holding up the school roof. But plain concrete would not survive: it needs steel. Understanding why is understanding composite design. And once you see how composites solve single-material limitations, you see that sustainability is just the same logic applied across time: design for what happens to the product after its first use.

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Composite material", definition: "A material made from two or more constituent materials with different properties that, combined, produce better properties than either constituent alone. Composites have a matrix (the binding phase) and a reinforcement (the strengthening phase)." },
  { term: "Matrix", definition: "The continuous binding phase in a composite material that holds the reinforcement in place and transfers stress between reinforcing elements. Examples: polyester resin (in GRP), epoxy resin (in CFRP), cement (in reinforced concrete)." },
  { term: "Reinforcement", definition: "The discontinuous or fibrous phase in a composite that provides strength. Examples: glass fibres (in GRP), carbon fibres (in CFRP), steel bars (in reinforced concrete). The reinforcement carries the tensile load that the matrix alone cannot bear." },
  { term: "Thermoplastic", definition: "A plastic that softens when heated and re-solidifies when cooled, repeatedly. This means it can be remoulded, recycled by melting, and joined by heat welding. Examples: PET, polypropylene, ABS, acrylic (PMMA), PVC." },
  { term: "Thermoset", definition: "A plastic that undergoes an irreversible chemical change when cured (set). Once set, it cannot be re-melted or recycled by heat. Examples: epoxy resin, polyester resin, melamine formaldehyde, Bakelite." },
  { term: "6Rs of sustainability", definition: "Six strategies for reducing the environmental impact of a product across its lifecycle: Rethink (question whether the product is needed), Reduce (use less material or energy), Reuse (design for multiple uses or users), Recycle (use recyclable materials, design for disassembly), Refuse (avoid unsustainable materials/processes), Repair (design so components can be fixed)." },

<Section title="Reinforced concrete" icon="🏛️" id="concrete">

### Why concrete needs reinforcement

Plain concrete is strong in **compression** — a compressive strength of around 30 MPa — but weak in **tension** (tensile strength less than 5 MPa, roughly 10% of its compressive strength). A loaded horizontal beam has:

- **Top face: compression** (the load pushes down, squashing the top)
- **Bottom face: tension** (the beam sags, stretching the bottom)

If the bottom face of a plain concrete beam is in tension and concrete cannot resist tension, the beam cracks and fails at the bottom.

**Solution:** Steel reinforcing bars (rebar) are embedded in the concrete where the tension occurs — typically in the lower zone of a horizontal beam. Steel has high tensile strength (approximately 400 MPa). The concrete handles the compression; the steel handles the tension. Together they outperform either material alone.

### Pre-stressed concrete

A further refinement: steel cables are tensioned (put under tension) before the concrete is poured. When the tension is released after curing, the cables try to shorten, putting the concrete beam into compression. This pre-existing compression counters the tension from loads — allowing longer spans or thinner beams.

</Section>

<Section title="Composites" icon="⚡" id="composites">

### GRP — Glass-Reinforced Plastic

| Element | Material | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Matrix | Polyester or epoxy resin | Binds the glass fibres, transfers shear between layers |
| Reinforcement | Woven glass fibre cloth or mat | Carries tensile loads; resists cracking |

**Properties:** Corrosion-resistant (useful for boat hulls and outdoor applications), relatively light (density around 1.8 g/cm³), mouldable into complex curved forms, lower tensile strength than CFRP but much cheaper.

**Used in:** Boat hulls, wind turbine blades, car body panels (classic/specialist cars), bath tubs, canoes.

### CFRP — Carbon Fibre Reinforced Plastic

| Element | Material | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Matrix | Epoxy resin | Binds fibres, transfers load, protects from impact |
| Reinforcement | Woven carbon fibre | Carries tensile loads; very high stiffness-to-weight |

**Properties:** Exceptional tensile strength (up to 3,500 MPa for the fibre), very low density (approximately 1.6 g/cm³), extremely stiff. Expensive — manufacturing is labour-intensive. Cannot be welded or repaired easily.

**Used in:** Aerospace structures, Formula 1 chassis, bicycle frames, racing cars, high-performance prosthetics.

</Section>

<Section title="Plastics" icon="♻️" id="plastics">

### Thermoplastics — key examples for IGCSE

| Name (abbreviation) | Properties | Common use |
|---|---|---|
| Polypropylene (PP) | Flexible, chemical resistant, living hinge possible | Packaging, storage boxes, chairs |
| ABS | Tough, impact resistant, good surface finish | LEGO bricks, phone cases, 3D printing |
| Acrylic (PMMA) | Clear, scratch-resistant, laser-cuts well | Signage, display cases, protective covers |
| PET | Clear, tough, recyclable (code 1) | Drinks bottles, food trays |

### Thermosets — key examples

| Name | Properties | Common use |
|---|---|---|
| Epoxy resin | Very strong adhesive, two-part, heat-resistant once cured | Structural adhesive, CFRP matrix, coatings |
| Polyester resin | Cheaper than epoxy, used with glass fibre | GRP boat hulls, garden furniture |
| Melamine formaldehyde | Hard, heat-resistant surface | Kitchen worktop laminates, crockery |

</Section>

<Section title="The 6Rs and your project" icon="🌿" id="sustainability">

Apply the 6Rs to your Component 2 project now — before you manufacture. Each one represents a design decision.

| R | Question to ask | Example for your project |
|---|---|---|
| Rethink | Does this product need to exist? Could the user's problem be solved another way? | Could a hook on the wall solve the problem instead of a whole shelf? |
| Reduce | Can I use less material without losing structural integrity? | Reduce shelf thickness from 18 mm MDF to 12 mm if the load test allows. |
| Reuse | Could the product be used by someone else, or in a different context later? | Design the product to be wall-mounted with removable hooks — adaptable. |
| Recycle | Can the materials be separated and recycled at end of life? | Use KD fittings so the product disassembles; then wood goes to wood recycling, metal fixings to metal recycling. |
| Refuse | Are any materials or processes avoidable on sustainability grounds? | Refuse solvent-based varnishes (VOC emissions) — use water-based varnish instead. |
| Repair | Can a broken part be replaced without replacing the whole product? | Design so that the shelf surface can be replaced without dismantling the whole structure. |

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Applying the 6Rs to a shelf organiser">

A student is designing a timber shelf organiser with an acrylic front panel and a switch-operated LED. The 6Rs analysis:

**Reduce:** The original design uses 18 mm MDF throughout. After load testing, 12 mm MDF is sufficient for the horizontal shelf — this reduces material use by one-third for that component.

**Reuse:** The organiser is designed with a modular system — the shelf heights can be reconfigured using KD cam fittings. When the user no longer needs a guitar-cable organiser, the same structure becomes a book shelf.

**Repair:** The LED is connected with a plug connector, not soldered in permanently. If the LED fails, it can be unplugged and replaced without dismantling the product.

This analysis becomes a paragraph in C4 (development) of the Component 2 folder.
</WorkedExample>
