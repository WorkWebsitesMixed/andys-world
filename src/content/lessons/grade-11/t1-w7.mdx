<Section title="The M1 quiz format" icon="🎯" id="quiz-format">

### What Section A looks like

Paper 4 Section A consists of approximately 10–15 short-answer questions, each worth 1–4 marks. Questions test:
- Identification (name a component, identify a structure type, label a force)
- Description (describe what an NTC thermistor does)
- Explanation (explain why concrete is reinforced)
- Calculation (find beam reaction, FoS, resistor value)

Total marks for Section A: approximately 40. Recommended time: 40–45 minutes in the real Paper 4.

### Today's M1 quiz

Same format, shorter version: 8–10 questions covering T1 W1–W6 content. 20 minutes.

**Strategy:** Read each question fully before answering. If a question has multiple parts, answer all parts. Never leave a question blank — a blank earns zero; a partial answer may earn a mark.

</Section>

<Section title="The no-generic-words rule" icon="✏️" id="generic-words">

### Why generic words earn zero

Paper 4 mark schemes use a principle called **positive marking**: marks are awarded for specific correct statements, not for generally sensible-sounding answers. "Steel is strong" matches no mark point. "Mild steel has a tensile strength of approximately 400 MPa" matches the mark point for "high tensile strength with a specific value or comparison."

### The rewrite process

For every generic word in an answer, ask:
1. **What specific property does 'strong' / 'durable' / 'good' refer to?** (tensile strength? hardness? corrosion resistance?)
2. **What is the measured value or comparison?** (400 MPa? three times higher than aluminium? lasts 20 years without treatment?)
3. **What is the consequence for the product?** (means the bracket resists bending under a 5 kg load without permanent deformation)

### The five generic words that lose the most marks

| Generic word | Specific replacement |
|---|---|
| Strong | "High tensile strength (approx. 400 MPa for mild steel)" |
| Durable | "High corrosion resistance — anodised aluminium forms a passive oxide layer" or "High hardness — resists surface scratching from daily use" |
| Good | Identify what specific property is good (hardness? cost? availability?) and name it |
| Light | "Low density (2.7 g/cm³ for aluminium — approximately one-third the density of steel)" |
| Rigid | "High Young's modulus (stiffness) — resists elastic deformation under load" |

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Rewriting four vague answers into mark-worthy ones">

**Q: Explain why mild steel is a suitable material for a structural shelf bracket.**

Vague: "Mild steel is strong and durable, which makes it good for a shelf bracket."
Rewritten: "Mild steel has a tensile strength of approximately 400 MPa, which means the bracket's arms resist bending when loaded with 5 kg without permanent deformation. It can also be powder-coated to prevent corrosion in the indoor environment where the shelf is mounted."

---

**Q: State one advantage of using MDF for a laser-cut shelf component.**

Vague: "MDF is easy to work with and gives a nice finish."
Rewritten: "MDF has no grain direction, which means it cuts cleanly on a laser cutter in any orientation without splitting along a grain line."

---

**Q: Give one reason why a tilt switch is used in this circuit.**

Vague: "The tilt switch is appropriate for this product."
Rewritten: "The tilt switch closes when the product is lifted, activating the LED automatically — without requiring the user to press a separate button."
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="M1 quiz and rewrite exercises" time="90 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["Exam paper (M1)", "Black pen for M1 (no pencil)", "Pencil for rewrite exercises"]}>

**M1 quiz (0–20 min):** Sit in exam conditions. Complete the mini-quiz. No notes, no discussion, no assistance.

**Self-mark (20–30 min):** Mark scheme distributed. Score your paper. For each question below 100%: circle the word or phrase that lost the mark.

**Rewrite exercise (30–70 min):**
1. Rewrite each of your own M1 answers that used a generic word. Target: specific property name + measured value or comparison + consequence for the product.
2. Complete the teacher-provided rewrite set: six vague Section A answers to improve. Each answer must improve from 0–1 marks to 2–3 marks through specificity alone.
3. Write your own "most likely to go generic" list: the three property words you are most likely to write without thinking. Post this on the inside cover of your folder.

**Units drill (70–85 min):**
4. Complete the units matching exercise: pair current, voltage, resistance, force, stress with their SI units (A, V, Ω, N, N/mm²). Then convert: 20 mA to A; 4.7 kΩ to Ω; 500 N/mm² to MPa; 2.7 g/cm³ to kg/m³.

**Wrap-up (85–90 min):**
5. One-sentence reflection on your M1 score: what you would revise before the next assessment.
</Activity>

<Support title="Rewrite scaffolds">

**Three-step rewrite frame:**
1. Name the specific property: "[material] has high/low [property name]..."
2. Add a value or comparison: "...approximately [value with units] / compared to [alternative]..."
3. State the consequence: "...which means [consequence for the product's function or performance]."

**Units conversion card:**
- mA to A: divide by 1,000 (20 mA = 0.020 A)
- kΩ to Ω: multiply by 1,000 (4.7 kΩ = 4,700 Ω)
- N/mm² and MPa are the same unit (1 N/mm² = 1 MPa)
- g/cm³ to kg/m³: multiply by 1,000 (2.7 g/cm³ = 2,700 kg/m³)
</Support>
