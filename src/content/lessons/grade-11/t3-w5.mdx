Section B of Paper 4 is where the gap between students who prepared and students who did not becomes visible. It requires applying knowledge to an unfamiliar design context — not just recalling facts. This session teaches the structure and planning techniques for Section B extended answers.

<KeyTerms terms={[

A Section B mark scheme bullet point that reads 'the joint must resist bending forces' means the student must say something about why bending forces are present in THIS specific design context — not just define bending." },
  { term: "Mark point planning", definition: "Before writing a multi-mark answer, write the mark points in the margin as bullet points. For a 6-mark answer, write 6 bullet points. Then expand each into a sentence. This prevents answers that repeat the same point twice and earn only one mark." },
  { term: "Sub-part dependency", definition: "In Section B multi-part questions, later sub-parts often use results or decisions from earlier sub-parts. Writing an incorrect answer to (a) can make (b) and (c) impossible to answer correctly." },

<Section title="Section B question types" icon="📄" id="types">

### Type 1: Explanation questions (2–4 marks)

"Explain why [material/join/process] is used in [specific context]."

Formula: property name → specific value or comparison → consequence for the product in this context.

For 4 marks: two full explanations (property + value + consequence), each clearly distinct.

### Type 2: Calculation questions (3–4 marks)

Structural calculations (FoS, stress, beam reactions) or electronics calculations embedded in the design context. Always show: formula, unit conversion, substitution, result, sense check.

### Type 3: Suggest-and-justify questions (2–4 marks)

"Suggest a suitable material/process/join for [specific application] and justify your choice."

Formula: name the choice → state one specific property that makes it suitable → link to the context → acknowledge one trade-off (for multi-mark answers).

### Type 4: Compare-and-evaluate questions (4–6 marks)

"Compare [two materials/processes/approaches] for use in [context]. Evaluate which is more suitable."

Formula: compare on 2–3 criteria (each a distinct mark point) → make a justified recommendation (1 mark) → acknowledge one trade-off (1 mark).

</Section>

<Section title="Planning a 6-mark extended answer" icon="✏️" id="planning">

### The margin plan technique

Before writing a 6-mark answer:
1. Write "6 points needed" in the margin.
2. List 6 bullet points in the margin — one per mark.
3. Check: are all 6 different? Are any just rephrasing the same point?
4. Write the answer, one sentence per bullet point.
5. Stop when all 6 points are covered. Do not write padding.

### What counts as a distinct mark point

A mark scheme awards marks for distinct knowledge claims. The same claim expressed in different words earns only one mark:
- "MDF is easy to machine" and "MDF can be laser-cut easily" → one mark (same property, same tool).
- "MDF has no grain direction" and "MDF can be laser-cut in any orientation without splitting" → might be one mark (both reference grain) or two (if the mark scheme separates the property from the consequence).

Always lead with the property name, then the consequence — the two are usually separate mark points.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="6-mark Section B answer: material comparison in context">

**Context:** A kitchen storage rack is being designed to hold spice jars. The designer is choosing between mild steel (powder-coated) and acrylic for the main shelving surface. Evaluate which material is more suitable. [6 marks]

**Margin plan (6 points):**
1. Mild steel: tensile strength ~400 MPa — high structural strength.
2. Mild steel: ferrous — must be powder-coated to prevent rusting in damp kitchen.
3. Acrylic: transparent — allows visual identification of jar labels from above.
4. Acrylic: brittle — risk of cracking if a heavy jar drops on it.
5. Steel: higher stiffness (Young's modulus 200 GPa vs acrylic 3 GPa) — less shelf sag.
6. Recommendation with justification.

**Written answer:**
"Mild steel has a tensile strength of approximately 400 MPa compared to acrylic at 55 MPa, giving the steel shelf greater structural capacity to resist the weight of filled spice jars without permanent deformation (1). However, mild steel is ferrous and must be powder-coated to prevent rust in the damp kitchen environment — adding a manufacturing step and cost (2). Acrylic's transparency allows the user to see jar labels through the shelf from above, improving usability in a kitchen context where visual access matters (3). Acrylic is brittle (impact strength approximately 15 kJ/m²) — a dropped heavy jar could crack the shelf, creating a hazard (4). Mild steel's Young's modulus is approximately 200 GPa versus acrylic's 3 GPa, meaning a steel shelf of the same geometry will deflect approximately 67 times less under the same load (5). For a kitchen environment where durability and structural performance outweigh transparency, mild steel (powder-coated) is the more suitable material — it meets the structural and safety requirements without the brittleness risk of acrylic (6)."
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Section B planning and extended answer practice" time="90 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["Section B question set (teacher-provided)", "Calculator for any calculations"]}>

**Technique review (0–15 min):**
1. Read the four Section B question types on this page. For each type, write one sentence describing what a full-mark answer requires (without looking at the page — recall from the last few minutes of reading).

**Planning practice (15–35 min):**
2. For three given 6-mark Section B questions: write the margin plan only. Do NOT write the full answer yet. Focus: can you identify 6 distinct mark points for each?
3. Compare your margin plans with a partner. Identify any mark points that are actually the same claim reworded.

**Full answer practice (35–75 min):**
4. Select two questions from the set. Write complete, planned answers for both. Allocate time: 15 minutes per question.
5. After both: read back each answer and count the distinct mark points. Is each sentence earning a different mark, or are some sentences padding?

**Self-mark and gap analysis (75–90 min):**
6. Mark scheme for the two questions is distributed. Compare your answer to the mark scheme. For each mark missed: is it a knowledge gap, vocabulary issue, or context-specificity failure (answered generically rather than about THIS design)?
</Activity>

<Support title="Section B extended answer help">

**Answer feels too long?** Quality over quantity. A well-targeted 6-sentence answer earns more than a 12-sentence answer where only 6 sentences contain mark-worthy content. Write 6 clear sentences, one per mark point, and stop.

**Cannot think of 6 distinct points?** Try these angles: material property, material value, consequence for this specific product, trade-off, manufacturing consideration, cost/sustainability. Each of these is typically a separate mark point.

**Generic answers losing marks?** For every sentence, ask: could I say this about ANY product? If yes, it is generic. Add the word "because" and a specific feature of THIS context.
</Support>
