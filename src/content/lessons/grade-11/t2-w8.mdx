Your project circuit uses a simple switch. Paper 4 tests a more sophisticated class of circuits: sensing circuits that respond automatically to environmental conditions. This session introduces the two most commonly tested sensors (NTC thermistor and LDR) and the voltage divider circuit that connects them to an output device.

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "NTC thermistor", definition: "Negative Temperature Coefficient thermistor. A resistor whose resistance decreases as temperature increases. At cold temperatures: high resistance. At hot temperatures: low resistance. Used to sense temperature — for example, in central heating thermostats and kettle cut-off circuits." },
  { term: "LDR (Light-Dependent Resistor)", definition: "A resistor whose resistance decreases as light intensity increases. In bright conditions: low resistance (typically 100–500 Ω). In dark conditions: high resistance (typically 1 MΩ). Used in automatic lighting circuits (streetlights, night lights) and light-activated alarms." },
  { term: "Voltage divider", definition: "A circuit of two resistors in series connected across a supply voltage. The output voltage is taken between the two resistors. Vout = Vsupply x R2 / (R1 + R2). When one of the resistors is a sensor (thermistor or LDR), the output voltage changes as the sensor resistance changes — this is the basis of sensing circuits." },
  { term: "Potential divider", definition: "Another term for voltage divider. The circuit 'divides' the supply voltage between two series resistors in proportion to their resistance values. The junction between the two resistors is the output (signal) point." },

<Section title="NTC thermistor: how it works" icon="🌡️" id="thermistor">

### Resistance vs temperature relationship

For an NTC thermistor:
- Cold (e.g. 0°C): resistance high (e.g. 10 kΩ)
- Room temperature (25°C): resistance medium (e.g. 5 kΩ)
- Hot (e.g. 100°C): resistance low (e.g. 500 Ω)

This non-linear relationship is what distinguishes a thermistor from a fixed resistor — its resistance value changes with temperature.

### In a voltage divider

If the thermistor is the top resistor (R1) and a fixed resistor is the bottom resistor (R2):

Vout = Vsupply x R2 / (R1 + R2)

- When the thermistor is cold: R1 is large → R1 + R2 is large → Vout is small (most voltage across R1).
- When the thermistor is hot: R1 is small → Vout is large (most voltage across R2).

**Application:** A high Vout when hot can switch on a transistor, which activates a fan or an alarm — this is how a temperature-controlled cooling fan works.

</Section>

<Section title="LDR: how it works" icon="💡" id="ldr">

### Resistance vs light level relationship

For an LDR:
- Dark (covered): resistance very high (e.g. 1 MΩ = 1,000 kΩ)
- Dim light: resistance medium (e.g. 50 kΩ)
- Bright light: resistance low (e.g. 500 Ω)

### In a voltage divider (dark-activated circuit)

If the LDR is the top resistor (R1) and a fixed resistor is the bottom (R2):

- In the dark: LDR resistance (R1) is very high → Vout is small → transistor OFF → LED off.
- In the light: LDR resistance is low → Vout is high → transistor ON → LED on.

This is a light-activated circuit (LED lights in bright conditions).

To make a dark-activated circuit (LED lights in dark conditions), swap the positions of the LDR and the fixed resistor — place the fixed resistor at the top and the LDR at the bottom.

</Section>

<Section title="The voltage divider formula" icon="🔢" id="formula">

### Formula

Vout = Vsupply x R2 / (R1 + R2)

Where R1 is the top resistor (between supply and the output junction), and R2 is the bottom resistor (between the output junction and 0 V).

### Worked steps

1. Identify R1 and R2 (top and bottom of the divider).
2. Identify Vsupply.
3. Substitute and calculate: Vout = Vsupply x R2 / (R1 + R2).
4. State units: volts.

**Sanity check:** Vout must be between 0 V and Vsupply. If your answer is outside this range, recheck the formula substitution.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Two voltage divider calculations">

**Scenario 1 — LDR in bright conditions:**
Supply = 9 V. LDR resistance in bright conditions = 500 Ω (R1, top). Fixed resistor = 4,500 Ω (R2, bottom).

Vout = 9 x 4,500 / (500 + 4,500) = 9 x 4,500 / 5,000 = 9 x 0.9 = 8.1 V

At 8.1 V, the transistor base voltage is well above 0.6 V — transistor ON, LED lights.

**Scenario 2 — LDR in dark conditions:**
Supply = 9 V. LDR resistance in dark = 100,000 Ω (100 kΩ) (R1, top). Fixed resistor = 4,500 Ω (R2, bottom).

Vout = 9 x 4,500 / (100,000 + 4,500) = 9 x 4,500 / 104,500 = 9 x 0.0431 = 0.39 V

At 0.39 V, the transistor base voltage is below 0.6 V — transistor OFF, LED does not light.

**Conclusion:** This LDR circuit lights the LED in bright conditions and switches it off in dark conditions. To reverse (dark-activated), swap R1 and R2.
</WorkedExample>

<Support title="Voltage divider and sensing circuit help">

**R1 and R2 confusion:** R1 is always the top resistor — between the supply and the output junction. R2 is the bottom — between the junction and 0 V. When the top resistor (R1) increases, Vout decreases (more voltage dropped across R1). When R2 increases, Vout increases (more voltage available at the junction).

**Calculation gives a voltage above Vsupply:** R1 and R2 have been swapped in the formula. Recheck which is at the top and which is at the bottom of the divider.

**Transistor always ON or always OFF regardless of sensor state:** The fixed resistor value may be too extreme. If the fixed resistor is very large compared to the sensor's maximum resistance, Vout will always be high (transistor always ON). If it is very small, Vout will always be low (transistor always OFF). The fixed resistor should be chosen so that the transition point (Vout = 0.6 V) falls within the sensor's normal operating range.
</Support>
