Two tools, one session. The first half you prepare a 3D model for printing — a component your laser cutter cannot make easily (curves, brackets, custom fittings). The second half you produce an orthographic working drawing of your main product: the technical communication document that tells a manufacturer exactly what to make.

<Callout type="exam" title="Why this matters" id="why">
  <ExamLink to="project">Component 2</ExamLink> criterion **C5 (working drawings and plans, 15 marks)** requires evidence of both digital fabrication planning (CAD) and technical drawing (orthographic projection). Today you produce direct evidence for both halves of C5. A working drawing that is incomplete or missing critical dimensions is one of the most common reasons strong projects score poorly on C5.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "STL file", definition: "Standard Tessellation Language — the file format used by virtually all 3D printers. An STL file represents the surface of a 3D shape as a mesh of triangles. Exported from any 3D CAD tool (Fusion 360, Tinkercad, OpenSCAD, FreeCAD)." },
  { term: "Third-angle projection", definition: "The convention used in the UK, US, and most international standards for engineering drawing. The front view is in the centre; the right view is to its right; the top view is above. A small triangular symbol on the drawing identifies it as third-angle." },
  { term: "Orthographic view", definition: "A two-dimensional view of a 3D object seen from exactly one direction, with no perspective distortion. Lines that are parallel on the object appear parallel on the drawing." },
  { term: "Hidden detail line", definition: "A dashed line on a working drawing used to show a feature that is behind or inside the object from the current view direction. Hidden detail lines allow a drawing to communicate features that would otherwise require a section view." },
]} />

<Section title="3D printing a component" icon="🖨️" id="print">

### When to 3D print vs laser cut

| Feature | 3D print | Laser cut |
|---------|---------|----------|
| Complex 3D curves | Yes | No |
| Internal channels | Yes | No |
| Flat sheet parts | Slow/wasteful | Yes |
| Speed (small part) | 20–120 min per part | 1–5 min per part |
| Best for | Brackets, fittings, custom knobs | Structural panels, shelves, frames |

Choose one component in your product that has a feature the laser cutter cannot produce: a curved bracket, a multi-axis joint, a custom switch bezel, or a cable channel.

### Preparing the STL

In Fusion 360 or Tinkercad:
1. Model the component in 3D. Work in mm.
2. Export as STL (File → Export → STL).
3. Open in slicing software (Cura or PrusaSlicer). Set layer height 0.2 mm, 20% infill, generate supports if needed.
4. Save the gcode to USB. Screenshot the slicer preview for your folder.

</Section>

<Section title="Working drawing — orthographic projection" icon="📐" id="drawing">

### What a working drawing must include

A working drawing for C5 needs:
- **At least three views:** front, side, and top (plan)
- **Projection lines** connecting views (drawn lightly)
- **Full dimensions** on every view: overall width, height, depth, and any critical detail dimensions
- **Title block:** product name, your name, date, scale, material
- **Third-angle projection symbol** (small triangle/circle symbol)
- **Hidden detail lines** (dashed) where relevant

### Drawing procedure

Draw front view first: this is the view that shows the most information — usually the face the user sees. Then project up (top view) and sideways (side view) using 45° projection lines. Dimension from outer boundary inward. Do not dimension the same feature on two views.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Working drawing checklist for a gear organiser">

**Front view:** Width 150 mm, height 280 mm. Shows peg positions with dimensions (first peg at 60 mm height, 80 mm spacing). Circuit panel visible on lower-left face.

**Side view:** Depth 200 mm. Shows base extension beyond panel face (20 mm). Hidden detail line shows internal cable channel.

**Top view (plan):** 150 × 200 mm footprint. Shows peg hole positions in plan.

**Checklist:** Title block complete | Third-angle symbol present | All three views drawn | Overall dimensions on all views | Peg detail dimensions | Hidden detail line for cable channel | Scale 1:2 noted
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="STL prep + working drawing" time="90 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["CAD application (Fusion 360 / Tinkercad)", "Slicer software (Cura / PrusaSlicer)", "A3 cartridge paper or CAD working drawing template", "Pencil, technical pen, ruler, set square, compass"]}>

**3D print setup (0–35 min):**
1. Choose your print component and justify the choice in one sentence (why laser cut would not work for this part).
2. Open in 3D CAD. Verify dimensions match your working design.
3. Export STL, open in slicer, screenshot the preview. Print to queue if machine is available.

**Working drawing (35–90 min):**
4. Set up your A3 sheet. Draw a title block at the bottom right (product name, scale, date, your name).
5. Draw the front view at scale 1:2 (or 1:1 if the product fits). Lightly block out proportions before adding lines.
6. Project up to the top view and sideways to the side view using 45-degree guide lines.
7. Dimension all views. Add the third-angle projection symbol.
8. Check the working-drawing checklist (see Worked Example above).
</Activity>

<Support title="Orthographic drawing going wrong?">

**Views not lining up?** The critical rule: the front and top views share the same horizontal extent (same width). The front and side views share the same vertical extent (same height). Use light projection lines to enforce this before you add any detail.

**Not enough room on the sheet?** If the product is large, use 1:5 scale. Note the scale in the title block. At 1:5 scale, every 5 mm of real dimension becomes 1 mm on paper.

**Can't find the third-angle symbol?** Draw a truncated cone (wider end left, narrower end right) with a circle to its right. This is the international symbol for third-angle projection. It must appear in or near the title block.
</Support>

<Section title="Homework" icon="🏠" id="homework">

**Home task:** Complete and ink-in your working drawing if not finished in class. Scan or photograph it and add it to your folder. Also: check the 3D print queue — if your part is printed, photograph it and annotate the photograph with its function and dimensions.

<Extension title="Section view">
Add a section view to your working drawing. Choose a cutting plane that passes through an internal feature (cable channel, peg socket, circuit cavity). Draw the cross-section as it would appear if the product were cut through that plane. Hatch the cut surfaces at 45°. Section views reveal internal geometry that is otherwise hidden and can replace several hidden-detail lines.
</Extension>

</Section>
