All parts need to be cut and finished before assembly can begin. This session completes any parts not finished in Week 2: hand-tool cuts, drilled holes, filed edges, and the 3D-printed bracket (collected from last session's print queue). By the end of this session every component on your cutting list exists as a real, measurable object.

<Callout type="exam" title="Why this matters" id="why">
  <ExamLink to="project">Component 2</ExamLink> criterion **C6 (making, 30 marks)** rewards accuracy and quality in manufacture. Parts that match the cutting list dimensions, have clean edges, and fit correctly in a dry assembly earn more marks than parts that are approximately right. The making log entry from this session is evidence that you worked precisely.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Dry assembly", definition: "Fitting all parts together without glue or permanent fixings, to check that joints align, dimensions match, and the product is the correct size. Dry assembly before gluing is the cheapest way to find a problem." },
  { term: "Marking out", definition: "Transferring dimensions from the working drawing onto the raw material using a pencil, marking gauge, or scriber, before cutting. Marking out accurately is what makes a cut accurate — you cannot cut more precisely than you mark." },
  { term: "Tolerance check", definition: "Measuring each finished part with a rule or calipers and comparing the result to the cutting list dimension. Any part outside the tolerance (typically ±1 mm for structural parts, ±0.2 mm for laser-cut joints) needs to be re-cut before assembly." },
  { term: "Chamfer / deburr", definition: "Removing the sharp edge left by sawing, laser cutting, or drilling. A 45° chamfer (cut with a block plane or file) or deburring with sandpaper makes joints easier to assemble and removes potential injury points." },

<Section title="Completing all parts" icon="🔨" id="parts">

### Parts manufacturing sequence

1. Collect any 3D-printed parts from the print queue. Measure: do they match the design dimensions? Remove support material with pliers.
2. Mark out any hand-cut parts (parts not in the laser file). Use the working drawing, not the cutting list, as the source of dimensions — the cutting list is a summary; the drawing has detail.
3. Cut hand-cut parts: use the appropriate tool for the material (tenon saw for timber, coping saw for curves, hand file for fine fitting).
4. Drill any holes (peg holes, cable exits, circuit mounting). Use the correct drill bit size — undersize is better than oversize for a first pass.
5. Chamfer or deburr all cut edges.

### Dry assembly check

Before this session ends, dry-assemble your main structure (no glue). Check:
- Do all joints fit? Not too tight (parts split under force) and not too loose (joints rattle)?
- Is the overall height, width, and depth within 3 mm of the working drawing?
- Are the peg holes in the right positions relative to each other?
- Does the circuit fit in its allocated space?

If anything does not fit: note it in the making log and adjust before next session.

</Section>

**Collect and check 3D print (0–10 min):**
1. Collect your 3D-printed bracket. Remove supports. Measure two key dimensions. Record in making log.

**Mark out and cut hand-tool parts (10–60 min):**
2. Mark out all remaining parts using the working drawing. Check: have you identified your datum edge?
3. Cut each part. Measure after cutting. Compare to cutting list.
4. Chamfer all laser-cut edges (light sanding, 120 grit). Deburr drilled holes.

**Dry assembly (60–80 min):**
5. Dry-assemble the main structure without glue. Check overall dimensions.
6. Note any joint that is too tight or too loose. Note any part that is out of position.

**Making log (80–90 min):**
7. Photograph the dry assembly. Write the log entry: parts completed, quality checks, problems found, changes planned for W4.

<Support title="Joint too tight or too loose?">

**Too tight — parts won't slide together?** Laser kerf compensation was insufficient. Use a small file or sandpaper wrapped around a dowel to widen the slot by 0.1–0.2 mm at a time. Do not force the joint — birch ply splits along the grain under axial force.

**Too loose — joint rattles?** Kerf compensation was too generous, or the slot was cut oversize. Options: (1) re-cut the mating part with a corrected slot width; (2) add a shim (thin strip of same material glued into the slot to narrow it); (3) if only slightly loose, PVA glue will fill the gap on final assembly.

**3D print dimensions wrong by more than 2 mm?** PLA shrinks 0.3–0.5% as it cools. A 60 mm dimension becomes approximately 59.7 mm — 0.3 mm undersize. This is normal and usually unimportant. If critical mounting holes are misaligned by more than 1 mm, re-print the part with dimensions scaled up by 0.5%.
</Support>

<Extension title="Joint strength test">
Before gluing: clamp one joint from your dry assembly and apply a lateral load (push sideways on the top of one panel). Observe how much the joint moves before and after adding a dry brace. Calculate (roughly) how much stiffer the structure becomes with the brace in place. This mirrors the kind of structural analysis you studied in Paper 4.
