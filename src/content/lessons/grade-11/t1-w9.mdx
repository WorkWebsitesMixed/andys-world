Every project needs its **switch-operated circuit** — and this is the week you build
one. Keep it simple: a battery, a switch, a resistor and an indicator. By the end
you'll have a working circuit and the artefacts your folder needs.

<Callout type="exam" title="Why this matters" id="why">
  Circuit symbols, switches and input–process–output are <ExamLink to="paper4">**Paper 4 Section A**</ExamLink> topics,
  and a working circuit is a <ExamLink to="project">required feature</ExamLink> of your
  product. Remember the bound: a **simple on/off, battery-powered** circuit only — no
  motors, sensors, PICs, gates or PCBs.
</Callout>

<Section title="The lesson" icon="📋" id="lesson">

### 1. Symbols and the IPO model

Draw circuits with the **standard symbols** (cell, switch, resistor, LED) wired as a
loop. Think in **input → process → output**: the **switch** is the input, the
**circuit** is the process, the **LED (or buzzer)** is the output.

### 2. Choosing your switch

Your circuit must be **switch-operated**, and the **switch type is your choice**:

- **Toggle** — latches on and stays on (good for a desk light).
- **Push-to-make (PTM)** — on only while pressed (good for a momentary signal).
- **Reed** — operated by a magnet, with no contact (good for "lid closed" sensing).

Pick the one that matches **how your product is used**, and justify it.

### 3. The current-limiting resistor

An LED needs a resistor in series or it burns out. Using **I = V/R** (rearranged to
**R = V/I**) with the supply voltage and the LED's voltage drop, you can size it.

<Callout type="warn" title="At the bench">
  Work patiently, follow the technician's instructions, and **ask before acting** if
  you're unsure. No soldering today — the kit uses solderless terminals.
</Callout>

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Worked example — sizing the resistor">
1. **Supply:** a 6 V battery. **LED:** drops about 2 V and needs about 20 mA (0.02 A).
2. **Voltage across the resistor:** 6 V − 2 V = 4 V.
3. **Apply R = V / I:** R = 4 ÷ 0.02.
4. **Resistor:** **R = 200 Ω** — choose the nearest standard value.
</WorkedExample>
