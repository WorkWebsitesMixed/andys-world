Sketching 1 (Week 2) gave you line confidence and crating. Today goes further: isometric projection gives your sketches a consistent, professional 3D quality, and annotation transforms a picture into an engineering communication. By the end of the session, every sketch you produce for C3 will carry four types of annotation.

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Isometric projection", definition: "A method of drawing 3D objects on a 2D surface where all three axes (x, y, z) are drawn at 120° to each other. The two horizontal-ish axes go at 30° above horizontal to left and right; the vertical axis goes straight up. All dimensions are drawn at true scale along these three axes." },
  { term: "30-degree guidelines", definition: "The construction lines drawn at 30° above horizontal (left and right) that form the basis of isometric drawing. A 30-60-90 set square or isometric dot paper provides these angles. All receding horizontal edges follow one of the two 30° directions." },
  { term: "Annotation", definition: "Written notes added to a sketch to communicate information that the drawing alone cannot show. Effective annotations are connected to the sketch by a leader line and state material + reason, join method + reason, function statement, or a key dimension." },
  { term: "Leader line", definition: "A thin straight line with an arrow at one end connecting an annotation to the specific part of the sketch it refers to. Leader lines do not cross other leader lines if avoidable. The arrow points to the part being described." },

<Section title="Isometric drawing in practice" icon="📐" id="isometric">

### The three axes

In isometric drawing:
- **Vertical axis:** goes straight up and down — draw vertical lines as actual vertical lines.
- **Left-receding axis:** goes at 30° above horizontal to the left.
- **Right-receding axis:** goes at 30° above horizontal to the right.

All measurements on the drawing are true scale along these three axes. A 200 mm wide shelf is drawn as 200 mm (or scaled, e.g. 1:5 = 40 mm) along the right-receding axis.

### Step-by-step isometric crating

1. **Choose a corner** to start from — usually the front-bottom corner of the object.
2. **Draw the three axes** from that corner: one up, one at 30° left, one at 30° right.
3. **Mark the dimensions** along each axis: width (right axis), depth (left axis), height (vertical axis).
4. **Complete the box** by drawing parallel lines to close the three visible faces.
5. **Cut the form** out of the box — remove lines that fall inside the object's solid area.
6. **Apply thick/thin line** — visible edges thick, construction/receding edges thin.

### Circles in isometric

A circle on an isometric face becomes an ellipse. On the right-facing or left-facing faces: draw an isometric square (a rhombus using the axis directions), then sketch the ellipse tangent to the midpoints of all four sides of the rhombus.

This is the same principle as Week 2 ellipses, but now governed by the 30° axes rather than freehand estimation.

<Section title="Annotation — four types that earn marks" icon="✏️" id="annotation">

### What IGCSE mark schemes reward in sketches

Paper 1 part (c) and Component 2 C3 both award marks for annotation. The four types that consistently earn marks:

| Annotation type | What to write | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Material + reason | Name the material and link it to a function or property | "Pine — lightweight and easy to machine in school workshop" |
| Join method + reason | Name the joint and explain why it is used here | "Mortise and tenon joint — handles the bending force at the bracket shoulder" |
| Function statement | Explain what this specific part does | "Horizontal shelf — carries the static load of stored items" |
| Dimension | A key measurement that shows the design is thought through | "150 mm deep — based on A4 textbook depth measurement" |

### How to lay out annotations

- Use a **leader line** from the annotation to the part it describes — a thin line with a clear arrowhead touching the part.
- Write annotations **outside** the sketch, not inside it.
- Use **horizontal text** regardless of where the leader line goes.
- Fit four annotations on every sketch — one of each type above.

### What NOT to do

- Do not annotate the obvious: "this is the shelf" adds nothing.
- Do not write general comments about the whole product as if annotating a specific part.
- Do not use generic words (Week 7 rule): "strong material" becomes "pine — Young's modulus approximately 10 GPa, sufficient to resist the shelf bending load."

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Annotating a shelf bracket in isometric">

**The sketch:** An isometric view of an L-shaped shelf bracket, 150 mm tall vertical arm, 200 mm long horizontal arm, 20 mm thick throughout, cut from 18 mm MDF.

**Four annotations:**

1. Material + reason (leader line to the horizontal arm): "18 mm MDF — no grain direction, cuts cleanly on laser cutter in any orientation."

2. Join method + reason (leader line to the corner joint between arms): "Bridle joint reinforced with PVA — mechanical interlock handles bending moment at the shoulder; PVA adds shear resistance after curing."

3. Function (leader line to the horizontal arm): "Shelf surface — carries static load of stored items; designed for 5 kg minimum."

4. Dimension (leader line with dimension line to the horizontal arm length): "200 mm — based on depth of user's A4 textbook (210 mm) with 10 mm front clearance."
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Isometric crating and annotation practice" time="90 min" grouping="Individual (drawing colleague demonstrates first 30 min)" materials={["A3 isometric dot paper or 30-60-90 set square", "HB pencil for construction, 2B for final lines", "Ruler (for leader lines only — not for crating)"]}>

**Drawing colleague demo (0–30 min):** Watch the demonstration of the full isometric workflow: axes first, bounding box, form cutout, line weighting, annotation layout.

**Exercise 1 (30–55 min) — Crate given objects:** Using isometric dot paper:
1. Draw a simple rectangular box (e.g. 80 mm x 50 mm x 40 mm) in isometric. Apply thick/thin line.
2. Draw the same box with a circular hole on the front face — draw the ellipse correctly using the rhombus method.
3. Draw an L-shaped bracket (dimensions given on the exercise sheet).

**Exercise 2 (55–80 min) — Annotate your own product:** Draw your Component 2 project concept in isometric. It does not need to be fully detailed — the main form and one joining area is enough. Add four annotations: material + reason, join + reason, function statement, dimension. Use leader lines correctly.

**Self-review (80–90 min):** Check your sketch against the four-annotation checklist. Circle any generic words in your annotations and rewrite them using specific property names (Week 7 rule).
</Activity>

<Support title="Isometric and annotation help">

**Axes going wrong?** Use isometric dot paper — the dots are pre-arranged in the correct 30° grid. All lines follow the dot connections. Freehand isometric without the paper requires careful attention to consistent 30° angles.

**Ellipses not looking right?** Draw the isometric square (rhombus) for the face first. Find the four midpoints of the rhombus sides. Sketch the ellipse touching all four midpoints — not crossing them, tangent to them.

**Cannot think of a fourth annotation?** The dimension annotation is often the easiest to add and the most often forgotten. Pick any significant dimension (shelf depth, bracket height, hole diameter), draw a dimension line with arrowheads, and state the value in mm.
</Support>
