Working drawings are the bridge between design and manufacture. This session finalises your C5 evidence: working drawings complete, dimensions verified, cutting list produced, and a simple production plan written. These documents tell the makerspace what to make and in what order. Without them, manufacture in Term 3 becomes guesswork.

<Callout type="exam" title="Why this matters" id="why">
  <ExamLink to="project">Component 2</ExamLink> criterion **C5 (working drawings and plans, 15 marks)** is assessed entirely on your folder evidence — not on the quality of the final product. A well-dimensioned working drawing set, a cutting list, and a realistic production plan can earn full C5 marks even if the product is not yet built.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Cutting list", definition: "A table that lists every component you need to manufacture: name, material, quantity, and finished dimensions (length × width × thickness). The cutting list is the shopping list for the makerspace — it tells the technician what to prepare." },
  { term: "Production plan", definition: "A sequenced list of manufacturing steps with estimated time for each. A production plan helps you manage your makerspace sessions efficiently and identifies bottlenecks before they happen. A numbered list with times is sufficient — it does not need to be a Gantt chart." },
  { term: "Tolerance", definition: "The acceptable range of variation in a dimension. 'Length 150 mm ±1 mm' means any part between 149 mm and 151 mm is acceptable. Tolerances appear on working drawings when precision matters for fit or function." },
  { term: "Assembly drawing", definition: "A drawing that shows how components fit together, usually with part numbers pointing to each component and a parts list alongside. An assembly drawing communicates assembly order and the relationship between parts." },
]} />

<Section title="Completing the working drawing set" icon="📐" id="drawings">

### Working drawing completion checklist

Go through each drawing you produced in Week 8 and verify:

- Title block complete (name, date, scale, material, drawing number)
- Third-angle projection symbol present
- At least three orthographic views (front, side, top)
- Overall dimensions on every view
- Detail dimensions for all critical features (peg spacing, slot dimensions, circuit panel position)
- Hidden detail lines (dashed) for internal features
- Tolerances noted where needed (e.g. slot width ±0.1 mm for laser-cut joints)
- Scale consistent across all views

### Producing the cutting list

List every component you need to cut or make:

| Part name | Material | Qty | L (mm) | W (mm) | T (mm) |
|-----------|---------|-----|--------|--------|--------|
| Side panel | 6 mm birch ply | 2 | 280 | 80 | 6 |
| Base panel | 6 mm birch ply | 1 | 150 | 200 | 6 |
| Top rail | 6 mm birch ply | 1 | 150 | 30 | 6 |
| Switch bracket | PLA (3D print) | 1 | 60 | 40 | 10 |

### Writing the production plan

Order the manufacturing steps and estimate times:
1. Laser cut flat ply panels — 30 min
2. Sand all panels — 20 min
3. 3D print switch bracket — 90 min running time, set during other work
4. Assemble frame with halving joints — 40 min
5. Wire and fit circuit — 60 min
6. Apply finish oil coat 1 — 15 min, 12 h dry time
7. Apply finish oil coat 2 — 15 min, 12 h dry time

<Activity title="Working drawing audit + C5 close" time="90 min" grouping="Individual (teacher circulates for quality check)" materials={["All working drawings from Week 8", "Cutting list template", "Production plan template", "Ruler, pencil, technical pen"]}>

**Working drawing audit (0–30 min):**
1. Work through the checklist above for every drawing in your set. Mark each item as complete or missing.
2. Fix any missing items: add missing dimensions, correct title block fields, add the third-angle symbol.

**Cutting list (30–55 min):**
3. List every component: name, material, quantity, all three dimensions.
4. Check: do these dimensions match your working drawings? Any discrepancy here will cause errors in manufacture.

**Production plan (55–75 min):**
5. Write a numbered list of manufacturing steps in order. Estimate time for each step.
6. Identify any steps that depend on others (e.g. assembly cannot happen before all panels are cut).

**Folder order and submission (75–90 min):**
7. Order your C5 evidence: working drawings → cutting list → production plan.
8. File the complete C5 section.
</Activity>

<Support title="Cutting list doesn't match drawings?">

**A dimension appears in the cutting list but not in the drawing?** The drawing is the authoritative source. If the cutting list has 280 mm for a side panel but the drawing says 270 mm, measure what you intended and correct both.

**Some parts not yet drawn?** If you have a component (circuit mounting bracket, base foot) that is not in any working drawing, add a quick dimensioned sketch. It does not need to be full orthographic — a dimensioned isometric sketch is acceptable as supplementary evidence.

**Production plan feels impossible to write?** Think in blocks: cutting session → assembly session → circuit wiring session → finishing session. Four blocks, each with 2–4 steps, is a credible production plan. You will refine it in Term 3 when you see how manufacture actually goes.
</Support>

<Section title="Homework" icon="🏠" id="homework">

**Home task:** Review the entire C5 section of your folder. Write a paragraph: "The drawings and plan I have produced will enable me to manufacture my product in Term 3 because…" This is the kind of evaluative writing you will need in the C7 evaluation criterion — practise it now.

<Extension title="Gantt chart">
Convert your production plan into a Gantt chart. Each row is a manufacturing step; each column is a session. Shade the sessions in which each step will occur. Identify any parallel activities (finish coat drying while you wire the circuit) that save time. A Gantt chart is optional for C5 but demonstrates strong planning rigour.
</Extension>
