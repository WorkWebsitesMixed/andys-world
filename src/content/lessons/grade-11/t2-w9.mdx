<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "6Rs framework", definition: "Reduce, Reuse, Recycle, Refuse, Rethink, Repair. A sustainability assessment tool applied to product design decisions. For IGCSE, each R is assessed against the product's design, material choice, manufacture, use, and end-of-life. Applied to Section A: 'Explain one way the product could be made more sustainable' = choose one R and give a product-specific example." },
  { term: "Reduce", definition: "Minimise material use, energy consumption, or waste in manufacture and use. Example: using CNC nesting to minimise material waste; designing for thinner walls that still meet the structural specification." },
  { term: "Reuse", definition: "Design a product so it can be used again for another purpose, or its components can be reassembled into a different product. Example: using KD fittings so the shelf can be disassembled and the MDF panels reused for a different project." },
  { term: "Recycle", definition: "Design the product so that at end-of-life, the materials can be recovered and processed into new materials. Example: single-material construction (all MDF, no metal inserts) makes sorting for recycling simple. Avoid mixing materials bonded with epoxy — they cannot be separated for recycling." },
  { term: "Refuse", definition: "Choose not to use a harmful material or process, even if it would make manufacture easier. Example: refuse to use solvent-based paint (VOC emissions) in favour of water-based acrylic; refuse to use tropical hardwoods without FSC certification." },
  { term: "Rethink", definition: "Fundamentally redesign the product or the system around it so that the sustainability problem disappears. Example: instead of designing a desktop shelf that requires manufactured timber, rethink the problem as a digital filing system that requires no physical product at all." },
  { term: "Repair", definition: "Design the product so that broken components can be individually replaced without discarding the whole product. Example: using screws rather than glued joints so that a warped shelf panel can be removed and replaced; using a removable LED holder so the LED can be replaced when it fails." },
]} />

<Section title="6Rs applied to the Component 2 project" icon="♻️" id="6rs">

### Application table

For each R, think about your own product. Write one product-specific example for each:

| R | Question to ask | Example answer for a desk shelf |
|---|---|---|
| Reduce | Where does material get wasted in manufacture? | CNC nest all components on one sheet — reduces offcuts from 35% to 8% |
| Reuse | Can the product serve another purpose when no longer needed? | KD fittings allow disassembly — panels reused as storage trays |
| Recycle | Can the materials be separated and recycled at end-of-life? | Single-material MDF construction — no mixed materials that prevent separation |
| Refuse | Was any material or process avoided for environmental reasons? | Water-based acrylic chosen instead of solvent-based enamel — no VOCs |
| Rethink | Could the design be fundamentally different to reduce impact? | Modular system: one set of brackets, multiple shelf depths — less total material for a range of users |
| Repair | Can components be individually replaced? | Screws (not glue) on circuit housing panel — LED and battery replaceable |

### In the exam

Paper 4 Section A sustainability questions typically ask:
- "Identify one of the 6Rs applied to this product" (1 mark): name the R.
- "Explain how this design feature makes the product more sustainable" (2–3 marks): name the R, describe the feature, explain the sustainability benefit.
- "Suggest one improvement to make the product more sustainable" (2 marks): propose a change + explain the sustainability benefit.

</Section>

<Section title="Section A past paper: strategy" icon="📄" id="strategy">

### The 40-mark Section A

Paper 4 Section A contains approximately 10–15 questions. Today's past paper run is 40 minutes for 40 marks — 1 minute per mark average.

### Triage strategy

1. **Read all questions first (3 minutes).** Mark: quick (definition, identification), medium (explanation, 2–3 marks), slow (calculation, 4 marks).
2. **Answer quick questions first.** These secure easy marks and build momentum.
3. **Calculations: show all working.** A wrong answer with correct method earns partial marks.
4. **Never leave a question blank.** A partial answer can earn 1 mark; a blank earns zero.
5. **Check units.** For every calculation answer: does the unit make sense? Force in N, stress in N/mm², current in A, voltage in V, resistance in Ω.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Three Section A question responses — 6Rs focus">

**Q1: The shelf is constructed from MDF and uses KD cam fittings. Explain how this design feature makes the product more sustainable. [2 marks]**

Answer: "The KD cam fittings allow the product to be disassembled at end-of-life (Reuse). The MDF panels can be removed and reassembled into a different product — such as a tray — extending the material's useful life and avoiding disposal to landfill."

**Q2: Give one way the designer could Refuse an unsustainable material choice. [2 marks]**

Answer: "The designer could refuse to use solvent-based enamel paint, which releases volatile organic compounds (VOCs) harmful to workshop air quality and the environment. Substituting water-based acrylic paint achieves the same finish with no VOC emissions."

**Q3: Explain one way the product could be Rethought to reduce its environmental impact. [2 marks]**

Answer: "Rather than a single fixed shelf, the product could be rethought as a modular clip-on bracket system. One set of standardised brackets accepts multiple shelf widths — reducing the total material needed to meet the same range of user needs."
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="6Rs product analysis and Section A past paper" time="90 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["Your finished Component 2 product (or photographs)", "Section A past paper (teacher-provided)", "Calculator", "Black pen"]}>

**6Rs analysis (0–20 min):**
1. Complete the 6Rs application table for your Component 2 product. One product-specific example per R. Use the format: R name → product feature → sustainability benefit.
2. Identify the ONE R that you believe is most strongly demonstrated by your product. Write a 50-word examination-style answer explaining this R applied to your product.

**Section A past paper (20–60 min):**
3. Sit the past paper in timed conditions. 40 minutes for the full Section A.
4. Triage: quick questions first, then medium, then calculations.
5. Show all working for all calculations. Never leave a question blank.

**Self-mark and analysis (60–80 min):**
6. Mark scheme distributed. Score the paper. Calculate your percentage.
7. For each question below 80%: identify whether the mark was lost to (a) knowledge gap, (b) generic vocabulary, or (c) missing unit or working step.
8. Write a two-sentence "topic I need to review" note for each category with missed marks.

**Sustainability extension (80–90 min):**
9. Write one Paper 1 part (e) style answer applying the 6Rs to your product (two paragraphs, two different Rs, one design change proposed for each). This is exam preparation for T3.
</Activity>

<Support title="6Rs and past paper help">

**6Rs answers feel repetitive?** Each R addresses a different lifecycle stage. Reduce = manufacture. Reuse = use phase. Recycle = end-of-life. Repair = maintenance. Refuse = material selection. Rethink = fundamental design. If you apply each to a different stage, answers will be distinct.

**Past paper calculations wrong?** Check: units converted (mA to A, kN to N, MPa to N/mm²)? Formula correct? Substitution correct? Working shown? The most common single cause of lost marks in Paper 4 calculations is not converting units before substituting.

**Section A time running out?** If fewer than 5 minutes remain, prioritise any unanswered questions over refining answers already given. A first attempt at an unanswered question earns more marks than perfect polish on a question already complete.
</Support>
