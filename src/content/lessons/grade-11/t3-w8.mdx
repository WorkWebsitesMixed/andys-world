The product is as good as you can make it. Today you complete any final touches from the snagging list, then photograph the product professionally for your folder. The M2 graded checkpoint (30% of term) happens at the end of this session — the product and making log are submitted together.

<Callout type="exam" title="Why this matters" id="why">
  <ExamLink to="project">Component 2</ExamLink> criterion **C6 (making, 30 marks)** is assessed entirely on what the examiner can see in your folder — and that means your photographs. A well-made product photographed poorly loses C6 marks. A well-photographed product with clear, well-lit images that show quality of finish, accurate joints, and integrated electronics communicates your making quality to the examiner.
</Callout>

<Section title="Product photography guide" icon="📸" id="photography">

### Setting up the shot

1. **Background:** plain white card or fabric, or a surface that does not compete with the product.
2. **Lighting:** natural light from one side (near a window) is ideal. Avoid overhead fluorescent lighting — it creates flat, washed-out images. A second light source from the other side fills shadows.
3. **Camera position:** eye-level with the product or slightly above. Avoid photographing from below (distorts proportions) or at a steep angle (hides key features).
4. **Focus:** tap the product in the phone camera app to set focus on the product, not the background.
5. **Distance:** close enough to fill the frame, far enough to show the whole product in context.

### Required shots for the folder

| Shot | Purpose |
|------|---------|
| Front view, full product | Shows overall form and proportions |
| Side view, full product | Shows depth and profile |
| Detail: a joint or edge | Shows quality of manufacture |
| Detail: the switch or circuit feature | Shows electronics integration |
| In-use: product loaded (gear on pegs), LED lit | Shows function fulfilled |

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Photography session for a cyclist gear organiser">

**Setup:** Product placed on a white foam board on a windowsill. Natural light from the left (window), white card reflector on the right to fill shadows.

**Shot 1 — Front view:** Camera at 900 mm height (eye level of a standing user), 1.2 m from the product. Focus set on the front face. Full product visible, straight.

**Shot 2 — Side view:** Camera repositioned to 90° from the front. Shows the 200 mm base depth and the LED recess on the top rail.

**Shot 3 — Detail: halving joint at base corner.** Camera 300 mm from the joint. Shows the flush fit and the clean finish applied to the end grain.

**Shot 4 — Detail: switch panel.** Shows the toggle switch set flush in the lower face, clean bezel, switch operates in this photograph (toggled for the picture).

**Shot 5 — In-use:** Three water bottles on the lower pegs, helmet on the top peg. LED lit (switch on). Product loaded to approximately 3.5 kg. Shows the product fulfilling its brief.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Photography session + M2 submission" time="90 min" grouping="Individual (photography station set up in classroom)" materials={["Completed product", "White background card", "Phone camera or digital camera", "Making log — complete W2–W8", "User's items (or equivalent) for in-use shot"]}>

**Final snagging (0–15 min):**
1. One last snagging pass. Fix anything small that can be fixed in 15 minutes.

**Photography setup (15–30 min):**
2. Set up the photography station: white background, window light, reflector if available.

**Photography session (30–65 min):**
3. Take all five required shots (see table above).
4. For each shot: check focus, check the product fills the frame, check no shadows obscure key features.
5. Review each photograph on the camera screen before moving on — retake if blurry or poorly lit.

**Making log final check (65–75 min):**
6. Check every session W2–W8 has a log entry. Add anything missing.

**M2 submission (75–90 min):**
7. Submit: complete product + making log (W2–W8 entries) + five folder photographs.
</Activity>

<Support title="Photographs not looking right?">

**Photos too dark?** Move closer to the window or add a second light source. Do not use the phone flash — it creates harsh flat lighting with heavy shadows directly behind the product.

**Product looks distorted?** You are probably too close and using a wide-angle lens setting. Step back and use the phone's digital zoom instead. Wide-angle distortion makes vertical lines lean outward.

**Shadows obscuring the joint or circuit detail?** Place a white card or a mirror on the shadow side to bounce light into the shadow area. A piece of white foam board works perfectly.

**In-use photo looks staged?** That is fine — it is staged. The point is to show the product working, not to capture a candid moment. If you have the user's items (a real helmet, water bottles), use them — it is more convincing.
</Support>
