Two sessions remain before the final revision period closes. This session is the last full timed practice paper — the final data point on exam readiness. The second half of the session focuses on the exam mindset: what to do the day before, the morning of, and in the first 5 minutes of the exam.

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Peak performance window", definition: "The 2–3 hour period during which a person's alertness, working memory capacity, and cognitive speed are at their daily maximum. For most people: mid-morning (9–11 am) or early afternoon (2–4 pm). If the exam is in the morning, revision and practice papers in the morning shift your performance peak to that window." },
  { term: "Pre-exam protocol", definition: "A fixed sequence of actions performed before every exam to create a consistent performance state. Includes: equipment check (calculator, pens, ruler), breathing routine, reading the paper before answering, and writing personal error reminders on the inside cover." },
  { term: "Cognitive load management", definition: "The process of reducing unnecessary mental work during the exam so that maximum working memory is available for the actual questions. Techniques: write formulas in the margin immediately; number sub-parts; use abbreviations for long terms (TS = tensile strength, FoS = factor of safety) — this frees cognitive capacity for problem-solving." },

<Section title="Exam mindset: the day before and the morning of" icon="🧠" id="mindset">

### The day before the exam

**Do:**
- Review formula cards (one final active recall — close the cards and write the formulas from memory).
- Review your personal error list (the ten error types, your personal worst offenders).
- Sleep 8+ hours. Sleep is when consolidation happens — the final night before an exam may be the highest-return sleep of the year.

**Do not:**
- Attempt to learn new material. If it is not in long-term memory by the night before, cramming it the night before typically increases anxiety without improving retention.
- Stay up past midnight. Cognitive speed drops approximately 20% with 6 hours of sleep vs 8 hours. The exam is a speed test as well as a knowledge test.

### The morning of the exam

- Eat a breakfast. Glucose is the brain's primary fuel. A missed breakfast reduces cognitive performance.
- Arrive 15 minutes early. Equipment check: calculator working? Spare pen? Ruler?
- In the first 5 minutes of the exam: write formulas in the margin before reading the questions. Write: R formula, Vout formula, FoS, stress, beam reaction. This offloads them from working memory to the page.

### In the exam: the first 5 minutes

1. Write candidate information.
2. Write formulas in the margin: R = (V_s - V_LED) / I_LED; Vout = V_s x R2 / (R1 + R2); FoS = UL / WL; Stress = F / A; RA = W x d_B / span.
3. Read the Section B context.
4. Note total marks per section and allocate time.
5. Begin.

</Section>

<Section title="Final revision consolidation — ten bullet points" icon="📋" id="consolidation">

### Everything on one page

**Structures:** (1) Frame/shell/mass types. (2) Tension/compression/bending/shear/torsion. (3) FoS = UL / WL. (4) Stress = F / A (N and mm²). (5) Beam reactions: RA = W x d_B / span; RA + RB = W.

**Materials:** (6) Mild steel: density 7.8 g/cm³, tensile strength 400 MPa. (7) Aluminium: density 2.7 g/cm³ (one-third of steel). (8) MDF: no grain direction, high machinability. (9) CFRP: highest stiffness-to-weight ratio. (10) 6Rs: Reduce/Reuse/Recycle/Refuse/Rethink/Repair.

**Electronics:** (11) IPO: switch = I, resistor = P, LED = O. (12) R = (Vs - V_LED) / I_LED. Convert mA to A first. Choose E12 above. (13) Vout = Vs x R2 / (R1 + R2). R1 = top; R2 = bottom. (14) NTC thermistor: hot → low resistance. LDR: bright → low resistance. (15) Transistor ON when base > 0.6 V.

</Section>

<Activity title="Final mixed practice paper" time="90 min" grouping="Individual (exam conditions)" materials={["Final practice paper (teacher-provided)", "Calculator", "Black pen + pencil", "Ruler"]}>

**Pre-exam protocol (0–5 min):**
1. Write formulas in the margin (from the list above) before reading questions.
2. Write your personal top-two error types on the inside cover.

**Paper (5–80 min):**
3. Section A: first pass (immediate answers) then second pass (ticked skips).
4. Section B: read all sub-parts before answering; margin plan for 4+ mark answers.
5. Check-back (final 5 minutes): sub-parts answered? Units? Blanks? Correct command word addressed?

**Post-paper analysis (80–90 min):**
6. Self-mark using the mark scheme.
7. Write: (a) total score; (b) improvement from M6 (or comparison); (c) final two remaining weak points.
8. Write a one-page action plan for the final independent revision week: what to practise, how, and when.
</Activity>

<Support title="Cognitive load management during the exam">

**Write formulas before reading questions.** Five minutes writing formulas at the start of the exam is the highest-return five minutes of the paper — it removes formula retrieval from working memory, leaving more capacity for the actual questions.

**Use abbreviations.** TS = tensile strength; FoS = factor of safety; WL = working load; UL = ultimate load; E = Young's modulus. Writing abbreviations saves time and keeps answers concise. Define the abbreviation the first time: "TS (tensile strength)" then use TS throughout.

**Number every sub-part before answering.** Write (a)(i), (a)(ii) in the margin before writing any answer — this prevents accidentally combining two separate answers into one paragraph.
</Support>
