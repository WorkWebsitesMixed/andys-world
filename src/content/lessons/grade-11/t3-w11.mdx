<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Principle of moments", definition: "For a beam in equilibrium (not rotating and not moving): the sum of all clockwise moments about any point equals the sum of all anticlockwise moments about the same point. This principle allows beam reactions to be calculated for any number of loads at any position." },
  { term: "Bending moment", definition: "The product of a force and the perpendicular distance from the force to the point being considered. Measured in N·m or N·mm. A bending moment diagram (BMD) shows how the bending moment varies along the length of a beam — essential for identifying the critical section (where bending moment is maximum)." },
  { term: "Hollow section advantage", definition: "A hollow tube (circular or rectangular) has a higher second moment of area (I) for the same mass of material compared to a solid section of the same outer dimensions. This means hollow sections are more efficient structurally — they resist bending with less material. Used in bicycle frames, scaffolding, and aircraft fuselage frames." },
  { term: "Second moment of area (I)", definition: "A geometric property of a cross-section that determines its resistance to bending. For a solid rectangle: I = b x h³ / 12, where b is the width and h is the height. For a hollow rectangle: I = I_outer minus I_inner. Higher I means less deflection for the same applied force — a deep I-beam is more efficient than a wide flat plate of the same area." },
]} />

<Section title="Two-point-load beam reactions" icon="🔧" id="two-loads">

### Setting up the equilibrium equations

For a beam with two point loads W1 at position a from A, and W2 at position b from A (span L):

**Taking moments about A (to find RB):**
RB x L = W1 x a + W2 x b
RB = (W1 x a + W2 x b) / L

**Sum of vertical forces (to find RA):**
RA + RB = W1 + W2
RA = W1 + W2 - RB

**Check:** RA + RB = W1 + W2 (must balance)

### Why take moments about A?

Taking moments about A eliminates RA from the moment equation (RA has zero moment arm about A), leaving only RB as the unknown. This allows direct calculation of RB. Once RB is known, RA follows from vertical equilibrium.

</Section>

<Section title="Hollow section stress calculation" icon="📐" id="hollow">

### Stress in a hollow rectangular tube

For a hollow tube carrying a tensile force F:
Stress = F / A_net

Where A_net is the net cross-sectional area: A_outer minus A_inner.

Example: Outer section 50 mm x 50 mm. Wall thickness 5 mm. Inner section: (50-2x5) mm x (50-2x5) mm = 40 mm x 40 mm.
A_outer = 50 x 50 = 2,500 mm².
A_inner = 40 x 40 = 1,600 mm².
A_net = 2,500 - 1,600 = 900 mm².

If F = 9,000 N: Stress = 9,000 / 900 = 10 N/mm² = 10 MPa.

### Comparing solid vs hollow for the same stress

A solid 50 x 50 mm section has A = 2,500 mm². At 9,000 N: stress = 3.6 MPa.
The hollow section achieves 10 MPa — higher stress for the same force, but uses 900/2500 = 36% of the material.

For the same stress: the hollow section supports the same force with less than half the cross-section area and therefore much less mass. This is the structural efficiency of hollow sections.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Two-point-load beam with three reactions">

**Scenario:** A steel bracket supports a shelf. The shelf carries a uniformly distributed load (but for this problem, two concentrated loads): W1 = 200 N at 300 mm from A, W2 = 150 N at 700 mm from A. Span = 1,000 mm.

**Step 1 — Take moments about A:**
RB x 1,000 = 200 x 300 + 150 x 700 = 60,000 + 105,000 = 165,000 N·mm.
RB = 165,000 / 1,000 = 165 N.

**Step 2 — Vertical equilibrium:**
RA + RB = W1 + W2 = 200 + 150 = 350 N.
RA = 350 - 165 = 185 N.

**Check:** RA + RB = 185 + 165 = 350 N = W1 + W2. Correct.

**Interpretation:** Support A carries 185 N (53%), support B carries 165 N (47%). W1 is closer to A (300 mm from A vs 700 mm from B) — so A carries more. Result is consistent with expectation.
</WorkedExample>
