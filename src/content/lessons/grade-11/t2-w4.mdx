You selected your design last week. Now you build a quick version of it to find out what drawing cannot tell you: whether the proportions feel right in 3D, whether the base is stable, whether the joints actually fit. A model that falls apart or tips over in the next ten minutes is a success — it just saved you from making those same mistakes in the makerspace.

<Callout type="exam" title="Why this matters" id="why">
  <ExamLink to="project">Component 2</ExamLink> criterion **C4 (development) is worth 15 marks**. The mark scheme specifically looks for evidence that you made and tested something physical — not just drew more ideas. A model photograph with annotated problems found scores strongly; a page of refined drawings with no physical testing does not.
</Callout>

  { term: "Development point", definition: "A specific design problem found during modelling or testing, recorded with the evidence (what happened) and a proposed fix (what you will change). Development points are the core of C4 evidence." },

<Section title="Why model before making" icon="🔧" id="modelling">

### What drawing cannot tell you

A technical drawing fixes dimensions on paper but cannot tell you:
- Whether the product feels stable when you load one side
- Whether the switch is in a place the user can reach without awkward movement
- Whether two parts that fit on paper actually fit in 3D when assembled

A 30-minute card model answers all three — and costs almost nothing.

### Types of model, matched to purpose

| Type | Purpose | Material |
|------|---------|----------|
| Sketch model | Check overall form and proportion | Card, foam, tape |
| Block model | Check scale and ergonomics | Foam board, clay |
| Scale model | Check spatial fit within 400 × 400 mm | Card at 1:2 scale |
| Mock-up | Test a specific detail (joint, switch position) | Offcuts of real material |

For this session: build a sketch model at roughly 1:1 scale so you can test it with real objects (your user's items, the actual load).

### Recording development points

For each problem the model reveals, write a development point with three parts:
1. **What happened:** "The base tips forward when I place the helmet on the top peg."
2. **Why it matters:** "This means the product would fall in use and could damage the helmet."
3. **What I will change:** "I will widen the base footprint from 150 mm to 220 mm and add a rear brace."

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="A card model of a cyclist gear organiser reveals two problems">

**Build:** A rough card frame — two vertical side panels, three horizontal pegs, a rectangular base. Assembly with masking tape, approx 1:1 scale.

**Problem 1 found:** When a 900 g helmet is placed on the top peg, the whole frame tips forward. **Fix:** Widen the base to 200 mm depth and add a rear horizontal member at the bottom to counterbalance.

**Problem 2 found:** The switch position (planned at the top of the left panel) is above arm reach for a seated user. **Fix:** Move the switch to the front face of the base at 120 mm height — reachable without stretching.

**Development points:** Two problems found in 20 minutes of building. Neither would have appeared from the plan drawing alone.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Build, break, fix" time="90 min" grouping="Individual (technician supports)" materials={["Card, foam board, corrugated card", "Masking tape, PVA", "Scissors, craft knife, cutting mat", "Your chosen design sketch and specification"]}>

**Model briefing (0–15 min):**
1. Decide what scale and what type of model best tests your design's main uncertainties. Plan it in two minutes, then start cutting — do not over-plan the model itself.

**Build (15–70 min):**
2. Construct a rough model of your structure. Prioritise the structural skeleton first (load-bearing frame), then add the main features (pegs, shelves, circuit position).
3. As you build, note anything that is fiddly or does not fit — these are development points in the making.

**Test and record (70–90 min):**
4. Load the model with the actual weight your spec requires (use water bottles, books, or weights). Does it hold? Does it tip? Does it flex?
5. Operate the switch position as your user would. Is it accessible?
6. Record at least two development points: what happened, why it matters, what you will change.
</Activity>

<Support title="Model not coming together?">

**Structure too floppy?** Real structures are rigid because of triangulation. Add a diagonal brace across any rectangular panel and the floppy rectangle becomes rigid in seconds — the same principle works in card.

**Parts not fitting?** Score card along fold lines with a blunt tool before folding — the edges will be crisp. For joints, cut tabs (1 cm extension on each part) that slot into slits in the adjacent part.

**Can't think of two development points?** Load it (does anything move that shouldn't?), hold it (does it feel like the right size for your user?), reach for the switch (is it in the right place?). Three tests, guaranteed to find something.
</Support>

<Section title="Homework" icon="🏠" id="homework">

**Home task:** Photograph your model and annotate the photograph to show the problems you found and the fixes you plan. Add the photograph and annotations to your C4 folder pages.

<Extension title="Second variant">
Build a second version of one specific feature of your model — the joint at a critical corner, or the base configuration — using a different approach. Compare the two versions: which is more stable? Which is easier to assemble? Add this comparison to your development page.
</Extension>
