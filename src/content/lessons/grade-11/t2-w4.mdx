Manufacturing begins. C6 is the practical evidence of your Component 2 project. This session covers the first half of the making process: transferring dimensions from your C5 working drawings to the material, and making accurate cuts. The quality of everything that follows depends on accurate marking out.

<Callout type="info" title="C6 manufacturing record requirement" id="c6">
  As you manufacture, photograph each stage: marked-out components before cutting, components after cutting, after joining, and after finishing. These photos form your C6 manufacturing record and are submitted alongside the finished product. Photograph before discarding any offcuts or templates.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Datum", definition: "A fixed reference point from which all measurements are taken. Using a single datum edge (usually the straightest factory edge of a sheet) minimises accumulated measurement error — each measurement is independent and starts from the same reference, rather than being measured from the last mark." },
  { term: "Marking gauge", definition: "A tool used to scribe a line parallel to a reference edge at a set distance. It has a fence (rides along the edge) and a marking pin. Used for setting out rebate depths, dado positions, and parallel lines along a timber face or edge." },
  { term: "Try square", definition: "An L-shaped precision tool used to mark or check 90-degree angles. The stock (short arm) is pressed against the reference face; the blade (long arm) is perpendicular. Lines scribed along the blade are guaranteed to be square to the reference face." },
  { term: "Kerf", definition: "The width of material removed by a saw cut. Typically 2–3 mm for a hand saw, 1–2 mm for a scroll saw. Always cut on the waste side of the line (the side to be removed), leaving the marked line intact on the component to be used." },
  { term: "Tolerance", definition: "The acceptable range of variation from a nominal dimension. For this project, a tolerance of ±1 mm is acceptable for most dimensions. A specification dimension of 200 mm means any value from 199 mm to 201 mm is acceptable." },
]} />

<Section title="Marking out: the sequence" icon="📏" id="marking-out">

### Step 1: Select and prepare the datum edge

Before making any marks, identify the datum edge of your material — typically the factory-cut straight edge. Check it with a try square against the face. If not perfectly square, rip a straight edge with a hand plane or circular saw guide before using it as a datum.

### Step 2: Mark the overall blank dimensions

Using the try square and a pencil (for wood/MDF) or scriber (for metal):
1. From the datum edge, mark the full width of the component (not the final width — the blank with material to spare).
2. From the datum end, mark the full length.
3. Cross-hatch the waste areas immediately (marks the regions to be removed — prevents cutting the wrong side).

### Step 3: Mark the joint positions

Using the marking gauge and try square:
1. Set the marking gauge to the rebate or dado depth. Scribe the depth line along the edge.
2. Using a try square, mark the shoulder lines (the positions where the dado or rebate starts and ends).
3. Mark the waste area within the joint with hatching.

### Step 4: Mark drilling positions

For holes (LED hole, wire exit hole, mounting holes):
1. Mark the centre of each hole with crossed lines.
2. Use a centre punch (for metal) or awl (for wood/MDF) to create a starting point for the drill bit — prevents the bit wandering at the start of the cut.

</Section>

<Section title="Cutting: technique by process" icon="🔧" id="cutting">

### Hand saw

Hold the workpiece firmly against a bench stop or in a vice. Start the cut with a pull stroke (fine teeth pull the saw into the material — safer to start). Maintain a consistent angle (approximately 45° to the workpiece surface for general cuts). Let the saw do the work — do not force the blade.

### Scroll saw / band saw

Set the fence parallel to the blade for rip cuts. Never force the material — let the blade feed at its own pace. Keep fingers away from the blade line by at least 50 mm (use a push stick for narrow rip cuts). After cutting, check the dimension with a steel rule immediately.

### Laser cutter (MDF/acrylic)

All geometry is transferred from the C5 drawing CAD file. Check: scale is correct in the software, material thickness matches the cut settings. Run a small test cut on a scrap piece first to verify kerf width and ensure dimensions are accurate after cutting.

### Drill press

Clamp the workpiece to the table — never hold it freehand for large holes. Select the correct bit size: 3 mm for LED (standard 3 mm LED), 2 mm for wires, countersink for fixing holes. Set depth stop to prevent drilling through (unless a through-hole is required).

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Marking out a dado joint for a shelf bracket">

**Component:** MDF back panel, 220 mm wide x 300 mm tall x 18 mm thick. A shelf (also 18 mm MDF) slots into a dado 12 mm deep.

**Step 1 — Datum:** Bottom factory edge is the datum. Check square.

**Step 2 — Shelf height position:** From datum, measure 120 mm up. Mark a horizontal pencil line. This is the bottom shoulder of the dado.

**Step 3 — Dado width:** From the 120 mm line, measure 18 mm up. Mark a second horizontal line. This is the top shoulder. The 18 mm gap between the lines matches the shelf thickness. Cross-hatch the area between.

**Step 4 — Dado depth:** Set the marking gauge to 12 mm. Run along both face edges of the panel, scribing depth lines on both edges. These are cut-off lines when chiselling.

**Step 5 — Waste hatching:** Hatch the area inside the dado (the wood to be removed) clearly. The finished component uses the wood above and below the dado, not inside it.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Mark out and cut all components" time="90 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["Your C5 working drawings", "Material prepared to blank size", "Try square, steel rule (600 mm), marking gauge", "Pencil/scriber, centre punch, awl", "Saw (hand saw or scroll saw as appropriate)", "Steel rule for post-cut checking"]}>

**Preparation check (0–10 min):**
1. Line up your C5 working drawings and identify every component to be cut in this session. Write a cut list: component name, material, quantity, dimensions (length x width x thickness).
2. Confirm you have all materials and tools at your bench before starting.

**Datum and first marks (10–25 min):**
3. Select the datum edge for your main sheet material. Check with a try square.
4. Mark the first component blank from the datum. Cross-hatch waste.

**Joint positions and hole centres (25–45 min):**
5. Mark all joint positions using the marking gauge and try square. Cross-hatch waste inside joint areas.
6. Mark all hole centres with crossed lines. Use a centre punch or awl on each centre mark.

**Cutting (45–80 min):**
7. Cut components in this sequence: large panels first, then small parts from offcuts. Cut on the waste side of every line.
8. After each cut, measure with a steel rule immediately and compare to the C5 dimension. Record any variance.

**Post-cut check (80–90 min):**
9. Complete the cut list: tick each component. Record actual dimensions versus nominal. Note any components that need re-cutting or adjustment. Photograph all marked and cut components before tidying up.
</Activity>

<Support title="Marking and cutting accuracy">

**Lines not square?** The try square must be held firmly against the reference face before scribing. If the stock is not seated flat, the blade is not perpendicular. Check by scribing a line, flipping the square, and scribing the same line again from the opposite side — the two lines should coincide.

**Cuts consistently 2–3 mm over?** You are cutting on the line (removing the line itself). The cut must be on the WASTE side — leave the pencil line visible on the finished component.

**Laser cut dimensions slightly smaller than specified?** Kerf takes material from both sides of the cut line. If a 200 mm wide part comes out at 197 mm, the kerf is 3 mm — offset the CAD shape by 1.5 mm on each side in the software to compensate.
</Support>
