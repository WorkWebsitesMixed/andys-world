The question on Paper 4 will not say "name a wood." It will say "justify your choice of material for the vertical support, referring to two properties." The difference between a zero-mark answer and a full-mark answer is justification. This session gives you the vocabulary and the habits to justify any material choice in any context.

<Section title="Wood families" icon="🌲" id="wood">

### Hardwoods

Hardwoods come from broad-leaved trees that typically take 50–200 years to reach harvestable size. This slower growth produces denser, more complex grain structure.

| Hardwood | Key properties | Common use |
|---|---|---|
| Oak | Hard, heavy, durable, attractive grain | Furniture, flooring, structural beams |
| Teak | Naturally oily, highly rot-resistant, heavy | Outdoor furniture, boat decks |
| Mahogany | Easy to work, stable, attractive colour | High-quality furniture, veneers |
| Ash | Tough, flexible (high impact resistance) | Tool handles, sports equipment |

### Softwoods

Softwoods come from coniferous trees, harvested in 25–60 years. Their faster growth makes them cheaper and more available.

| Softwood | Key properties | Common use |
|---|---|---|
| Pine | Relatively easy to work, light, knotty | Furniture, construction framing |
| Spruce | Light, good strength-to-weight ratio | Aircraft (historically), musical instruments |
| Larch | Durable, more resin-resistant | Cladding, outdoor structures |

### Manufactured boards

Manufactured boards are stable, flat, and available in large sheets — advantages over solid timber for workshop projects.

| Board | Made from | Properties | Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| MDF | Wood fibres + resin binder | Very smooth surface, no grain direction, easy to machine, absorbs moisture | Furniture, shelving, laser cutting |
| Plywood | Thin veneers glued with alternating grain | Strong in multiple directions, holds screws well, available in structural grades | Boxes, structural panels, aircraft |
| Chipboard | Wood chips + resin | Cheap, heavy, poor edge quality, absorbs moisture | Budget shelving, kitchen carcasses |

**For your project:** MDF is the most common choice for laser-cut structural parts — it cuts cleanly, machines easily, and is dimensionally consistent. Its weakness is moisture absorption — use it only for indoor products.

</Section>

<Section title="Metals" icon="⚙️" id="metals">

### Ferrous vs non-ferrous

| Category | Contains | Key characteristic | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ferrous | Iron | Magnetic; rusts unless protected | Mild steel, cast iron, stainless steel |
| Non-ferrous | No iron | Non-magnetic; generally corrosion-resistant | Aluminium, copper, brass, titanium |

### Key metals for your project context

**Mild steel:** Strong in both tension and compression; relatively cheap; easy to weld; rusts without protection (paint, galvanise, or powder-coat). Tensile strength approximately 400 MPa.

**Aluminium:** Light (density 2.7 g/cm³ vs steel's 7.8 g/cm³); naturally oxidises to form a protective layer; cannot be welded without specialist equipment; anodised for colour and extra protection. Tensile strength approximately 270 MPa (pure) to 500 MPa (alloyed).

**Stainless steel:** Contains at least 10.5% chromium — the chromium forms a passive oxide layer that prevents rust. Used where both strength and corrosion resistance are needed (kitchen appliances, medical instruments, architectural fixtures).

### Working properties — the vocabulary of justification

| Property | Definition | How to use it in a justification |
|---|---|---|
| Hardness | Resistance to surface indentation or scratching | "I chose oak because its hardness means the surface will resist scratching from daily use." |
| Toughness | Ability to absorb impact without fracturing | "Ash is tough — it absorbs the repeated impact of tool handles without cracking." |
| Ductility | Ability to be drawn into a wire without breaking | "Copper's high ductility allows it to be drawn into thin wiring for the circuit." |
| Malleability | Ability to be hammered/pressed into shape | "Aluminium's malleability allows it to be pressed into curved bracket forms." |
| Tensile strength | Resistance to being pulled apart | "Mild steel's high tensile strength resists the tensile force in the bottom chord of the shelf bracket." |
| Density | Mass per unit volume | "Aluminium's low density (2.7 g/cm³) keeps the product lightweight for a wall-mounted application." |

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Justify mild steel vs aluminium for a shelf bracket vertical post">

**The question:** "Justify the choice of mild steel for the vertical support post of a wall-mounted shelf bracket. Refer to two working properties."

**Full-mark answer:** "Mild steel has a tensile strength of approximately 400 MPa, which means the post can resist the bending force induced when the shelf is loaded without permanent deformation. Its high density (7.8 g/cm³) is not a disadvantage for this application because the post is wall-mounted and does not need to be lightweight. Mild steel can also be powder-coated to prevent corrosion in the indoor environment where the shelf will be used."

**Why this scores:** it names a specific property (tensile strength), states the numerical value (400 MPa), and links it to the function (resisting bending under load). A second property (density) is mentioned with a contextual justification (indoor, wall-mounted). The answer uses technical vocabulary throughout.

**The generic version (zero marks):** "Mild steel is strong and durable, which makes it a good choice for the post."
</WorkedExample>

<Support title="Justification sentence framework">

**Template:** "[Material] was chosen for [component] because its [property name] means [consequence for the product]. Its [second property] also [links to function or user need]."

**Example:** "Oak was chosen for the shelf surface because its hardness means daily use (placing and removing textbooks) will not scratch or dent the surface. Its attractive grain also meets the user's aesthetic requirement for a natural-looking product."

**For non-ferrous metals:** Begin with "As a non-ferrous metal, [material] does not contain iron, which means it does not rust — an advantage for a product stored near a window."
</Support>
