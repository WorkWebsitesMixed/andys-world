You can't choose the right material for your product until you know how materials
behave. This week we start **types of materials** with the two families you'll meet
most in structures: **woods** and **metals**.

<Section title="The lesson" icon="📋" id="lesson">

### 1. Woods

- **Hardwoods** (oak, beech) — dense and strong, but more expensive.
- **Softwoods** (pine) — lighter, cheaper and easy to work, good for frames.
- **Manufactured boards** (plywood, MDF) — come in big, stable sheets, ideal for
  laser-cutting.

### 2. Metals

- **Ferrous** metals contain **iron** — e.g. **mild steel**: strong and cheap, but
  it can **rust** unless protected.
- **Non-ferrous** metals have **no iron** — e.g. **aluminium**: light and naturally
  **corrosion-resistant**.

### 3. Talking about properties

Describe materials with precise **working properties** — *strength* (resists force),
*hardness* (resists scratching), *toughness* (resists snapping), *workability* (easy
to shape). Never use empty words like "strong" or "good" on their own.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Worked example — choosing a beam material">
1. **The job:** a shelf beam that must not sag and won't cost much.
2. **Option — pine (softwood):** light, cheap and easy to work, but can bend under heavy loads.
3. **Option — mild steel:** resists bending and most forces, can be formed into stiff sections, but heavier and may rust.
4. **Decide & justify:** *"Mild steel angle, because it resists bending under the load and can be bolted easily — accepting extra weight and a protective coating to stop rust."*
</WorkedExample>
