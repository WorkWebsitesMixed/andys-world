This year's project starts with **a person, not a product.** Today you'll find a
real person to design for, meet the brief, and see how a genuine frustration turns
into a structural product worth building. That project runs all year — and it's
worth **half of your whole IGCSE**.

<Section title="The lesson" icon="📋" id="lesson">

### 1. Design is a process, not a guess

Good products aren't invented in one flash of genius — they're worked out step by
step. The **design process** runs **identify → research → specify → generate →
develop → plan → make → test/evaluate**. It's a *loop*: testing an idea often sends
you back to research. This year you'll walk the whole cycle once, properly.

### 2. Start with the person

Here's the rule that makes the difference: **don't start by choosing a product —
start by understanding a person.** Find someone you know (family, friend, classmate,
neighbour) and learn what they do and what **frustrates** them about how they store,
access or display the things that matter to them. The *product* comes later, out of
that real need. A product designed for a real person almost always beats one copied
from a shop.

### 3. The brief — what's fixed, what's open

Your product **must**: **carry a defined load** (a weight you state and test);
include a **low-voltage, switch-operated circuit**; be made from the **make-palette**
in the makerspace; **fit within 400 × 400 mm** (one dimension ≥ 150 mm); and be a
**real, finished product** your user would be proud to own.

**You decide** everything else: the user and their context, the problem, what the
product is and does, and its form and dimensions.

<Callout type="tip" title="Constrain the HOW, open the WHAT">
  The *making requirements are fixed* — so making stays fair for the whole class. But
  your *user, problem, product, form and finish are completely yours.* That freedom is
  where the marks live.
</Callout>

Here's the pattern in three real examples. Notice how each one runs **person →
frustration → product**, and how different they are from each other. Follow the
*pattern* — don't copy the products.

<ProjectExamples />

<WorkedExample title="Worked example — from a person to a brief">
1. **Meet the person:** Ana, a cyclist who rides several times a week.
2. **Observe the frustration:** before every ride she hunts for her helmet, shoes and tools, scattered by the door.
3. **Find the structural need:** one place that holds all her gear, off the floor, ready to grab.
4. **Picture the form:** a wall or floor organiser with hooks and a small shelf.
5. **State the load:** it must hold her gear — say 4 kg — without sagging (something she can measure).
6. **Add the circuit:** a "ready to go" light that's on when everything is in its place.
7. **Write the brief:** *"I'm designing a gear organiser for Ana, a daily cyclist, who needs to grab her kit quickly because she loses time hunting for it before rides."*
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Write your person profile" time="30 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["Folder page 1", "Pencil"]}>
This is <ExamLink to="project">**Criterion 1** (need &amp; brief)</ExamLink> — the first page in your folder.

1. **Write a person profile.** Choose a real person and note their **name or role**,
   **what they do or love doing**, and **at least three things that frustrate them**
   about how they store, access, display or organise something. **No products yet.**
2. **Draft a one-sentence brief** using this frame:
   *"I am designing a [product] for [name/role] who needs to [function] because
   [observed need]."*

Keep your profile — it's where your whole project grows from.
</Activity>

<Support title="Need a hand? Frustration starters + a brief frame">
Spot a frustration with one of these:

- *"… finds it hard to …"*, *"… wastes time when …"*, *"… has nowhere to …"*

Then turn it into a brief with the frame:
*"I am designing a **……** for **……** who needs to **……** because **……**."*
</Support>
