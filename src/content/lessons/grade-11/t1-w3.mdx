Structures is the theory backbone of the entire IGCSE course and of your Component 2 project. This session compresses everything — types of structure, forces, stress, factor of safety, beam reactions, and joints — into one 90-minute session with a diagnostic at the start and calculations practice at the end. It feels like a lot, but every concept here shows up in Paper 4 Section B.

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Frame structure", definition: "A structure made of individual members (rods, beams, or struts) connected at joints. The members carry the load; the spaces between them carry nothing. Examples: bicycle frame, roof truss, shelf bracket, Eiffel Tower." },
  { term: "Shell structure", definition: "A structure where the outer skin carries the load. The surface itself is the structural element — there is no internal frame. Examples: egg, car body panel, dome, tin can." },
  { term: "Mass structure", definition: "A solid structure where the whole volume of material is under compression. No internal cavity. Examples: dam wall, concrete foundation, brick wall, cliff." },
  { term: "Factor of Safety (FoS)", definition: "The ratio of the load at which a structure fails to the maximum load it is designed to carry in use. FoS = Failure Load divided by Working Load. A FoS of 1 means the structure would fail exactly at its working load — far too risky. Structural engineering uses FoS of 2 to 6 or higher." },
  { term: "Strut", definition: "A structural member under compression. It pushes back against the load trying to squash it. Columns and vertical posts are typically struts." },
  { term: "Tie", definition: "A structural member under tension. It resists being pulled apart. The bottom chord of a roof truss is typically a tie." },
]} />

<Section title="Types of structure" icon="🏗️" id="types">

### Frame, shell, and mass

| Type | How load is carried | Key feature | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Frame | Members and joints | Spaces between members carry nothing | Bicycle frame, roof truss, your shelf project |
| Shell | Outer skin | Surface is the structural element | Egg, car bonnet, dome |
| Mass | Entire volume | All material in compression | Dam, brick wall, concrete foundation |

**Exam trap:** A cardboard box is a shell structure, not a frame. The faces carry the load. A box made of straws glued at the corners is a frame — the straws carry load, the spaces do not.

### Triangulation: why triangles are rigid

A triangle cannot be deformed without changing the length of one of its sides. A square frame can be racked (pushed into a parallelogram) without changing side lengths. Adding a diagonal brace converts the square into two triangles — now rigid. This is why trusses, bicycle frames, and roof structures use triangulated geometry.

</Section>

<Section title="Forces on structures" icon="⚡" id="forces">

### The five forces

| Force | Direction | What it does | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tension | Pulls apart along the axis | Stretches the member | Bottom chord of a truss, guy wire |
| Compression | Pushes together along the axis | Squashes the member | Column, strut, concrete wall |
| Shear | Acts across the member | Cuts or slides layers | Bolt through a joint, scissors |
| Torsion | Twists around the axis | Rotates the cross-section | Driveshaft, screwdriver bit |
| Bending | Combination of tension + compression | Curves the member | Loaded beam (top face in compression, bottom in tension) |

**On a loaded beam:** the top face is in compression (being squashed by the load); the bottom face is in tension (being stretched as the beam sags). The point in between where stress is zero is the **neutral axis**. This is why I-beams have flanges top and bottom — most of the material is placed where the stress is highest, away from the neutral axis.

</Section>

<Section title="Stress and Factor of Safety" icon="📐" id="stress-fos">

### Stress

Stress = Force / Area

If a force of 500 N acts on a cross-section of 50 mm², the stress is 500 / 50 = 10 N/mm² (which equals 10 MPa).

A thicker cross-section reduces stress for the same force. This is why structural members get wider at points of high load.

### Factor of Safety

FoS = Failure Load / Working Load

**Example:** A shelf bracket fails (permanently deforms) at 200 N. It is designed to carry a maximum load of 50 N.
FoS = 200 / 50 = 4.0

A FoS of 4.0 means the bracket could carry four times its working load before failing. For structures that affect human safety (buildings, bridges), FoS values of 2.5–6 are typical.

</Section>

<Section title="Beam reactions" icon="⚖️" id="beam-reactions">

### Simple supported beam

A simply supported beam rests on two supports (A and B). When a load W is applied at some point along the beam, the two supports share the reaction. The method:

1. Take moments about support A to find reaction at B.
2. Then use equilibrium (RA + RB = total load) to find RA.

**Moments about A:** Sum of clockwise moments = Sum of anticlockwise moments. The reaction at B creates an anticlockwise moment about A.

Example: A beam 4 m long, supported at A (left) and B (right). A load of 600 N is applied 1 m from A.

Moment of load about A = 600 N x 1 m = 600 N·m (clockwise)
Moment of RB about A = RB x 4 m (anticlockwise)

Setting equal: RB x 4 = 600, so RB = 150 N
Then: RA = 600 - 150 = 450 N

Check: 450 + 150 = 600 N (correct — equals total load).

</Section>

<Section title="Structural joints" icon="🔩" id="joints">

| Joint | Sketch description | Load it handles | Used for |
|---|---|---|---|
| Halving joint | Two members notched halfway through, overlapping | Shear at crossing point | Cross-frames, timber grids |
| Bridle joint | One member has a slot, the other a tongue — mechanical interlock | Tension and shear | Corners in timber frames |
| Mortise and tenon | Tenon (rectangular peg) fits into mortise (rectangular hole) | Bending and shear | Furniture legs to rails |
| Dowel joint | Round wooden pegs align two pieces | Shear | Furniture panels |
| KD fitting | Cam lock and bolt — disassembles without tools | Tension and compression | Flatpack furniture |

For your Component 2 project, you must use **at least two** different joining methods. KD fittings plus wood adhesive is one valid combination.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Three calculations in sequence">

**Q1 — FoS:** A shelf is designed to hold 30 kg (approximately 300 N). It fails at 1,200 N. FoS = 1200 / 300 = 4.0. Is this adequate for a domestic shelf? Yes — a FoS of 4 provides comfortable safety margin.

**Q2 — Stress:** The vertical support of the shelf bracket is 25 mm x 25 mm square section timber. The maximum compressive load is 300 N.
Stress = Force / Area = 300 / (25 x 25) = 300 / 625 = 0.48 N/mm² (well within timber's compressive strength of ~30 N/mm²).

**Q3 — Beam reaction:** The shelf (length 600 mm) is supported at the wall (A, left) and a bracket tip (B, right). A load of 400 N acts 200 mm from A.
Moment about A: RB x 600 = 400 x 200 = 80,000 N·mm
RB = 80,000 / 600 = 133 N
RA = 400 - 133 = 267 N
Check: 267 + 133 = 400 N.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Structures diagnostic and four exercises" time="90 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["Graph paper or squared paper", "Ruler", "Calculator"]}>

**Diagnostic quiz (0–10 min):** Teacher-led — 6 questions, no marks. Answers discussed immediately.

**Exercise 1 (10–25 min) — Classify:** Given eight photographs of real structures (truss bridge, eggshell, stone wall, bicycle wheel, car bonnet, tent, concrete dam, roof truss) — classify each as frame, shell, or mass. Justify in one sentence each.

**Exercise 2 (25–40 min) — Label forces:** On three given diagrams (loaded beam, roof truss, guy wire) — label each member or region with its force type (tension, compression, shear, bending). Use T, C, S, B labels.

**Exercise 3 (40–65 min) — Calculations:** Complete four problems:
- FoS given failure load and working load (two problems).
- Working load given FoS and failure load (one problem).
- Beam reaction for a simply supported beam with one point load.
Show all working: formula, substitution, calculation, units.

**Exercise 4 (65–85 min) — Joints:** Sketch three structural joints from the list (halving, bridle, mortise and tenon, dowel, KD fitting). For each: name, sketch (clear enough to identify the joint type), and one sentence on where it would be used in your Component 2 project.

**Review (85–90 min):** Swap papers with a neighbour. Check one calculation each.
</Activity>

<Support title="Calculation and classification support">

**FoS calculation strip:**
- FoS = Failure Load / Working Load
- Working Load = Failure Load / FoS
- Failure Load = Working Load x FoS

**Beam reaction method card:**
1. Draw the beam, mark supports A and B, mark load with position.
2. Take moments about A: (RB x beam length) = (Load x distance from A).
3. Solve for RB.
4. Find RA = Total load - RB.
5. Check: RA + RB must equal total load.

**Structure classification — if uncertain:** Ask "does the skin/surface carry the load, or do separate members carry it?" If separate members (rods, beams, struts), it is a frame. If the surface itself carries the load, it is a shell. If it is solid throughout and all material is under compression, it is a mass.
</Support>
