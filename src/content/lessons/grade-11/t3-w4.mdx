This week your product becomes a real object: you assemble the **main structure**.
The goal is a body that's **rigid and square** — and if a joint fights you, that's
normal. Stay calm and solve it.

<Callout type="exam" title="Why this matters" id="why">
  This is <ExamLink to="project">Criterion 6</ExamLink> (realisation). A rigid, square
  structure is the backbone of a product that "functions well" — and it's also this
  week's **making milestone** (see below).
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Temporary joint", definition: "One that can be undone — fixings, KD fittings." },
  { term: "Permanent joint", definition: "One that can't — adhesive, rivet, weld." },
  { term: "Rigidity", definition: "How well the structure resists bending or racking — often from triangulation." },
  { term: "Square", definition: "Corners at a true 90°." },

<Section title="The lesson" icon="📋" id="lesson">

### 1. Choose the right join

Recap: **temporary** joints (fixings, KD fittings) can be undone; **permanent** ones
(adhesive, rivet, weld) can't. Use what your design calls for.

### 2. Rigid and square

Assemble so the body is **square** (check the diagonals) and **rigid**. If it racks,
the cure is **triangulation** — add a diagonal brace, exactly as you learned in
Term 1.

<TriangulationDiagram />

### 3. Clamp and cure

Glued joints need **clamping** while they **cure** — rushing this is how products come
apart later.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Worked example — a square, rigid frame">
1. **Dry-fit** the frame and check the diagonals are equal (square).
2. **Glue and clamp** the joints, keeping it square while it cures.
3. **Add a corner brace** (a small triangle) to stop the frame racking.
4. **Test:** push it gently — rigid and square? The structure is sound.
</WorkedExample>
