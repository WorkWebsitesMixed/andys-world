All parts are made. Today you commit. Gluing up a frame is a series of permanent decisions: once the PVA is cured, joints cannot move. This session covers gluing the main structure, assembling any sub-assemblies, and confirming squareness before the glue sets.

<Callout type="exam" title="Why this matters" id="why">
  <ExamLink to="project">Component 2</ExamLink> criterion **C6 (making, 30 marks)** includes marks for quality of assembly — square frames, flush joints, clean clamping. Photographs taken during and after glue-up are evidence for these marks. A frame that is assembled skewed or with gaps at joints reduces C6 quality marks significantly.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "PVA woodworking adhesive", definition: "Polyvinyl acetate glue — the standard adhesive for wood-to-wood joints. Drying time: 30–60 minutes to handle; full cure: 12–24 hours. PVA joints in birch ply are stronger than the wood when fully cured. Clean off squeeze-out immediately with a damp cloth before it dries." },
  { term: "Squareness check", definition: "Measuring the diagonal dimensions of an assembled frame before the glue cures. If both diagonals are equal, the frame is square. If they differ, apply clamp pressure at the longer diagonal corner to close the angle." },
  { term: "Sub-assembly", definition: "A component group assembled separately before being fitted to the main structure. In this project: the circuit board mounted in its bracket, and the peg assembly (pegs glued into their holes). Sub-assemblies are easier to work on before they are permanently fitted to the frame." },
  { term: "Clamping pressure", definition: "The force applied to a joint during glue curing to ensure full surface contact. Too little: gaps remain, the joint is weak. Too much: the adhesive is squeezed out of the joint and the mating surfaces are dry. One or two F-clamps per joint at hand-tight pressure is usually correct." },
]} />

<Section title="Glue-up sequence" icon="🔨" id="glueup">

### Preparing for glue-up

Before applying any glue:
1. Set out all clamps — you will not have time to search for them once the glue is wet.
2. Set out a flat surface (assembly board) to glue up on. Warp the board slightly to check it is flat.
3. Do a final dry assembly. This is your last chance to find a problem.
4. Have a damp cloth ready for squeeze-out.

### Gluing the main frame

Apply PVA to one face of each joint only (not both) — a thin even layer, not thick. Assemble in the correct order (usually from the bottom up). Apply clamps. Check squareness immediately: measure both diagonals. If they differ by more than 2 mm, adjust clamps before the glue sets.

Leave in clamps for at least 30 minutes before handling, and a full 12 hours before applying any load.

### Sub-assemblies

**Circuit bracket:** Screw or glue the circuit board to the 3D-printed bracket. Route the wires through the cable exit. Mount the bracket in the base cavity — do not permanently fix it yet (you need access for wiring in W5).

**Peg assembly:** Apply a small amount of PVA to each peg dowel end. Insert into the holes. Wipe excess immediately. Leave overnight.

</Section>

<Activity title="Glue-up + sub-assemblies" time="90 min" grouping="Individual (technician on hand; clamps available from resource store)" materials={["All parts from W3", "PVA woodworking glue", "F-clamps and G-clamps", "Tape measure / rule for diagonal check", "Damp cloth", "Camera"]}>

**Prepare for glue-up (0–20 min):**
1. Final dry assembly on the flat assembly board. Check everything fits.
2. Set out all required clamps. Have cloth and glue ready.

**Glue main frame (20–60 min):**
3. Apply PVA to joint faces (one face per joint).
4. Assemble in the planned order.
5. Apply clamps at normal hand pressure.
6. Measure both diagonals. Adjust if they differ by more than 2 mm.
7. Wipe all squeeze-out with damp cloth immediately.
8. Leave clamped — do not move for the rest of the session.

**Sub-assemblies (60–80 min):**
9. Glue peg dowels. Check they stand vertical.
10. Dry-fit circuit bracket in its cavity.

**Making log (80–90 min):**
11. Photograph the clamped assembly. Write the making log entry: glue-up sequence, squareness measurement, sub-assembly status.
</Activity>

<Support title="Glue-up going wrong?">

**Frame is out of square and glue is setting?** Work quickly. Apply a sash clamp or rope tourniquet diagonally across the longer diagonal (corner to corner). The pressure closes the acute angle and opens the obtuse angle. Check diagonals again. If the glue has already set, the frame is out of square permanently — note it in the making log and discuss with the teacher whether it affects C6 marks.

**Too much squeeze-out?** You used too much PVA. Remove excess with the damp cloth immediately — PVA is water-soluble before it sets but very hard to remove afterwards. A thin squeeze bead around the joint is normal and good (shows full coverage); large blobs need removing.

**Clamps sliding off?** Use clamp pads (offcuts of scrap timber) between the clamp jaw and the part. The pad distributes pressure and prevents the clamp jaw from marking the surface or sliding off a rounded edge.
</Support>
