Paper 1 tests the design process directly: brief analysis, idea generation, development, sustainability, testing, and evaluation — all applied to a given context in 60 minutes. This session reviews the seven-part structure and runs a complete Paper 1 simulation.

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Design context", definition: "The scenario given at the start of Paper 1 — a design problem with a named user, a function, and specific constraints. All seven parts of the paper are applied to this context. The context may include a photograph of an existing product, a brief, or a description of a user's need." },
  { term: "Function point", definition: "A specific, testable statement about what the product must do. Paper 1 part (a) asks for three to five function points. Each must begin 'The product must...' and describe a specific function, not a material or appearance. 'The product must be red' is not a function point — 'The product must be visually distinctive from the desk surface' is a function point." },
  { term: "Part (d) development move", definition: "Paper 1 part (d) asks to 'show a development of one of the ideas from part (c)'. A development move must: identify a problem in the original idea, show the change (sketch), and explain how the change improves the design. It uses the same before-and-after format practised in C4." },
]} />

<Section title="Paper 1 parts at a glance — 60 minutes total" icon="📄" id="structure">

| Part | What is asked | Marks | Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| (a) | Three function points for the brief | 3 | 5 min |
| (b) | Identify the target user | 2 | 3 min |
| (c) | Three annotated design ideas | 12 | 15 min |
| (d) | Development of one idea | 4 | 8 min |
| (e) | Sustainability (apply one or two 6Rs) | 4 | 7 min |
| (f) | Two testing criteria with test methods | 4 | 7 min |
| (g) | Evaluation statement — met or not met | 4 | 7 min |
| Spare | Buffer for review | — | 8 min |
| **Total** | | **33** | **60 min** |

Note: actual mark totals vary by paper. The time allocations above are a guide.

### Critical time rules

- Part (c) is the most time-consuming and the biggest trap: a student who spends 30 minutes on three sketches earns the same marks as one who spends 15 minutes — but loses 15 minutes from every other part.
- After the allocated time for each part, write a brief partial answer for any uncompleted section and move on.

</Section>

<Section title="Part (c): three ideas in 15 minutes" icon="💡" id="ideas">

### The 5-minute idea protocol

Each idea gets exactly 5 minutes:
- 1 minute: sketch the overall form (isometric or oblique).
- 2 minutes: add thick/thin line and the main structural features.
- 2 minutes: write four annotations (material + reason, join, function, dimension).

At 5 minutes: the idea is complete. Start the next idea.

### Annotation minimum for full marks

Each idea must show annotations covering:
- Named material with one property linked to function.
- Named join method with force it resists.
- Function statement for the key structural element.
- One key dimension in mm.

Without all four types, part (c) sub-marks are lost.

</Section>

<Section title="Parts (f) and (g): testing and evaluation" icon="🧪" id="testing">

### Part (f) — testing criteria

Two testable criteria from the specification. Each must have:
- The criterion stated (what is measured).
- The test method (how it is measured, what equipment).

Example: "The product width must not exceed 400 mm — tested by measuring with a steel rule from the left edge to the right edge of the assembled product."

### Part (g) — evaluation

One sentence per spec point (at minimum): did the product meet this criterion? Evidence? If not — what modification?

The mark scheme awards marks for: specific evidence, a clear met/not met judgement, a specific modification suggestion for any shortfall.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Complete part (a)–(b) response for a desk cable organiser">

**Context:** Design a product to manage charging cables on a student's desk. The user is a 16-year-old student who uses three devices simultaneously and wants a neat desk surface.

**Part (a) — Function points:**
1. "The product must hold a minimum of three charging cables without tangling."
2. "The product must secure cables so they remain accessible when not plugged in."
3. "The product must attach to the desk surface without permanent adhesive or fixings that would damage the surface."

**Part (b) — Target user:**
"The target user is a 16-year-old secondary school student who charges a phone, tablet, and laptop simultaneously at their desk. They value a neat workspace and use the desk for 4–6 hours per day. Their desk has a maximum clearance of 120 mm to the wall, which constrains the product depth."
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Paper 1 simulation" time="90 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["Paper 1 simulation sheet (teacher-provided)", "Pencil and black pen", "30-60-90 set square"]}>

**Timed Paper 1 simulation (0–60 min):**
1. Sit the simulation under timed conditions. Use the time allocations from the table above.
2. Set a phone timer or watch the clock — move on from each part at the end of its allocated time, even if the part feels incomplete.
3. Do not erase — time spent erasing wastes marks. Cross out and redo rather than erasing if space allows.

**Self-mark (60–75 min):**
4. Mark scheme distributed. Score the simulation.
5. For part (c): award 1 mark per idea that has all four annotation types. Award 0 if any annotation type is missing on that idea.
6. For parts (f) and (g): award marks only for specific, measurable criteria and test methods — not for generic statements.

**Gap analysis (75–90 min):**
7. Write which of the seven parts lost the most marks. Why?
8. Write one specific improvement you will apply to each part in the real Paper 1 (or next simulation).
</Activity>

<Support title="Paper 1 time management">

**Running out of time on part (c)?** Stop at 5 minutes per idea regardless of completion level. A third idea that is 60% complete earns partial marks; a missing third idea earns zero. The examiner credits what is there.

**Part (g) feels vague?** For each function point from part (a): state whether the designed product (from part (c)) meets it. Use the language: "Function point [number]: MET/NOT MET because [specific feature of your design from part (c)]."

**Running out of time overall?** In the last 5 minutes, write one bullet point for each unanswered sub-part. A bullet point may earn 1 mark. Nothing earns zero.
</Support>
