Materials questions appear throughout Paper 4 Section A and Section B. The single most common mark-loss in materials answers is generic vocabulary — so this session pairs a complete materials reference review with intensive vocabulary drilling.

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Working properties", definition: "Properties that describe how a material behaves during manufacturing processes. Key working properties: machinability (ease of cutting/drilling), ductility (ability to be bent without breaking), malleability (ability to be pressed into shape), weldability (ability to form permanent joins by melting), castability (ability to flow into a mould and reproduce detail)." },
  { term: "Mechanical properties", definition: "Properties that describe how a material responds to applied forces. Key mechanical properties: tensile strength (resistance to being pulled apart, in MPa), compressive strength (resistance to being crushed), hardness (resistance to surface indentation), toughness (resistance to impact — absorbs energy without fracturing), stiffness/Young's modulus (resistance to elastic deformation, in GPa)." },
  { term: "Physical properties", definition: "Properties that describe intrinsic characteristics not related to force application. Key physical properties: density (mass per unit volume, g/cm³), thermal conductivity (heat transfer rate), electrical conductivity (current flow rate), melting point (°C)." },
  { term: "Comparative statement", definition: "A materials property claim that compares two materials rather than stating an absolute value. Example: 'Aluminium has a density approximately one-third that of mild steel (2.7 g/cm³ vs 7.8 g/cm³).' Comparative statements earn marks even when an absolute value is not memorised, because they show understanding of relative properties." },
]} />

### Timber and manufactured boards

| Material | Density (g/cm³) | Key property for exam |
|---|---|---|
| Balsa (softwood) | 0.1–0.2 | Very low density — lightest structural wood |
| Pine (softwood) | 0.5–0.6 | Low cost, moderate strength, visible grain |
| Oak (hardwood) | 0.7–0.9 | High hardness, high durability, expensive |
| MDF | 0.7–0.8 | Uniform density, no grain, high machinability |
| Plywood | 0.5–0.7 | Cross-ply construction — strength in all directions |

### Metals

| Material | Density (g/cm³) | Tensile strength (MPa) | Key note |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aluminium | 2.7 | 90–500 (alloy dependent) | Low density, corrosion-resistant, non-ferrous |
| Mild steel | 7.8 | 400 | High strength, ferrous, rusts without treatment |
| Copper | 8.9 | 210 | High electrical conductivity, non-ferrous |
| Stainless steel | 8.0 | 500–900 | Corrosion-resistant (chromium content), ferrous |

### Plastics

| Material | Type | Key property |
|---|---|---|
| Acrylic (PMMA) | Thermoplastic | Transparent, brittle, easy to laser-cut |
| ABS | Thermoplastic | Impact-resistant, used in 3D printing |
| Polypropylene | Thermoplastic | Living hinge (flexes without breaking) |
| Epoxy resin | Thermoset | High strength adhesive/composite matrix |
| Polyester resin | Thermoset | GRP matrix; cheap; yellows with UV |

### Composites

| Composite | Matrix | Reinforcement | Key property |
|---|---|---|---|
| GRP (fibreglass) | Polyester resin | Glass fibre | Corrosion-resistant, mouldable, boat hulls |
| CFRP | Epoxy resin | Carbon fibre | Very high stiffness-to-weight ratio, aerospace |
| Reinforced concrete | Concrete | Steel rebar | High compressive AND tensile strength |

<Section title="The vocabulary substitution drill" icon="✏️" id="vocab">

### Seven words to eliminate forever

| Never write | Always replace with |
|---|---|
| Strong | High tensile strength (state value in MPa) |
| Light | Low density (state value in g/cm³) |
| Durable | High corrosion resistance / high hardness (specify which) |
| Easy to work | High machinability — can be laser-cut / drilled / formed with school equipment |
| Rigid | High Young's modulus / high stiffness — resists elastic deformation |
| Safe | Meets SELV voltage rating / smooth edges (specify the safety criterion) |
| Sustainable | Applies [named R] — e.g. "Recyclable at end of life because it is a single material" |

### Three-part materials answer format

For any "justify the material choice" question:
1. **Name the specific property and its value:** "Mild steel has a tensile strength of approximately 400 MPa..."
2. **Link to the product's function:** "...which means the bracket arm resists the bending force from a 5 kg shelf load without permanent deformation..."
3. **Acknowledge the trade-off (for multi-mark answers):** "...although mild steel is ferrous and will corrode without a surface treatment — powder coating was applied to protect the surface."

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Transforming four 0-mark answers into full-mark answers">

**Q: Explain why acrylic is suitable for a transparent display shelf. [2 marks]**

0-mark: "Acrylic is good because it is see-through and looks nice."
Full-mark: "Acrylic is a thermoplastic with high optical transparency (transmits over 92% of visible light) — more transparent than glass at equivalent thickness. It can also be formed into complex curves using a line bender, allowing the shelf profile to be shaped to the display context without requiring mould tooling."

**Q: State one advantage of using MDF for a laser-cut component. [1 mark]**

0-mark: "MDF is easy to use."
Full-mark: "MDF has no grain direction, so it cuts cleanly on a laser cutter in any orientation without splitting — unlike pine which may tear along grain lines."

**Q: Compare the densities of aluminium and mild steel. [2 marks]**

0-mark: "Aluminium is lighter than steel."
Full-mark: "Aluminium has a density of approximately 2.7 g/cm³, compared to mild steel at 7.8 g/cm³ — approximately one-third the density. This means an aluminium bracket of the same geometry has approximately one-third the mass of a mild steel equivalent, which reduces the total mass of wall-mounted products and the load on the wall fixing."
</WorkedExample>

<Support title="Materials values you need memorised">

**The four values Paper 4 most often tests (learn these exactly):**
- Mild steel tensile strength: 400 MPa
- Aluminium density: 2.7 g/cm³
- Mild steel density: 7.8 g/cm³
- Young's modulus for steel: 200 GPa (aluminium: 70 GPa; MDF: approximately 7 GPa)

**For any property you cannot remember:** Use a comparative statement. "Aluminium has a lower tensile strength than mild steel" earns the ORA mark even without a specific value.
