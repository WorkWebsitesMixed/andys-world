Cutting is the foundation; joining is the structure. A perfectly cut component assembled with a misaligned joint produces a product that fails the specification. This session covers the joining methods for your project — from timber joints through adhesives to mechanical fasteners — and the dry-fit protocol that prevents errors from being glued permanently in place.

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Dry fit", definition: "Assembling all components without any adhesive to check that every joint closes correctly and the overall assembly is square. Dry fitting reveals misaligned joints, components that are too long, or assembly sequences that do not work, before any adhesive is applied. It is mandatory before gluing." },
  { term: "Squareness check", definition: "Verifying that an assembled frame or box is at 90° in all relevant planes. Method: measure both diagonals — if the two diagonal measurements are equal, the assembly is square. If they differ, the longer diagonal indicates which corner must be pushed in to correct the square." },
  { term: "PVA (polyvinyl acetate)", definition: "The standard woodworking adhesive for MDF and timber joints. Applied to both mating surfaces, left to become tacky (1–2 minutes), then clamped. Full cure: 24 hours. Working time: approximately 5–10 minutes (enough for a complex assembly if dry fit is practised first)." },
  { term: "Clamp pressure", definition: "The force applied by clamps to squeeze glued joints together. Adequate clamp pressure ensures the PVA film is thin and uniform, maximising bond strength. Insufficient pressure leaves glue-thick joints that are weaker. Excess pressure squeezes all glue out — also weaker. Target: glue squeezes out uniformly around all edges of the joint." },
  { term: "Mechanical fastener", definition: "A non-adhesive joining method: screws, nails, bolts, KD fittings. Mechanical fasteners allow disassembly and are used where joints must be demountable or where clamps cannot reach. For MDF, use coarse-thread (cross-headed) screws with a pilot hole to prevent splitting." },
]} />

<Section title="Joint types for the project" icon="🔨" id="joints">

### Timber joints used in the project

| Joint | Best for | Forces resisted | Assembly note |
|---|---|---|---|
| Butt + PVA | Simple corner in non-load-bearing area | Shear (limited) | Requires 90° alignment and clamping |
| Housed (dado) joint | Shelf-to-panel horizontal connection | Vertical shear, pull-out | Dado must match shelf thickness exactly |
| Dowel joint | Butt joint reinforcement | Tension, alignment | Requires accurate drilling jig |
| KD fitting (cam lock) | Demountable joints | Tension, alignment | No adhesive — mechanical clamping only |
| Screw + pilot hole | Fixing circuit housing panel | Withdrawal force | Countersink head flush with surface |

### Adhesives

| Adhesive | Materials | Set time | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| PVA | MDF, hardwood, softwood | 24 hr full cure, 1 hr working strength | Apply to both faces; clamp; wipe squeeze-out |
| Epoxy resin | Metal-to-metal, metal-to-wood | 5 min to 2 hr depending on type | Mix equal parts; apply thin coat; ventilate |
| Superglue (CA) | Small parts, MDF edge sealing | 30–60 seconds | No clamping; surface must be oil-free |

</Section>

<Section title="The dry-fit and glue-up sequence" icon="🔄" id="sequence">

### Step 1: Dry fit

1. Assemble ALL components without adhesive. Check every joint closes.
2. Measure diagonals. Are they equal? If not, identify the cause before continuing.
3. Run through the glue-up sequence mentally: which joint goes first? Which needs to be clamped while the next is assembled?
4. Pre-position clamps so they are within reach.

### Step 2: Glue-up

1. Apply PVA to both mating surfaces of the first joint. Use a spreader for even coverage — avoid large glue-free areas (bond failures) or excessive glue (squeeze-out that stains visible surfaces).
2. Close the joint. Apply clamp pressure — check for squeeze-out around all edges of the joint.
3. Check square immediately with a try square or diagonal measurement. Adjust if necessary before the PVA begins to set.
4. Add the next joint while the first is still uncured (PVA bonds between surfaces — multiple joints curing simultaneously is fine if you work quickly).

### Step 3: Post-cure check

After the PVA has cured (minimum 1 hour for working strength, 24 hours for full strength):
1. Remove clamps.
2. Check squareness again.
3. Test rigidity: apply a light twisting force. The assembly should feel solid with no flex at the joints.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Dry fit and glue-up of a three-component shelf unit">

**Components:** Back panel (300 x 220 x 18 mm MDF), two side panels (150 x 300 x 18 mm MDF), one shelf (220 x 150 x 18 mm MDF — slides into dado in back panel).

**Dry fit:** Slot the shelf into the dado in the back panel. Attach both side panels to the back panel (butt joints at corners). Measure diagonal A (top-left to bottom-right): 378 mm. Measure diagonal B (top-right to bottom-left): 375 mm. Difference: 3 mm — not square. Push the bottom-left corner toward top-right while measuring until both diagonals read 376 mm. Assembly is now square.

**Glue-up sequence:**
1. Apply PVA to both faces of the dado joint (shelf-to-back panel). Slide shelf in. Clamp. Check horizontal level.
2. While dado is clamping: apply PVA to the back panel face where the left side panel butts. Attach left side panel. Clamp. Measure diagonals — adjust to square.
3. Apply PVA for right side panel. Attach and clamp.
4. Leave all clamps on for 1 hour.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Dry fit, glue-up, and squareness check" time="90 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["Your cut components from T2 W4", "PVA adhesive and spreader", "G-clamps and sash clamps (request from store)", "Try square (300 mm)", "Steel rule (600 mm)", "Damp cloth (for PVA wipe-down)"]}>

**Dry fit (0–20 min):**
1. Assemble all structural components without adhesive. Each joint must close cleanly. If any joint requires force, stop and investigate — do not force it.
2. Measure both diagonals. Record: diagonal A = ___mm, diagonal B = ___mm. Difference = ___mm. Target: less than 2 mm difference.
3. Plan the glue-up sequence. Write down the order: joint 1 first, then joint 2, etc. Set up all clamps.

**Glue-up (20–55 min):**
4. Apply PVA to joint 1. Close, clamp, check square.
5. Apply PVA to joint 2. Close, clamp, check square again.
6. Continue for all joints. Work without pausing — PVA working time is 5–10 minutes.
7. Final diagonal check after all clamps applied. Adjust if necessary.

**Circuit housing and battery panel (55–75 min):**
8. Screw the circuit housing to the inside of the product. Use pilot holes. Do not over-tighten (MDF splits). The housing panel should be removable — use screws, not adhesive.

**Clean-up and record (75–90 min):**
9. Wipe all PVA squeeze-out with a damp cloth before it cures.
10. Photograph the clamped assembly. Record in your C6 log: which joints were glued, which were screwed, PVA applied to both faces (yes/no).
</Activity>

<Support title="Assembly problems">

**Joint will not close despite correct dimensions:** The dado depth or width may be slightly over-cut, leaving a gap. Pack the gap with a thin sliver of MDF and PVA — once cured, plane or sand flush.

**Diagonals will not equalise:** Check that all corner joints are at 90° before gluing. If one component is cut slightly non-square (a trapezoid instead of a rectangle), the assembly cannot be square. Identify the non-square component by placing it on a known-flat surface with a try square.

**PVA squeezing out excessively:** Too much adhesive was applied. For future joints, reduce the amount. Wipe all squeeze-out immediately with a damp cloth — dried PVA resists most paint finishes.
</Support>
