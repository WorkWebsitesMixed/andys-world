This week you move from drawing on paper to drawing for a machine. The laser cutter follows the vector paths you create — so every line you draw is an instruction to the machine. Get the file right this week and the cutter does the precise work. Get it wrong and the machine cuts the wrong parts.

<Callout type="exam" title="Why this matters" id="why">
  CAD/CAM evidence is required for <ExamLink to="project">Component 2</ExamLink> criterion **C5 (working drawings and plans), worth 15 marks**. The mark scheme asks for evidence that you used digital tools to plan and communicate your design. A screen-captured CAD file plus a photo of a laser-cut part is the standard evidence for this.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Vector file", definition: "A digital drawing defined by mathematical paths (lines, curves, polygons) rather than pixels. Vector files can be scaled to any size without losing quality and can be sent directly to a laser cutter or vinyl cutter as cutting paths." },
  { term: "Cut line", definition: "In laser cutting software, a vector line set to the cutting colour (usually red or a specific named layer). The laser follows this path at full power to cut through the material." },
  { term: "Engrave / score line", definition: "A vector line or filled area set to a lower-power setting. Engraving burns the surface; scoring makes a shallow cut for folding. Both are controlled by the colour or layer assigned in the software." },
  { term: "Kerf", definition: "The width of material removed by the laser beam — typically 0.1–0.3 mm for a 60 W CO2 laser on plywood. Joints designed to fit exactly on-screen will be 0.1–0.3 mm too loose in reality. Designers compensate by making slot widths slightly smaller in the file." },
]} />

<Section title="Designing for the laser cutter" icon="💻" id="laser">

### File requirements

Most school laser cutters accept SVG, DXF, or AI files. The key rules:

- All cut lines: **0.001 pt stroke weight** in the designated cut colour (check your machine's configuration — usually red #FF0000 or a named layer)
- All engrave areas: **filled** in the designated engrave colour (usually black)
- No raster images in the cut layer
- All dimensions in mm (set document units before drawing)
- Outer boundary of each part is a single closed path

### Setting up your parts file

1. Open your CAD application (Inkscape is free; Illustrator or Fusion 360 also work).
2. Set up a document the same size as your sheet material (e.g. 600 × 400 mm for an A2 sheet of 3 mm ply).
3. Draw each part to scale. Use the dimension tool to verify key measurements.
4. Assign cut lines to the cut layer/colour.
5. Nest parts on the sheet: arrange tightly, leaving at least 5 mm clearance from the sheet edge and from each other.

### Kerf compensation

For laser-cut joints (finger joints, slotted connections):
- Target slot width = material thickness minus 0.2 mm (compensates for the kerf burning away the fit)
- Test your kerf on a scrap piece before cutting the real parts

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Nesting two parts for a gear organiser base">

**Parts to cut:** Base panel (200 × 150 mm) and two side uprights (200 × 80 mm each) from 6 mm birch ply.

**Sheet size:** 600 × 400 mm.

**Nesting layout:** Base panel placed at top-left with 5 mm margin. Two uprights placed side-by-side to the right of the base panel, with 5 mm between them. Total used area: 290 × 200 mm. Unused sheet area can be used for smaller parts or kept for re-test samples.

**Cut lines:** All outlines in red (#FF0000), 0.001 pt stroke. Slot openings at the top of each upright for the horizontal member: slot width = 5.8 mm (6 mm material minus 0.2 mm kerf compensation).
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="CAD file — two parts ready to cut" time="90 min" grouping="Individual at CAD stations" materials={["CAD application (Inkscape / Illustrator / Fusion 360)", "Working sketch from folder", "Sheet size specifications from teacher", "Calipers or rule for measuring existing material"]}>

**Setup (0–15 min):**
1. Open your CAD application. Set document units to mm and canvas size to your sheet dimensions.
2. Measure your actual material with calipers. Note the exact thickness — this controls slot dimensions.

**Draw parts (15–70 min):**
3. Draw at least two parts of your product: typically the main structural panels or the base.
4. Add any internal slots or holes. Apply kerf compensation to slot widths.
5. Assign all cut lines to the cut layer/colour.

**Nest and check (70–90 min):**
6. Arrange both parts on the sheet with 5 mm clearance. No overlaps.
7. Use the dimension tool to verify key dimensions match your design.
8. Save as SVG or DXF. Screenshot the nested layout and put it in your folder as C5 evidence.
</Activity>

<Support title="Software not cooperating?">

**Lines not cutting correctly?** The most common issue is stroke weight. In Inkscape: select all cut lines, open Object then Fill and Stroke, set stroke to 0.001 pt and the correct cut colour. Thick strokes (e.g. 1 mm) may be interpreted as engrave or ignored by the laser software.

**Parts not dimensionally accurate?** Check your document scale: if you drew in a default px document and the laser software interprets it as inches, every dimension is wrong. Set the document to mm and verify with the ruler tool before drawing anything.

**Inkscape specific:** Use Extensions → Generate from Path → Interpolate for slot arrays, or simply draw rectangles for slots and use Align and Distribute to space them evenly.
</Support>

<Section title="Homework" icon="🏠" id="homework">

**Home task:** If your CAD file is not yet ready to cut (fewer than two parts complete), finish it at home. Check: (1) all cut lines in correct colour/weight, (2) dimensions verified with the dimension tool, (3) parts nested on the sheet with clearance.

<Extension title="Finger joint generator">
Research how to generate a finger joint (box joint) pattern in Inkscape or another CAD tool. Most have a plugin or extension for this. Design one corner of your product using a finger joint. Calculate the tab width and number of tabs for your material thickness. How does this joint compare to your current planned join method in terms of strength and assembly?
</Extension>
