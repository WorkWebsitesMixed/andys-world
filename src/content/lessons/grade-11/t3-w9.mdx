The product is made and photographed. Now you find out how good it is by testing it against the specification and writing an honest evaluation. Term 3 closes with the M3 graded checkpoint — worth 40% of the term — which is the complete folder submission: C1 through C7.

<Callout type="exam" title="Why this matters" id="why">
  <ExamLink to="project">Component 2</ExamLink> criterion **C7 (evaluation, 15 marks)** closes the project. It rewards honest, evidence-based evaluation: what the product does well, what it does not, and what you would change in a redesign. C7 that says everything is perfect scores 0–3/15. C7 that identifies specific weaknesses with test evidence and proposes specific improvements scores 12–15/15.
</Callout>

<Section title="Testing the finished product" icon="🔬" id="testing">

### Setting up specification tests

You built a test plan in T2 W9. Today you run those tests on the actual finished product:

1. **Load test:** Apply the specified load (e.g. 4 kg) at the critical point. Measure deflection or check for tipping. Pass or fail?
2. **Function test:** Switch on — LED lights. Switch off — LED extinguishes. Battery accessible without tools. Pass or fail?
3. **Dimension test:** Measure overall width, height, depth. Compare to the specification maximum (400 mm). Pass or fail?
4. **Stability test:** Load one side only with half the total specified load. Does the product tip? Pass or fail?
5. **Usability test:** Simulate the user's action — load and unload the product as your user would. Is every item accessible? Is the switch in a usable position? Pass or fail on each point?

Record each test: method, result (measurement), pass/fail. Photograph where possible.

### Writing C7

Three sections:

**Section 1 — Strengths:** Spec points the product passes. Link each strength to the test result. Do not list opinions — list evidence.

**Section 2 — Weaknesses:** Spec points the product fails or partially fails, plus quality issues from the snagging list that could not be fixed. Be specific — name the dimension or specification point that is not met.

**Section 3 — Proposed improvements:** For each weakness, a specific improvement: what you would change, how, and why it would fix the issue. Include whether the improvement would affect other specification points.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="C7 evaluation extract — cyclist gear organiser">

**Test results:**
- Load test (4 kg, symmetric): deflection at top = 2 mm. Specification: max 5 mm. **PASS**
- Stability test (2 kg asymmetric): no tipping at 2 kg. Tipped at 2.4 kg. Specification: must not tip at 2 kg. **PASS**
- Dimension: 151 mm × 201 mm × 283 mm. All within 400 mm. **PASS**
- Circuit: LED lights on switch-on, extinguishes on switch-off. Battery accessible without tools. **PASS**
- Usability: helmet hooks securely on top peg. Water bottle clips in lower pegs. Switch reachable at standing reach. **PASS**

**Weakness identified:** Peg no. 3 is 3° off-vertical (snagging list W7). This is not a specification failure (the spec does not specify peg angle) but is a quality issue visible on close inspection.

**Proposed improvement:** In a redesign, peg holes would be drilled using a pillar drill with a fence jig to ensure perpendicularity. The hand drill used in T3 W3 allowed slight wander — a pillar drill eliminates this. Alternatively, a CNC-cut guide jig for hand drilling would achieve the same result without a pillar drill.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Testing + C7 evaluation + folder submission" time="90 min" grouping="Individual (testing equipment available from teacher)" materials={["Finished product", "Weights, ruler, calipers (for tests)", "Camera", "Folder — all C1–C7 sections", "C7 writing template"]}>

**Specification tests (0–35 min):**
1. Run each test from your T2 W9 test plan.
2. Record: spec point, test method, result (with measurement), pass/fail.
3. Photograph any test that can be photographed (product under load, LED lit, etc.).

**C7 writing (35–70 min):**
4. Write Section 1 (Strengths): at least two strengths, each linked to a specific test result.
5. Write Section 2 (Weaknesses): at least two weaknesses, each named specifically — spec point or quality issue.
6. Write Section 3 (Improvements): a specific improvement for each weakness, with reasoning.

**Folder final check and submission (70–90 min):**
7. Check all seven criteria sections are present: C1, C2, C3, C4, C5, C6, C7.
8. Check the making log is complete (W2–W8).
9. Submit the complete folder to teacher for M3 marking.
</Activity>

<Support title="C7 feels too positive?">

**Everything passed the spec?** If all your tests pass, your evaluation should still identify what could be improved in a production version — a faster assembly method, a more accurate process, a material substitution that would reduce cost or environmental impact. C7 does not require failures — it requires critical thinking.

**Weaknesses feel like excuses?** The difference: an excuse says why the problem is not your fault ('the drill slipped'). An improvement says what you would do differently to prevent it. Focus on the improvement, not the excuse.

**Can't write three full sections in 35 minutes?** Write in bullet points first, then convert to sentences. Three bullets per section = enough content for a 10/15 C7. Complete sentences and full explanations push you toward 12–15/15.
</Support>

<Section title="Homework" icon="🏠" id="homework">

**Home task:** Review your submitted folder once it is returned. Note the criteria where you scored strongest and where you scored weakest. Write one sentence per criterion explaining what additional evidence would have increased that criterion's mark. This exercise prepares you for the post-project debrief and for any moderation review.

<Extension title="Market evaluation">
Imagine your product is being sold commercially. Research one comparable product available in a retail store or online. Compare it to yours across four criteria: materials, price point, quality of finish, and user features. Write a two-paragraph evaluation: in what ways does your product match or exceed the commercial product? In what ways does the commercial product surpass yours? What design decision accounts for each difference?
</Extension>
