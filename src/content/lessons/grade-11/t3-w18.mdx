Ergonomics and anthropometrics appear in Paper 4 Section B when design contexts involve user dimensions, reach, force, or comfort. These topics require specific numerical application — not general statements about 'fitting the user'.

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Ergonomics", definition: "The science of designing products, systems, and environments to fit the people who use them — accounting for physical dimensions, cognitive load, sensory capabilities, and biomechanical forces. The goal: minimise user effort, error, and injury while maximising efficiency and comfort." },
  { term: "Anthropometrics", definition: "The measurement and statistical analysis of human body dimensions. Data is presented as percentile values: the 5th percentile value is the dimension exceeded by 95% of the population; the 95th percentile is exceeded by only 5%. For example: 5th percentile grip diameter = 28 mm (small hands); 95th percentile = 42 mm (large hands)." },
  { term: "Percentile", definition: "A statistical measure: the Nth percentile is the value below which N% of the population falls. The 50th percentile (median) is the middle value. Designing only for the 50th percentile (the 'average person') excludes users who are significantly smaller or larger than average." },
  { term: "Inclusive design", definition: "Design that considers the full range of human diversity — physical ability, age, cognitive capacity, language, and cultural background — aiming to create products that are accessible to as wide a range of users as possible without special adaptation. Also called universal design." },
  { term: "5th to 95th percentile design range", definition: "The most commonly used design range for consumer products — accommodates 90% of the target population. Users below the 5th percentile (very small) and above the 95th (very large) are excluded. For critical applications (emergency equipment, medical devices), the design range must be wider (1st to 99th percentile)." },
]} />

<Section title="Applying anthropometric data" icon="📏" id="data">

### Typical exam anthropometric data (adult UK population)

| Dimension | 5th percentile | 50th percentile | 95th percentile |
|---|---|---|---|
| Standing height | 1,555 mm | 1,710 mm | 1,855 mm |
| Seated eye height | 1,110 mm | 1,215 mm | 1,320 mm |
| Hand grip span | 160 mm | 195 mm | 230 mm |
| Forward reach (seated) | 580 mm | 700 mm | 815 mm |
| Hand grip diameter | 28 mm | 35 mm | 42 mm |

### Design principle: accommodate the range, not the average

A product designed only for the 50th percentile excludes the tallest and shortest 25% of users from each end. For a standing-height desk: a single fixed height of 710 mm (50th percentile elbow height) is comfortable for the middle user but forces the 5th percentile user (shorter) to raise their arms and the 95th percentile user (taller) to bend over. An adjustable height range of approximately 640–790 mm (5th to 95th percentile elbow height) accommodates 90% of the adult population.

</Section>

<Section title="Inclusive design principles" icon="♿" id="inclusive">

### Seven principles of universal design

1. **Equitable use:** Useful for people with diverse abilities (not two products — one 'standard' and one 'accessible').
2. **Flexibility in use:** Accommodates a wide range of individual preferences (left and right hand use; adjustable angles).
3. **Simple and intuitive:** Easy to understand regardless of experience or literacy.
4. **Perceptible information:** Communicates necessary information effectively (visual + tactile + audio cues for users who may lack one sense).
5. **Tolerance for error:** Minimises hazards and adverse consequences of accidental actions (e.g. rounding off edges, can't insert a plug in the wrong orientation).
6. **Low physical effort:** Can be used efficiently and comfortably with minimum fatigue.
7. **Size and space for approach and use:** Appropriate size and space regardless of user's body size, posture, or mobility.

### Applying to the Component 2 project

For the C2 shelf product: does the switch position accommodate both left and right-handed users? Is the LED visible from a seated position (eye height 5th–95th percentile)? Can the product be picked up by someone with limited hand strength (handle or grip feature)?

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Applying percentile data to a product design decision">

**Context:** A wall-mounted tool rack for a school workshop. The teacher needs to access tools without reaching up uncomfortably. The workshop is used by students aged 14–18 (UK school population, approximate range: 5th percentile height 1,520 mm; 95th percentile 1,855 mm).

**Design question:** At what height should the top row of tool holders be positioned so that 95% of users can reach them without strain?

**Relevant anthropometric data:** Maximum comfortable overhead reach (students) — approximate: 5th percentile 1,750 mm; 95th percentile 2,150 mm.

**Design decision:** Position the top row at 1,750 mm from the floor — this is within the comfortable reach of the 5th percentile student (the shortest user who can still comfortably access it). Users above the 5th percentile can also reach it easily. Positioning higher (e.g. 2,000 mm) would exclude students below approximately the 30th percentile.

**Inclusive modification:** Add a step stool hook directly below the top row for students who cannot comfortably reach — accommodating the excluded 5th percentile without redesigning the full rack.
</WorkedExample>
