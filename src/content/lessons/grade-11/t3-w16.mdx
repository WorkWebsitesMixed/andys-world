This bonus extends the 6Rs sustainability content from T2 W9 into Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) — a more quantitative approach to sustainability that appears in the most challenging Section B questions.

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Life Cycle Assessment (LCA)", definition: "A systematic analysis of the environmental impact of a product across its entire life: from raw material extraction, through manufacturing, use phase, to end-of-life disposal or recovery. LCA identifies which stage contributes most to environmental impact (energy use, CO₂ emissions, water use, waste), allowing targeted design improvements." },
  { term: "Cradle-to-grave", definition: "An LCA scope that covers all four life cycle stages: extraction of raw materials, manufacturing, product use, and end-of-life disposal. A cradle-to-grave assessment gives the full environmental picture." },
  { term: "Cradle-to-gate", definition: "An LCA scope limited to the first two stages: raw material extraction and manufacturing (up to the factory gate). Used when the use phase is identical between products being compared — for example, comparing two chair designs made from different materials." },
  { term: "Embodied energy", definition: "The total energy consumed in extracting and processing a material from its raw form into a usable product (before the product is used). Aluminium has high embodied energy (approximately 200 MJ/kg for virgin aluminium) because smelting requires large amounts of electricity. Recycled aluminium uses only 5% of the embodied energy of virgin aluminium." },
  { term: "Carbon footprint", definition: "The total greenhouse gas emissions (expressed in equivalent kilograms of CO₂) produced across a product's life cycle or a specific stage. Used to compare products or processes on a common scale. 1 kg of CO₂e = 1 CO₂ equivalent unit, which can represent different greenhouse gases weighted by their warming potential." },
]} />

<Section title="The four life cycle stages" icon="♻️" id="stages">

### Stage 1: Raw material extraction

Impact from mining, logging, oil extraction. High energy and water use. Habitat disruption. Key drivers: material type (virgin aluminium has high impact; sustainably certified timber has lower impact) and location (local vs imported).

### Stage 2: Manufacturing

Impact from energy used in machines, chemicals used in surface treatment, transport between suppliers. Key drivers: process energy intensity (casting steel is more energy-intensive than cutting MDF) and factory location (renewably powered vs coal-powered factory).

### Stage 3: Use phase

Impact from energy used during operation (heaters, powered appliances) and maintenance (replacement parts, cleaning chemicals). For passive products (a shelf, a chair), the use phase impact is low. For powered products (refrigerators, cars), the use phase dominates total LCA impact.

### Stage 4: End-of-life

Impact from disposal method: landfill (permanent material loss), incineration (CO₂ release), recycling (material recovered, new embodied energy avoided), reuse (maximum impact avoidance). Design decisions (single material vs composite, mechanical vs adhesive joins) determine which end-of-life route is available.

### Which stage dominates?

| Product type | Dominant stage | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Car | Use phase | Fuel consumption over 15 years dwarfs manufacture |
| Refrigerator | Use phase | Electricity for cooling over 20 years |
| Wooden chair | Manufacturing | Use phase is passive (no energy); most impact from timber processing |
| Aluminium laptop case | Raw material extraction | Virgin aluminium smelting has very high embodied energy |

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="LCA stage analysis for MDF shelf vs aluminium shelf">

**Product:** 600 mm x 200 mm shelf. Compare MDF 18 mm vs aluminium 3 mm.

**Stage 1 — Extraction:**
MDF: softwood logs felled (FSC-certified = managed replanting, lower impact). Resins for bonding (chemical extraction, modest impact).
Aluminium: bauxite mining (open-cast, high land disturbance), then smelting (approximately 15 kWh per kg electricity-intensive). Recycled Al: impact approximately 5% of virgin. If virgin Al: high extraction impact.

**Stage 2 — Manufacturing:**
MDF: mechanical pressing and drying (moderate energy). Laser cutting: electricity-based (low carbon if renewable grid).
Aluminium: extrusion or rolling (moderate energy after smelting).

**Stage 3 — Use:**
Both passive (no electricity consumed). Equal low impact.

**Stage 4 — End-of-life:**
MDF: composite material (MDF = wood fibres + urea-formaldehyde resin) — cannot be recycled into new MDF; typically landfill or incineration.
Aluminium: 100% recyclable. Recycled aluminium uses 95% less energy than virgin. High end-of-life recovery rate (approximately 75% global recycling rate for aluminium).

**LCA conclusion:** Virgin aluminium has a higher stage 1 impact than MDF. However, at end-of-life, aluminium is fully recyclable (low stage 4 impact) while MDF cannot be recycled. Over multiple product cycles, recycled aluminium has lower cumulative impact than MDF. The decision depends on whether the aluminium will actually be recycled — design for disassembly (screwed not riveted) and user behaviour matter.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="LCA stage analysis and carbon comparison" time="45 min" grouping="Individual" materials={["LCA worksheet (teacher-provided)", "Carbon footprint data table (teacher-provided or use estimates in this page)"]}>

**LCA stage analysis (0–20 min):**
1. For two products assigned by the teacher (or choose from: a steel bicycle frame vs a carbon fibre bicycle frame; a glass jar vs a plastic container; a hardwood table vs an MDF table):
   - Rate each stage (1–3: 1 = low impact, 3 = high impact) for both products.
   - Identify the dominant stage for each product.
   - State one design change that would reduce impact in the dominant stage.

**6Rs and LCA alignment (20–35 min):**
2. For each of the 6Rs, identify which LCA stage it primarily affects:
   - Reduce: which stage?
   - Reuse: which stage?
   - Recycle: which stage?
   - Refuse: which stage?
   - Rethink: which stage?
   - Repair: which stage?
3. Write a two-sentence explanation for the most surprising alignment you found.

**Section B style question (35–45 min):**
4. Write a 4-mark answer to: "Compare the end-of-life impact of a solid pine shelf and an aluminium shelf. Explain which is more sustainable at end-of-life and suggest one design decision that would improve the less sustainable option." Use the LCA evidence from this session.
</Activity>
