<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Alloy", definition: "A mixture of a metal with one or more other elements (metals or non-metals), designed to achieve specific property improvements over the pure metal. The added elements disrupt the crystal lattice of the base metal, making it harder for dislocations to move — which increases hardness and tensile strength. Example: steel = iron + carbon (typically 0.02–2.0%)." },
  { term: "Annealing", definition: "A heat treatment process where a metal is heated to a specific temperature and then cooled slowly. Annealing relieves internal stresses, reduces hardness (increasing ductility), and makes the material easier to work. Used before cold-working operations that would otherwise crack a hardened material." },
  { term: "Hardening (quenching)", definition: "A heat treatment process where steel is heated to a critical temperature (above 723°C for most steels) and then rapidly cooled by quenching (plunging into water or oil). Rapid cooling traps carbon atoms in the iron crystal lattice, creating a very hard, brittle microstructure (martensite). Produces hard but brittle steel — usually followed by tempering." },
  { term: "Tempering", definition: "A heat treatment applied after hardening: the quenched steel is re-heated to a lower temperature (150–650°C depending on application) and cooled slowly. Tempering reduces brittleness while retaining much of the hardness gained by hardening. The higher the tempering temperature, the tougher (and less hard) the result." },
  { term: "Shape memory alloy (SMA)", definition: "A smart material that returns to a pre-programmed shape when heated above a specific transition temperature. Nitinol (nickel-titanium alloy) is the most common SMA. Used in dental braces, medical stents, and aerospace actuators. The material 'remembers' its original shape through a reversible crystal structure change." },
]} />

<Section title="Alloying: why it works" icon="🔬" id="alloying">

### Steel: iron + carbon

Pure iron is relatively soft (tensile strength approximately 200 MPa) and ductile. Adding carbon (0.3–2.0%) introduces carbon atoms into the iron lattice, disrupting the regular crystal structure and preventing dislocations from moving as freely. The result: higher hardness and tensile strength.

| Steel type | Carbon content | Tensile strength | Characteristics |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mild steel | 0.1–0.3% | 400 MPa | Weldable, machinable, not hardenable by quenching |
| Medium carbon | 0.3–0.6% | 550–700 MPa | Springs, crankshafts, can be heat-treated |
| High carbon | 0.6–1.4% | 700–1000+ MPa | Cutting tools, high hardness, brittle |

### Brass: copper + zinc

Brass = copper + 10–45% zinc. Zinc solid-solution strengthens the copper lattice. Brass has: higher strength than pure copper (310 MPa vs 210 MPa), better machinability (brass machines more cleanly than copper), and retained electrical and thermal conductivity. Used for plumbing fittings, musical instruments, and electrical terminals.

### Duralumin (aluminium alloy): Al + Cu + Mg

Duralumin = approximately 95% Al + 4% Cu + 1% Mg. The result: tensile strength increased from approximately 90 MPa (pure Al) to 450 MPa. Density stays approximately 2.7 g/cm³. Used in aircraft fuselage skins — high strength-to-weight ratio is critical.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Three alloy and heat treatment explanation responses">

**Q: Explain why mild steel is alloyed with chromium to produce stainless steel. [3 marks]**

"Mild steel (iron + 0.1–0.3% carbon) corrodes in the presence of water and oxygen, forming iron oxide (rust). Chromium is added (typically 10.5% or more) because chromium reacts preferentially with oxygen to form a thin, stable chromium oxide layer on the surface (passivation layer). This passive layer is self-repairing — if scratched, it immediately re-forms in air. The result: stainless steel has very high corrosion resistance without the need for paint or surface coating, making it suitable for kitchen equipment, medical instruments, and outdoor structures."

**Q: Describe the effect of annealing on a hardened high-carbon steel knife blade. [2 marks]**

"Annealing the blade involves heating it above the recrystallisation temperature and cooling slowly. The slow cooling allows the metal crystals to reform in a more regular lattice, relieving internal stresses and reducing the hardness gained by quenching. The blade becomes more ductile — it can be bent without fracturing — but loses the cutting-edge hardness needed for its function. Annealing is used before re-grinding or reshaping the blade, not as a final treatment."
</WorkedExample>
