This is not an exam session. It is the closing session of the G11 year: a structured showcase of everything each student has designed, built, and evaluated across the three terms. The presentations consolidate the design vocabulary learned this year and prepare students for the verbal examination element that may appear in coursework moderation.

<Callout type="info" title="Coursework moderation context" id="moderation">
  Cambridge IGCSE D&T moderators may ask oral questions about submitted coursework. The ability to explain your design decisions verbally — not just in writing — demonstrates genuine understanding. Today's presentation practice prepares you for that possibility.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Design journey narrative", definition: "A coherent account of how the product came to be: starting from the user need (C1), through research and specification (C2), ideas and development (C3, C4), working drawings and manufacture (C5, C6), to testing and evaluation (C7). A design journey narrative explains why each decision was made, not just what was decided." },
  { term: "Critical reflection", definition: "An honest assessment of what worked and what did not in the design process — including decisions you would change with hindsight. Critical reflection is not negative self-criticism; it is evidence of design maturity. Examiners award marks for honest evaluation; they do not penalise for identifying shortfalls." },
  { term: "Peer feedback", definition: "Structured observations from classmates about a presentation. Effective peer feedback is specific (not 'good job' but 'the joint justification was strong because you quoted the force type and the material property'), balanced (one strength and one specific improvement), and constructive (the improvement suggestion is actionable)." },
]} />

<Section title="The 5-minute presentation structure" icon="🎤" id="structure">

Each student presents their Component 2 project for 5 minutes. The structure:

| Minute | What to cover |
|---|---|
| 0:00–0:45 | C1: The brief. Who is the user? What problem does the product solve? What were the three most important function points? |
| 0:45–1:30 | C2: The specification. What were the two specification points you are most proud of? Were they measurable and justified? |
| 1:30–2:30 | C3 and C4: Ideas and development. Which structural principle did you use? What was the most important development move — what changed and why? |
| 2:30–3:30 | C6: Manufacture. What was the hardest part to make? What technique did you use? |
| 3:30–4:30 | C7: Evaluation. Did the product meet the specification? Which point was hardest to meet? What would you change? |
| 4:30–5:00 | Final sentence: one thing you would do differently if you started C1 again. |

</Section>

<Section title="Peer feedback protocol" icon="💬" id="feedback">

### Listening and giving feedback

After each presentation, the audience completes a feedback card with two items:
1. **Strength:** One specific thing the presenter explained well — name the folder section and what made it specific (not just "good ideas" — "the development move for the joint was strong because you identified the force type and named the specific joint that resists it").
2. **Improvement:** One specific thing the presenter could strengthen — not "speak louder" but a content improvement: "The specification point for the aesthetics did not state the test method — 'visual inspection at 300 mm' would make it testable."

### Receiving feedback

Read every feedback card. Annotate your folder with any suggested improvement that is valid — this creates evidence of ongoing reflection for moderation.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="A 5-minute presentation outline (annotated)">

**C1 (0:45):** "My user is my younger brother, aged 12, who needed a way to store his gaming accessories and charge two devices simultaneously on his bedroom desk. The three function points I focused on were: the product must hold four items upright without falling, the product must contain a switch-operated LED for use in low light, and the product must fit within a 400 mm width on his existing desk."

**C2 (0:45):** "My two strongest specification points were the load test — 3 kg at the midspan without more than 1.5 mm deflection — and the circuit specification — the LED must light within one second of the switch being toggled. Both had clear test methods and came from measurements I took of my brother's actual items."

**C3+C4 (1:00):** "I chose a frame-and-panel structural principle for idea 1. The development move that changed the design most was replacing the butt joint at the shelf shoulder with a dado joint — because the butt joint under a 3 kg load was in shear at only two nail points, and the dado distributes the same force over the full 18 mm shelf cross-section."

**C6 (1:00):** "The hardest part was drilling the 8 mm hole for the switch accurately — I needed it centred and perpendicular. I used a V-block jig to hold the side panel at the correct angle on the drill press. Without the jig, the first hole came out 4° off-perpendicular and the switch would not lie flat."

**C7 (1:00):** "The product met five of the six specification points. The one it did not meet was the aesthetics point — brush marks were visible at 300 mm on the right side panel. The modification would be to use a spray paint finish, which eliminates brush marks entirely. I learned that surface finish takes longer than I budgeted for."

**Final sentence (0:30):** "If I started C1 again, I would interview my user before writing the function points — not after. Two of my function points turned out not to be what he actually needed."
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Portfolio showcase and peer feedback" time="90 min" grouping="Whole class (individual presentations)" materials={["Your Component 2 folder and finished product", "Presentation timer (5 minutes)", "Peer feedback cards (teacher-provided)"]}>

**Preparation (0–10 min):**
1. Lay out your folder and product. Plan your 5-minute route through the sections using the table above.
2. Practise the opening sentence of each section — do not read it, say it.

**Presentations (10–80 min):**
3. Each student presents for 5 minutes (maximum — a timer will signal the end).
4. Audience: one feedback card per presenter. Specific strength + specific improvement.

**Feedback reading and annotation (80–88 min):**
5. Read your feedback cards. Circle any suggestion you agree with. Write a one-sentence response to each card in your folder.

**Closing reflection (88–90 min):**
6. The final two minutes of the year: write one sentence about who you are as a designer now versus who you were at the start of T1 W1.
</Activity>

<Support title="Presentation nerves and fluency">

**"I am nervous about presenting."** The presentation is in class, to people you know, about your own work. No one in the room knows your project better than you — which means no question can be asked that you cannot answer from direct experience.

**"I do not know how to explain my development move clearly."** Use the before-and-after format: "The C3 idea had [description of joint/material]. The problem was [specific issue]. I changed it to [new approach] because [specific property or force that the change addresses]."

**"My evaluation was honest — I said things did not work."** This is a strength, not a weakness. Use the same language in your presentation: "Specification point 4 was not met because... and the modification would be..."
</Support>
