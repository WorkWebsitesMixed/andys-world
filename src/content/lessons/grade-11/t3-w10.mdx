The project is submitted. The making log is complete. Before the year ends, this session bridges the project and the written papers: reviewing what you built, why it works, and mapping the technical decisions you made in the makerspace back to the Paper 4 topic structure. Your making experience is revision material — it just needs to be named.

<Callout type="exam" title="Why this matters" id="why">
  <ExamLink to="paper4">Paper 4</ExamLink> tests technical knowledge in context. You have spent three terms making decisions about electronics, materials, structures, and manufacturing processes. Every one of those decisions is a worked example for Paper 4. This session converts making experience into revision knowledge.
</Callout>

<WorkedExample title="Transfer example — stability test to Paper 4">

**What happened in the makerspace:** In T2 W5, the model tipped when load was placed asymmetrically on one side. Adding a wider base footprint (150 mm to 210 mm) stopped the tipping.

**Paper 4 equivalent question:** "A display stand tips sideways when loaded unevenly. Suggest one change to the design that would improve stability. Explain how your change would work." (4 marks)

**Exam answer using project experience:** "Increase the width of the base from 150 mm to 210 mm. A wider base lowers the centre of gravity relative to the base edge — the point about which tipping would occur. By increasing the distance between the tipping point (the edge of the base) and the vertical line of action of the load, a larger overturning moment is needed to cause tipping, so the stand is more stable under the same asymmetric load."

**This is full marks — because you experienced it and can explain the mechanism.**
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Design cycle reflection + revision mapping" time="90 min" grouping="Individual / pair (discussion partner for reflection)" materials={["Complete project folder", "Paper 4 topic list from T2 W1", "Revision mapping template", "Collins textbook for cross-reference"]}>

**Design cycle reflection (0–25 min):**
1. On a blank page, draw the design cycle as a diagram (C1 → C7 → and back to C1).
2. For each criterion, write one sentence: "The most important decision I made in C_ was ___."
3. Circle the one criterion where your evidence is strongest and the one where it is weakest.

**Revision mapping (25–60 min):**
4. Using the table above, fill in your own project decisions for each Paper 4 topic.
5. For each decision: write one exam-style question you could now answer from memory.
6. Answer two of those questions (4 minutes each, written, no notes).

**Paper 4 gap analysis (60–80 min):**
7. Identify which Paper 4 topics are NOT covered by your project experience (mechanisms? graphics and CAD?).
8. Write a two-week August revision plan: which topics, which days, which resource.

**Year-end debrief (80–90 min):**
9. Share one project success and one project learning with your class — something you would tell next year's G11 class before they start.
</Activity>

<Support title="Revision mapping feels superficial?">

**Project decisions feel too specific for Paper 4?** Start with the principle behind the decision. "I used 6 mm birch ply for the frame" is specific. "Birch ply has a high strength-to-weight ratio and consistent properties across the sheet because of its cross-grain construction" is a Paper 4 answer. Translate the decision into a principle.

**Can't remember the technical details?** Open the folder. Find the session where you made the decision. Read the section heading and the worked example from that lesson. Your folder is a revision resource — the notes in it were written at the moment you were learning the content.

**Mechanism topic not covered by the project?** Gears, cams, and linkages are in Paper 4 but not in the structural product project brief. This is a known gap for most G11 students. Plan to cover Collins §5.2 (Mechanisms) in August revision — it is a relatively small topic with high return for the mark investment.
</Support>

Write the mark scheme for it. This exercise forces you to understand both the content and the marking standard — and produces a useful revision tool.
