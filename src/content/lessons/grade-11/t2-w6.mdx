The second development session combines two things: a focused look at materials and sustainability decisions, and the closure of your C4 evidence. At the end of this session you submit your C3 and C4 folder pages for the M2 graded checkpoint — worth 30% of the term.

<Callout type="exam" title="Why this matters" id="why">
  <ExamLink to="project">Component 2</ExamLink> criterion **C4 (development) is worth 15 marks**. The M2 submission today captures your C3 (20 marks) and C4 evidence in one checkpoint — together they represent 35 marks of your 100-mark project. C4 marks come specifically from evidence that you changed your design in response to testing. "Same design as Week 4 with no changes" scores near zero.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Sustainability in design", definition: "Reducing the negative environmental or social impact of a product through deliberate design choices — choice of material (recycled, renewable, low-process), finish (water-based, no finish), end-of-life (disassembly for recycling), or reduced material use through efficient cutting." },
  { term: "Life cycle thinking", definition: "Considering a product's environmental impact at each stage: raw material extraction, manufacturing, use, and disposal. A design can reduce impact at any of these stages." },
  { term: "Nesting", definition: "Arranging cut parts on a sheet so that the gaps between them are minimised, reducing waste material. A tightly nested laser-cut layout can reduce material waste by 20–40% compared to an un-nested layout." },
  { term: "C4 development evidence", definition: "The folder pages that document your development: model photographs, test-results tables, sketched design changes, and a comparison of before and after each change. Without these, there is nothing for the examiner to mark." },

<Section title="Sustainability and development" icon="🌱" id="sustainability">

### Where sustainability decisions happen in C4

Sustainability is not a separate task — it lives inside your development decisions:

| Decision | Sustainability dimension |
|---------|----------------------|
| Material choice (birch ply vs MDF) | Birch ply: FSC-certified, no formaldehyde binder. MDF: synthetic resin binder, not recyclable |
| Finish (oil vs lacquer) | Hardwax oil: plant-based, breathable, repairable. Lacquer: solvent-based, VOC emissions |
| Cutting layout | Tight nesting minimises offcut waste |
| Joining method | Knock-down fittings allow disassembly and recyclable components at end of life |
| Electronics | Low-power LED less than 0.5 W vs traditional bulb 3–10 W |

Pick two decisions from your own design and justify them in sustainability terms.

### Closing your C4 evidence

Your C4 folder section should contain, in order:
1. Sketch model photograph (Week 4)
2. Problem annotations on that photograph
3. Test-results table (Week 5): spec point / method / result / pass? / change
4. Sketched design changes (what you changed and why)
5. Re-test result confirming the change fixed the problem
6. Sustainability decision notes (this session)

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Sustainability justification for a cyclist gear organiser">

**Material:** 18 mm birch plywood selected over MDF. Birch ply is manufactured with lower formaldehyde adhesives and is produced from sustainably managed European forests (FSC). MDF uses urea-formaldehyde resin which off-gasses during cutting and is not easily recycled at end of life.

**Finish:** Hardwax oil rather than acrylic lacquer. Oil is plant-derived (linseed, sunflower, carnauba wax), applied cold, and can be re-applied locally when worn without stripping the whole surface. Lacquer requires solvent thinners and full re-coating.

**Cutting layout:** The four laser-cut acrylic switch covers are nested within a 100 × 200 mm sheet rather than cut from four separate strips — saves approximately 60 cm² of acrylic offcut.
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Sustainability notes + M2 folder submission" time="90 min" grouping="Individual (teacher circulates to check completeness before submission)" materials={["All C3 and C4 folder pages", "Development notes from Weeks 4 and 5", "Sustainability decision sheet"]}>

**Sustainability section (0–25 min):**
1. Pick two material or process decisions in your design. For each, write: what you chose, what the alternative was, and why your choice reduces environmental impact.
2. Annotate your cutting plan (or sketch one) to show nesting — label the total sheet size and note what area is waste.

**C4 folder order check (25–55 min):**
3. Lay out your C4 evidence in order: model photo → test table → design changes → re-test.
4. If any section is missing (no re-test? no measured result?), write it up now.
5. Add the sustainability notes as the final page of C4.

**M2 submission (55–90 min):**
6. Final read-through of C3 and C4 together. Does the story make sense: ideas → selection → testing → development → sustainability?
7. Submit C3 + C4 folder pages to teacher.
</Activity>

<Support title="Sustainability section feels thin?">

**Can't justify the material choice?** Look at what your chosen material is made from and how it is processed. Birch ply comes from managed forests with a water-based glue option. Acrylic is petroleum-derived, not biodegradable — but long-lasting and repairable (a sustainability argument). Steel is recyclable and high-strength per unit weight. Find the trade-off and name it.

**Nesting seems too simple?** A hand-drawn sketch of your laser-cut parts arranged on an A4 sheet, with dimensions labelled, is sufficient. The key is showing you thought about waste minimisation — not producing a perfect CAD layout.

**Test results page incomplete?** Write what you can reconstruct: "During the Week 5 test, the base tipped under asymmetric load. I added a rear brace (see sketched change, p. X). I then re-applied 4 kg symmetrically and 3 kg asymmetrically — no tipping observed." Reconstructed evidence is better than nothing but aim to capture results in real time next time.
</Support>

<Section title="Homework" icon="🏠" id="homework">

**Home task:** Review your M2 submission in your mind. What is the weakest page — the one with the least evidence? Write a brief note about what you would add to that page if you had one more hour. Keep this note in your folder — it will guide your working drawing preparation in Weeks 7–9.

<Extension title="Full life cycle analysis">
Choose your product material and trace its full life cycle: (1) extraction of raw material, (2) processing into the sheet product, (3) cutting in the makerspace, (4) use in the user's home, (5) disposal. At which stage is the environmental impact highest? What is one design decision that would reduce impact at that specific stage?
</Extension>

</Section>
