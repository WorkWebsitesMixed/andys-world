You can know the content and still lose marks — through how you answer, not what you know. This bonus session is about the technical habits of answering exam questions: matching answer length to marks, using command words correctly, and avoiding the predictable mistakes that examiners comment on year after year in their examiner reports.

<Callout type="exam" title="Why this matters" id="why">
  The Cambridge examiner's report for <ExamLink to="paper1">Paper 1</ExamLink> and <ExamLink to="paper4">Paper 4</ExamLink> identifies the same marking pitfalls across sittings. Knowing what the examiner is looking for — and what repeatedly loses marks — converts knowledge into marks more efficiently than studying more content.
</Callout>

<KeyTerms terms={[
  { term: "Examiner's report", definition: "The annual document published by Cambridge after each exam sitting, describing what candidates did well, what they did poorly, and specific answers that did or did not earn marks. The report is the single most useful revision resource for exam technique — it is the examiner telling you what they reward." },
  { term: "Command word response", definition: "Calibrating the length and type of your answer to the command word: 'state' requires a word or phrase; 'describe' requires several sentences explaining what happens; 'explain' requires what happens plus why; 'evaluate' requires weighing evidence with a conclusion." },
  { term: "Mark-per-line rule", definition: "A rough guide: one mark = one clear point, usually expressed in one line of writing. A 4-mark question needs four distinct points, not four paragraphs repeating the same point. Reading the question and counting the marks before writing prevents over- or under-writing." },
  { term: "Generic answer", definition: "An answer that could apply to any product, any material, or any situation — 'it is strong', 'it is easy to use', 'it is environmentally friendly'. Examiners reject generic answers because they do not demonstrate specific knowledge. Every answer must be specific to the context given in the question." },
]} />

<Section title="The five examiner pitfalls" icon="⚠️" id="pitfalls">

### Pitfall 1: Misreading the command word

**'State one advantage...'** requires one specific point. Writing three paragraphs loses time and earns no extra marks.

**'Explain why...'** requires the what AND the why. Writing what happens without why loses the explain mark.

**Fix:** Read the command word, count the marks, then write exactly that much.

### Pitfall 2: Generic answers without context

**Bad:** "Wood is used because it is strong and easy to work with."

**Good:** "Birch plywood is used because its cross-grain layer construction gives uniform bending strength in all sheet directions — important for a shelf carrying load in any orientation."

**Fix:** After every answer, ask "could this apply to a steel shelf? A concrete shelf?" If yes, add a specific reason.

### Pitfall 3: Unlabelled diagrams

Examiners can only mark what is labelled. A technically correct circuit diagram with no labels earns zero. A force diagram showing correct arrows but no force type labels earns zero.

**Fix:** Every diagram gets a title and leader lines to every significant feature.

### Pitfall 4: Missing units on calculations

V = IR gives the voltage in volts — not "7.2". The mark for the final answer is typically only awarded if the unit is stated.

**Fix:** Write the unit at every step, not just the final answer.

### Pitfall 5: Leaving blanks

A blank earns zero. A partially correct attempt earns at least 1 mark on many questions. Cambridge uses 'allow for' in many mark schemes — an answer that is partially right often scores.

**Fix:** For any question you cannot fully answer, write the most specific thing you know about the topic. Never leave blank.

</Section>

<WorkedExample title="Applying the fixes to a Paper 4 answer">

**Question:** "Explain why a triangulated frame is used in the construction of a roof truss. (3 marks)"

**Pitfall answer:** "Triangles are strong. Triangles make the structure better. Triangles are used in buildings because they are rigid." (1 mark at most — repetition of the same vague point, no mechanism explained)

**Correct answer:** "A triangle is the only polygon that is geometrically rigid — it cannot deform without changing the length of at least one side. (1) In a rectangular frame, the joints can rotate, causing the frame to rack into a parallelogram under a lateral load. (2) Triangulation eliminates this by subdividing rectangles into triangles, preventing joint rotation. (3) This distributes load along the length of each member in pure tension or compression rather than bending, making the structure more efficient per unit of material." (3 marks — three distinct, mechanistic points)
</WorkedExample>

<Activity title="Pitfall identification + personal checklist" time="45 min" grouping="Individual then pair (peer review)" materials={["Set of 5 exam questions with student sample answers (teacher issued)", "Personal checklist card", "Pen"]}>

**Sample answer analysis (0–25 min):**
1. Teacher issues five sample student answers (anonymised). Each answer contains one or more pitfalls.
2. Identify the pitfall in each answer and write the improved version.

**Personal checklist (25–35 min):**
3. Write your personal exam technique checklist — 5 items, one per pitfall. Use first-person: "I will always write the unit after every step in a calculation."
4. Put the checklist on a card to keep in your exam folder.

**Peer review (35–45 min):**
5. Swap a sample answer with your neighbour. Identify one pitfall they may have missed.
</Activity>

<Support title="Can't identify the pitfall in a sample answer?">

**Ask: what command word was used?** Then ask: did the answer match the command word? A 'state' question answered with three paragraphs = Pitfall 1. An 'explain' question answered with only what happened = Pitfall 1.

**Ask: could this answer apply to any product?** If yes = Pitfall 2 (generic answer).

**Ask: are all the numbers followed by units?** If not = Pitfall 4.
</Support>
